## Introduction

The **European Union's Artificial Intelligence Act** (AI Act) is radically transforming the landscape of AI use across all sectors, including legal services. With implementation deadlines approaching in 2026, it's crucial that law firms, legal advisors, and LegalTech companies understand their obligations.

This article provides a practical guide on how the AI Act specifically affects the Spanish and European legal sector, which systems require immediate attention, and how to prepare for regulatory compliance.

## What is the AI Act and Why Does It Matter to the Legal Sector?

The AI Act is the world's first comprehensive legislation on artificial intelligence, adopted by the EU in 2024. Its objective: to ensure that AI systems used in Europe are **safe, transparent, and respectful of fundamental rights**.

For the legal sector, this is especially relevant because:

- **Many firms already use AI**: contract analysis, legal research, automated drafting, outcome prediction
- **Legal services affect fundamental rights**: privacy, defense, property, freedom
- **The sector handles sensitive data**: confidential client information, personal data, trade secrets

## Risk-Based Classification System

The AI Act categorizes AI systems according to their risk level. Understanding this classification is the first step toward compliance.

### 1. Unacceptable Risk (Prohibited)

**Banned** systems that manipulate behavior or exploit vulnerabilities. In the legal context, this would include:

- Systems that psychologically manipulate clients or opposing parties
- AI that exploits vulnerabilities of people in procedural disadvantage

> **Practical takeaway**: Law firms should not develop or use systems designed to manipulate client decisions through subliminal techniques.

### 2. High Risk

This category requires **maximum attention** for the legal sector. It includes AI systems that:

- **Assist in judicial or administrative decisions** affecting fundamental rights
- **Evaluate eligibility for essential public or private services**
- **Process biometric data or sensitive information** systematically

**Examples in the legal sector:**

- **Sentence prediction software** that aids judges in decision-making
- **Risk assessment systems** for bail or parole decisions
- **Contract analysis AI** that automatically determines acceptance/rejection of important commercial agreements

#### Requirements for High-Risk Systems

If your firm uses or develops high-risk systems, you must comply with:

1. **Risk management system**: Continuously identify, assess, and mitigate risks
2. **Data governance**: Ensure training datasets are representative, bias-free, and updated
3. **Complete technical documentation**: Architecture, data, development process, validation
4. **Automatic logging**: Traceability of system decisions
5. **Transparency**: Inform users interacting with AI
6. **Human oversight**: Capacity for intervention, control, and override of AI decisions
7. **Accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity**: Technical measures against errors and attacks
8. **EU database registration**: High-risk systems must be publicly registered

### 3. Limited Risk (Transparency Obligations)

Includes legal chatbots, virtual assistants, and generative AI tools (like GPT for legal drafting).

**Main obligation**: **Clearly inform** users that they are interacting with an AI system.

**Practical example:**

If your firm uses a chatbot on your website for initial consultations, it must visibly indicate:

> "This assistant uses artificial intelligence. Responses are for guidance only and do not constitute binding legal advice."

### 4. Minimal Risk

Most AI tools used in administrative or internal support tasks: spam filters, spell checkers, calendar management tools.

**Obligations**: None specific to the AI Act (though GDPR and other regulations still apply).

## Key Implementation Deadlines in 2026-2027

The AI Act came into force in stages. For the legal sector, these are the critical deadlines:

| Date | Obligation |
|------|-----------|
| **August 2024** | Prohibition of unacceptable risk systems |
| **August 2025** | Obligations for general-purpose AI models (GPAI) |
| **August 2026** | **Full compliance for high-risk systems** |
| **August 2027** | Obligations for AI systems in regulated products |

**Current status (March 2026)**: **5 months remain** for high-risk systems to meet all obligations. It's time to act.

## Practical Steps for Compliance

### Step 1: AI Systems Inventory

Conduct a **complete census** of all AI tools your firm uses:

- Contract analysis software (e.g., Kira, LawGeex)
- AI-powered legal research platforms (e.g., vLex Vincent, Casetext)
- Automated drafting tools (e.g., ChatGPT, Claude for drafting)
- Document management systems with automatic classification
- Customer service chatbots

### Step 2: Classify Each System

For each tool, determine:

- Does it process sensitive client data?
- Does it make decisions affecting fundamental rights?
- Is there adequate human oversight?

**Expected outcome**: Classified list (High risk / Limited risk / Minimal risk).

### Step 3: Vendor Audit

If you use third-party software, contact vendors and request:

- **AI Act compliance certification**
- System technical documentation
- Information about audits performed
- Bias management policy
- Human oversight protocols

Established vendors (Microsoft, Google, European LegalTech providers) are already working on compliance. Small or non-European vendors may present risks.

### Step 4: Implement Controls for High-Risk Systems

If you identify high-risk systems:

1. **Designate an AI officer**: Person responsible for compliance
2. **Document processes**: How AI is used, who supervises, how results are validated
3. **Train the team**: Training on AI limitations and oversight obligations
4. **Establish periodic reviews**: Quarterly internal audits
5. **Register the system**: When the EU database becomes operational

### Step 5: Update Privacy Policies and Consent

If you use AI that processes client data:

- Update privacy notices mentioning AI use
- Obtain informed consent when necessary
- Ensure GDPR compliance (right to explanation, non-discrimination)

### Step 6: Review Client Contracts

Include clauses on:

- Use of AI tools in legal service delivery
- Human oversight of AI-generated results
- Liability limitations related to AI
- Client's right to request human review of automated decisions

## Intersection with GDPR

The AI Act **does not replace GDPR**, they complement each other. You must comply with both:

- **GDPR**: Personal data protection (consent, minimization, security, data subject rights)
- **AI Act**: Safety, transparency, and oversight of AI systems

**Dual compliance example:**

A contract analysis system that processes personal data must:
- **(GDPR)** Have legal basis for processing, ensure security, allow exercise of rights
- **(AI Act)** Document the system, manage bias risks, maintain human oversight, inform about AI use

## Liability and Sanctions

The AI Act establishes **significant financial penalties**:

- **Up to €35M or 7% of global turnover** for using prohibited systems
- **Up to €15M or 3% of turnover** for non-compliance with high-risk system obligations
- **Up to €7.5M or 1.5% of turnover** for providing incorrect information

For small and medium-sized firms, even minor sanctions can be devastating. **Prevention is the most cost-effective strategy**.

## Opportunity: Responsible AI as Competitive Advantage

Beyond regulatory compliance, adopting AI responsibly can be a **competitive advantage**:

- **Differentiation**: Corporate clients value legal providers demonstrating technology governance
- **Risk reduction**: Lower probability of errors, biases, or security breaches
- **Sustainable efficiency**: Well-supervised automation frees time for high-value tasks
- **Trust**: Transparency about AI use strengthens the attorney-client relationship

## Conclusion

The AI Act represents a paradigm shift in how the legal sector must manage technology. Far from being a barrier, it's an opportunity to professionalize AI use and ensure that technological innovation aligns with the fundamental values of the legal profession: **justice, transparency, and protection of rights**.

**Final recommendations:**

1. **Act now**: Don't wait until August 2026
2. **Invest in training**: Your team must understand the tools they use
3. **Collaborate with experts**: Seek advice from specialists in technology law and data protection
4. **Document everything**: Traceability is key to demonstrating compliance
5. **Stay updated**: AI regulation evolves rapidly
