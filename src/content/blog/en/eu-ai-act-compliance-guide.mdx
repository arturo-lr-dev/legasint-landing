## Introduction

The **European Union's Artificial Intelligence Regulation** (AI Act) marks a historic milestone as the world's first comprehensive AI regulation. With its progressive implementation throughout 2026, Spanish companies must prepare to meet specific obligations based on the risk level of their AI systems.

In this article, we analyze the key aspects of the AI Act and provide a practical guide to ensure regulatory compliance in the Spanish business context.

## What is the AI Act and Why Does It Matter?

The AI Act establishes a risk-based regulatory framework that classifies AI systems into four categories: **unacceptable risk, high risk, limited risk, and minimal risk**. This approach ensures that regulation is proportional to the potential impact of each system on fundamental rights and people's safety.

### Key Implementation Dates

- **February 2025**: General entry into force of the Regulation
- **August 2025**: Prohibition of unacceptable risk AI systems
- **August 2026**: Full obligations for high-risk systems
- **August 2027**: Obligations for general-purpose AI models

## Classification of AI Systems by Risk Level

### 1. Unacceptable Risk (Prohibited)

Systems completely prohibited in the EU:

- **Subliminal manipulation**: Techniques that distort human behavior causing harm
- **Exploitation of vulnerabilities**: AI that exploits age, disability, or socioeconomic status
- **Social scoring**: General behavior rating systems for citizens
- **Real-time remote biometric identification**: With limited exceptions for law enforcement

**Penalty**: Up to 35 million euros or 7% of annual global turnover.

### 2. High Risk

Systems requiring strict compliance:

#### Critical Areas:
- **Critical infrastructures**: Transport, water, gas, electricity
- **Education and training**: Educational access and assessment
- **Employment**: Hiring, promotion, dismissals
- **Public services**: Access to benefits and essential services
- **Law enforcement**: Evidence reliability assessment, crime prediction
- **Migration and border management**: Document verification, risk assessment
- **Justice**: Interpretation and application of legal norms

#### Provider Obligations:
- Robust risk management system
- Data governance and training data quality
- Exhaustive technical documentation
- Registration in EU database
- Transparency and user information provision
- Effective human oversight
- Accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity

**Penalty**: Up to 15 million euros or 3% of annual global turnover.

### 3. Limited Risk (Transparency)

Systems requiring transparency obligations:

- **Chatbots and conversational AI**: Clearly inform users they're interacting with AI
- **Emotion detection**: Notify when emotional recognition systems are used
- **Synthetic content**: Label deepfakes and AI-generated content

### 4. Minimal Risk

Most AI systems (spam filters, AI in video games, etc.) have no specific obligations under the AI Act, beyond general data protection and consumer regulations.

## Practical Steps for Compliance

### Step 1: Inventory and Classification

**Immediate action**: Conduct a complete inventory of all AI systems used or developed by your organization.

```plaintext
Key questions:
- What AI systems do we currently use?
- Who is the provider? Do we develop internally?
- What risk category does each system fall into?
- What personal data do they process?
- Is there effective human oversight?
```

### Step 2: Conformity Assessment

For **high-risk** systems, implement:

1. **Risk management system**: Identification, analysis, evaluation, and risk mitigation
2. **Data governance**: Ensure quality, relevance, representativeness, and absence of bias
3. **Technical documentation**: System design, development, performance
4. **Logging**: Events, decisions, system interventions
5. **Transparency**: Clear information about capabilities and limitations
6. **Human oversight**: Real capacity to intervene, override, or stop the system
7. **Accuracy and robustness**: Measures to ensure consistent and safe performance

### Step 3: Designation of Responsible Parties

**Recommendation**: Designate an **AI Compliance Officer** or integrate these functions into the DPO (Data Protection Officer).

Responsibilities include:
- Monitor regulatory updates
- Coordinate conformity assessments
- Manage documentation and records
- Serve as contact point with authorities

### Step 4: Training and Awareness

**Critical**: All personnel interacting with high-risk systems must receive specific training on:

- System capabilities and limitations
- Human oversight procedures
- Anomaly detection
- Intervention protocols

### Step 5: Continuous Monitoring

AI Act compliance is not a one-time event, but an ongoing process:

- **Post-market monitoring**: Performance tracking under real conditions
- **Risk assessment updates**: When usage conditions change
- **Documentation review**: Maintain updated records
- **Serious incident reporting**: Mandatory notification to authorities

## Interaction with GDPR

The AI Act **complements** GDPR, it doesn't replace it. Companies must comply with both frameworks:

| Aspect | GDPR | AI Act |
|---------|------|--------|
| **Scope** | Personal data | AI systems (with or without personal data) |
| **Legal basis** | Consent, legitimate interest, etc. | Conformity assessment by risk |
| **Rights** | Access, rectification, erasure | Information about automated decision logic |
| **Impact assessment** | DPIA for high risk | Conformity assessment for high-risk AI |

**Best practice**: Integrate Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIA) with AI Act conformity assessments for systems processing personal data.

## Practical Cases in the Spanish Context

### Case 1: Human Resources Company

**Situation**: CV pre-screening software using AI.

**Classification**: High risk (employment).

**Requirements**:
- Complete conformity assessment
- Risk management system to detect bias
- Human oversight in final decisions
- Information to candidates about AI use
- Registration in EU database

### Case 2: Online Store with Chatbot

**Situation**: Virtual assistant for customer service.

**Classification**: Limited risk.

**Requirements**:
- Clearly inform it's a chatbot
- Don't present the system as human
- GDPR compliance if processing personal data

### Case 3: Predictive Analytics Startup

**Situation**: Business data analysis tool without automated decisions about people.

**Classification**: Minimal risk.

**Requirements**:
- No specific obligations under AI Act
- Comply with general data protection and consumer regulations

## Recommendations for Spanish Companies

1. **Don't wait until the last moment**: Full implementation of compliance measures can take months
2. **Consult specialized legal experts**: The AI Act is complex and its interpretation requires specific knowledge
3. **Document the entire process**: The burden of proof of compliance lies with the organization
4. **Establish communication channels with providers**: If using third-party systems, ensure providers meet their obligations
5. **Stay updated**: Implementation guides and interpretative criteria will continue to develop

## Conclusion

The AI Act represents a paradigmatic shift in technology regulation, establishing standards that will likely influence legislation worldwide. For Spanish companies, compliance is not just a legal obligation, but an opportunity to:

- **Differentiate competitively** through ethical and transparent AI
- **Build trust** with customers and users
- **Mitigate risks** legal, reputational, and operational
- **Innovate responsibly** in the European AI ecosystem

Early preparation and genuine commitment to AI Act principles will not only avoid significant penalties but will position your organization as a leader in responsible use of artificial intelligence.
