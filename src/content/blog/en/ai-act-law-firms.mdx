## Introduction

The **European Union's Artificial Intelligence Regulation (AI Act)**, in full implementation phase since 2024, marks a turning point in how law firms and legal departments can use AI tools. With penalties reaching up to **€35 million** or **7% of global turnover**, compliance with this regulation is not optional.

In this article, we analyze what the AI Act means for legal practice, which AI systems are considered high-risk in our sector, and how to prepare to meet regulatory requirements.

## What is the AI Act and Why Does It Matter to the Legal Sector?

The AI Act establishes a regulatory framework based on **risk levels** for artificial intelligence systems. Higher risk means greater transparency, documentation, and control obligations.

### Risk Classification

The regulation categorizes AI systems into four levels:

1. **Unacceptable Risk** - Prohibited (e.g., subliminal manipulation, social scoring)
2. **High Risk** - Strictly regulated with multiple obligations
3. **Limited Risk** - Transparency obligations
4. **Minimal Risk** - No specific regulation

For the legal sector, the **high risk** category is most relevant.

## High-Risk AI Systems in Legal Practice

According to Annex III of the AI Act, systems considered high-risk include AI used in:

### 1. Administration of Justice and Democratic Processes

> "AI systems intended to assist a judicial authority in researching and interpreting facts and the law."

**Practical examples:**
- **Predictive case outcome analysis** - Tools that predict judicial outcomes
- **Case management systems** with AI that prioritize or classify matters
- **Legal research assistants** that suggest procedural strategies
- **Digital evidence analysis tools** with decision-making capabilities

### 2. Human Resources Management

If your firm uses AI for:
- Candidate selection (CVs, automated interviews)
- Performance evaluation of lawyers/employees
- Task or case assignment

These systems are also subject to high-risk requirements.

## Obligations for Providers and Users

### If You're an AI User (most law firms)

The AI Act distinguishes between **providers** (who develop AI) and **users** (who use it). Most law firms are users.

**Main obligations:**

1. **Provider Due Diligence**
   - Verify the system has CE marking
   - Check for declaration of conformity
   - Review available technical documentation

2. **Human Oversight**
   - Maintain effective human control over critical decisions
   - Don't fully delegate complex legal analysis to AI
   - Establish manual review protocols

3. **Continuous Monitoring**
   - Monitor system performance
   - Report serious incidents to authorities
   - Maintain usage logs

4. **Client Transparency**
   - Inform when AI influences legal decisions
   - Explain system limitations

### If You're a LegalTech Solution Provider

If your firm develops proprietary AI tools or tools for third parties, obligations are much more extensive:

- Third-party conformity assessment
- Documented risk management
- Training data governance
- Comprehensive technical documentation
- Quality management system

## Common Use Cases: What's Their Risk Level?

Let's look at concrete examples of tools commonly used by law firms:

| Tool | Risk Level | Obligations |
|------|------------|--------------|
| ChatGPT for contract drafting | **Minimal/Limited** | Basic transparency |
| AI-powered due diligence analysis | **Limited** | Inform clients of AI use |
| Predictive litigation system | **High Risk** | Full compliance, oversight |
| Automated CV screening | **High Risk** | Conformity assessment |
| Customer service chatbot | **Limited** | Indicate it's AI |

## How to Prepare Your Firm for the AI Act

### Step 1: AI Systems Audit

Identify **all** tools you use:
- Management software with AI functionalities
- Document analysis tools
- Legal research systems
- Process automation with machine learning

```markdown
## Firm AI Inventory

| System | Provider | Use | Classification |
|--------|----------|-----|----------------|
| LexisNexis AI | LexisNexis | Research | Limited |
| Predictive Analytics Tool | Vendor X | Litigation | High Risk |
| ContractAI | Vendor Y | Drafting | Limited |
```

### Step 2: Risk Assessment

For each system, determine:
- Does it make decisions affecting fundamental rights?
- Is it used in judicial or administrative processes?
- Does it process sensitive client data?
- Does it influence HR decisions?

### Step 3: Implement Controls

**For high-risk systems:**

1. **Human Oversight Protocol**
   - Define who reviews AI outputs
   - Establish review criteria
   - Document final decisions

2. **Activity Logging**
   - Maintain usage logs
   - Record incidents or errors
   - Document human interventions

3. **Team Training**
   - Train lawyers in responsible AI use
   - Train in bias or error detection
   - Establish clear usage limits

### Step 4: Update Internal Policies

Review and update:
- Privacy policy (mention AI use)
- Client contracts (AI clauses)
- Data management policy
- Regulatory compliance manual

### Step 5: Provider Due Diligence

Require from your legal software providers:
- Declaration of conformity with AI Act
- Risk assessment documentation
- CE certification (when applicable)
- Information about training data

## Penalties and Enforcement

The AI Act establishes a progressive penalty regime:

- **Up to €35M or 7% global turnover** - Use of prohibited systems
- **Up to €15M or 3% global turnover** - Non-compliance with high-risk obligations
- **Up to €7.5M or 1.5% global turnover** - Inaccurate information to authorities

Data protection authorities in each Member State will be responsible for enforcement, along with specialized AI bodies.

## Opportunities: Responsible AI as Competitive Advantage

While the AI Act imposes obligations, it also creates opportunities:

1. **Market Differentiation** - "AI Act compliant" firms generate trust
2. **Process Improvement** - AI auditing helps optimize workflows
3. **Talent Attraction** - New generations value responsible technology
4. **New Practice Areas** - AI Act compliance advisory

## Conclusion

The AI Act should not be seen as an obstacle, but as an opportunity to professionalize the use of artificial intelligence in the legal sector. Firms that act now, implementing AI governance frameworks, will be better positioned to:

- Avoid million-euro penalties
- Offer higher quality services
- Compete in an increasingly technological market
- Advise clients on their own compliance processes

**Final recommendation:** Don't wait for inspections to arrive. Start your AI systems audit now, establish human oversight protocols, and demand transparency from your LegalTech providers.
