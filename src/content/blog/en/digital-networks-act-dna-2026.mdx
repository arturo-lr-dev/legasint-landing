## Introduction

On **January 21, 2026**, the European Commission presented the **Digital Networks Act (DNA)**, an ambitious legislative proposal that will fundamentally modernize the legal framework for electronic communications in the European Union. This new regulation represents the most significant reform of the sector since the European Electronic Communications Code (EECC) of 2018.

For law firms and legal professionals, understanding the DNA is crucial, as it will transform the regulatory landscape of telecommunications, affecting operators, service providers, and technology companies throughout the EU.

## What is the Digital Networks Act (DNA)?

The DNA is a **regulatory proposal** aiming to create a more integrated and harmonized legal framework for electronic communications in the EU. Its primary objective is to establish an "advanced and resilient digital infrastructure" that supports the Union's connectivity, competitiveness, and resilience goals.

### Key Objectives of the DNA

1. **Regulatory Framework Modernization**: Simplify and update existing legislation to reflect current technological realities
2. **Investment Promotion**: Facilitate investment in next-generation network infrastructure
3. **Digital Resilience**: Strengthen the security and resilience of communications networks
4. **Single Market Harmonization**: Reduce barriers and create a true single market for communication services

## Major Changes Introduced by the DNA

### 1. Regulatory Simplification

The DNA **replaces and repeals** significant parts of the existing regulatory framework, including:

- The European Electronic Communications Code (EECC)
- Directives on communications networks and services
- Specific regulations on access and interconnection

### 2. New Service Categories

The regulation introduces a **more flexible classification** of electronic communication services:

| Category | Description | Examples |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Interpersonal Communications Services (ICS)** | Communications between specific individuals | Messaging, VoIP calls |
| **Public Communications Services (PCS)** | Broadcasting to wide audiences | Streaming, broadcasting |
| **Internet Access Services** | Internet connection | ISPs, fiber providers |

### 3. Network Access Regulation

The DNA **preserves the fundamental principles** of network access established in the EECC but introduces important modifications:

#### Maintained Asymmetric Regulation

Unlike previous proposals seeking to eliminate asymmetric regulation, the DNA **maintains the current model**:

```markdown
- [ ] Preservation of access obligations for operators with significant market power (SMP)
- [ ] Maintenance of non-discrimination principles
- [ ] Continuity of ex-ante regulation for specific markets
```

#### New Flexibility for Investments

The DNA introduces **flexible mechanisms** to incentivize infrastructure investments:

- **Reduced regulatory periods** for new network deployments
- **Regulatory incentives** for high-risk infrastructure projects
- **Facilitated co-investment** between operators

### 4. Security and Resilience

The regulation **significantly strengthens** security requirements:

#### Security Obligations

All providers of electronic communication services must:

1. **Implement robust security measures**
2. **Conduct periodic risk assessments**
3. **Report security incidents** to national authorities
4. **Establish incident response plans**

#### Critical Network Resilience

The DNA **classifies certain networks** as critical and applies additional requirements to them:

- **Vital support networks**: Essential services (healthcare, energy, transport)
- **Public interest networks**: Infrastructures with significant social impact
- **High-capacity networks**: Advanced broadband infrastructures

## Implications by Operator Type

### Public Network Operators (PNOs)

#### Strengthened Obligations

PNOs will face **stricter requirements**:

1. **Network Transparency**: Detailed information on quality and availability
2. **Third-Party Access**: Fair and non-discriminatory conditions
3. **Continuous Investment**: Deployment obligations in non-profitable areas

#### Investment Incentives

The DNA introduces **regulatory benefits**:

- **"Light regulation" periods** for new infrastructures
- **Tariff flexibility** in innovative segments
- **Cost recognition** for deployments in low-density areas

### Electronic Communication Service Providers (ECSPs)

#### Balanced Regulation

ECSPs will benefit from **greater flexibility**:

- **Reduced administrative burdens** for innovative services
- **Self-assessment of risks** for low-impact services
- **Adaptive framework** that evolves with technology

## Implementation Timeline

### Phase 1: Approval and Entry into Force (2026)

### Phase 2: Adaptation Period (2027)
