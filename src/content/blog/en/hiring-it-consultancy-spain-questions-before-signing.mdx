## The €30,000 Mistake That Happens Every Day

Three months ago, a logistics company from Barcelona called us with a problem we know all too well. They had signed with a "top" IT consultancy to develop their fleet management platform. Six months later, the project was stalled, they had spent €30,000, and all they had to show was a PowerPoint with mockups.

The mistake? They didn't ask the right questions before signing. They hired based on a recommendation, without verifying actual capabilities, without defining concrete deliverables, without a contract that protected them.

This article is the guide that would have saved them those €30,000.

## Why Hiring an IT Consultancy Is Different in 2026

The Spanish technology market has changed radically in recent years:

- **The supply has multiplied**: There are consultancies of all sizes, from freelancers calling themselves "agencies" to large firms with 200-person teams.
- **Specialization is key**: A consultancy that shines in e-commerce can be a disaster for complex B2B projects.
- **Remote work has globalized competition**: You're no longer competing only with consultancies in Madrid or Barcelona. You can hire in Valencia, Málaga, or even outside Spain.
- **AI has changed the rules**: Some consultancies sell "AI-powered development" that is really just a junior using ChatGPT. Others genuinely use AI tools to accelerate without lowering quality.

In this context, choosing well isn't just important. It's critical.

## The 10 Questions You Must Ask Before Signing

### 1. "Can I see similar projects you've delivered?"

Generic portfolios don't count. You want to see **concrete projects** in your sector, with your type of complexity, at your scale.

**Red flags:**
- "We can't show projects due to confidentiality" (without exceptions, this is suspicious)
- They only show landing pages when you need a complex platform
- The "success stories" have no concrete data (dates, metrics, technologies)

**Green flags:**
- They show you a similar project with technical details
- You can speak with the reference client
- They explain what they learned from projects that went wrong

### 2. "Who will actually work on my project?"

Many consultancies sell with their senior team and execute with juniors. It's the norm, not the exception.

**Follow-up questions:**
- Can I meet the team before signing?
- What percentage of the work will be done by the team you're presenting?
- What's the average experience of the assigned team?
- What happens if a key person leaves mid-project?

### 3. "How do you handle scope changes?"

Scope creep is where most IT projects die. Your consultancy must have a clear process.

**What you want to hear:**
- "Every change is evaluated: impact on time, cost, and quality. You decide whether to accept it."
- "We have a formal change request process."
- "The budget includes a buffer for minor changes."

**What you DON'T want to hear:**
- "Don't worry, we'll adjust as we go."
- "That's included" (without specifying exactly what's included)

### 4. "What's your working methodology?"

In 2026, any consultancy working in pure waterfall is obsolete. But don't let them sell you "agility" as an excuse for not planning.

**Good signs:**
- They use Scrum or Kanban with defined sprints
- There are regular demos (every 2 weeks maximum)
- They have visible management tools (Jira, Linear, Notion)
- They do retrospectives and improve their process

**Bad signs:**
- "We're agile" but can't explain how
- No concrete delivery dates
- No visible tracking tools for you

### 5. "What happens if the project is delayed?"

Delays are almost inevitable in software development. What matters is how they're managed.

**Clauses you should demand:**
- Immediate communication when there's a delay risk
- Mitigation plan with options (reduce scope, add resources, etc.)
- Penalties for delays attributable to the consultancy
- Your right to terminate the contract if the delay exceeds a threshold (e.g., 30%)

### 6. "How do you guarantee code quality?"

The code you don't see is what will cost you the most in the long run. Good development today can be a maintenance nightmare tomorrow.

**What they must have:**
- Mandatory code reviews
- Automated testing (unit, integration)
- Technical documentation
- Defined coding standards
- Security audits

**Direct question:** "What's your test coverage?" If they can't answer, run.

### 7. "Who owns the code and data?"

This is fundamental, and many companies overlook it.

**It must be clear in the contract:**
- You own the source code
- You have access to the repository from day 1
- The data is yours and you can export it whenever you want
- There's no technological dependency on the consultancy (vendor lock-in)

**Real horror story:** A company discovered that their "trusted consultancy" had registered the code in their name. They had to pay €15,000 extra to "buy" what they had already paid for.

### 8. "What's the total cost of ownership (TCO) at 3 years?"

The development price is just the tip of the iceberg. Ask about:

- Hosting and infrastructure
- Software licenses
- Maintenance and support
- Cost of future changes
- Team training

**A project that costs €20,000 can cost €60,000 over 3 years.** Better to know beforehand.

### 9. "How do you handle security and regulatory compliance?"

In 2026, this isn't optional. Depending on your sector, you'll need to comply with:

- GDPR (if you handle personal data)
- NIS2 (if you're an essential or important operator)
- AI Act (if you use AI systems)
- Sector-specific standards

**Your consultancy must:**
- Know the regulations applicable to your sector
- Have documented security processes
- Conduct periodic audits
- Have an incident response plan

### 10. "What support do you offer after launch?"

Launch isn't the end, it's the beginning. 40% of the total cost of a digital product happens after go-live.

**Support levels you should negotiate:**
- **Warranty**: Bug fixes at no cost (minimum 3 months)
- **Maintenance**: Security updates, patches
- **Evolution**: New features, improvements
- **Technical support**: Incident response, defined SLAs

## Alarm Bells That Should Disqualify

Regardless of how well they answer the questions, there are attitudes that are non-negotiable:

❌ **Pressure to sign quickly**: "The offer expires in 48 hours" is always a bad sign.

❌ **Budget without understanding the project**: If they give you a price in the first meeting without understanding your business, they don't understand what they're doing.

❌ **No verifiable references**: Any consultancy with a track record has clients who can speak for them.

❌ **Generic contract downloaded from the internet**: Terms must be specific to your project.

❌ **They don't talk about risks**: If they only tell you how beautifully everything will go, they're either naive or lying to you.
