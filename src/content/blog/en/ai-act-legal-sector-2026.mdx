## Introduction

March 2026 marks a turning point for the European legal sector: the **Artificial Intelligence Regulation (AI Act)** is now in full enforcement, and law firms, legal advisories, and corporate legal departments face new compliance challenges. This regulation, approved by the European Parliament in 2024, establishes the world's most comprehensive regulatory framework for AI systems.

In this article, we analyze how the AI Act specifically affects the Spanish legal sector, what obligations legal professionals must meet, and what opportunities emerge from this new regulatory reality.

## The AI Act: Risk-Based Approach

The European Regulation classifies AI systems into **four risk categories**:

### 1. Unacceptable Risk (Prohibited)
Systems that manipulate human behavior or exploit vulnerabilities. In the legal context, this includes tools that:
- Assess witness credibility through emotion recognition without scientific basis
- Predict criminal recidivism based solely on personal characteristics

### 2. High Risk (Strict Regulation)
AI systems used in **legal and judicial contexts** are considered high risk, including:
- Risk assessment tools in criminal proceedings
- Predictive analysis systems for sentencing
- Automated dispute resolution platforms

**Obligations for high-risk systems:**
- Mandatory conformity assessment
- Registration in EU database
- Exhaustive technical documentation
- Effective human oversight
- Transparency in system operation
- Robust cybersecurity measures

### 3. Limited Risk (Transparency)
Legal chatbots, virtual assistants, and document analysis systems requiring:
- Clearly inform that interaction is with an AI
- Explain basic system functioning
- Allow users to make informed decisions

### 4. Minimal Risk (Free Use)
Spell checkers, basic search tools, document management systems without automated decision-making.

## Direct Impact on Law Firms

### Technological Due Diligence

Firms using LegalTech tools with AI components must now:

**1. Audit all current tools:**
```markdown
- Identify which systems use AI
- Classify risk level according to AI Act
- Verify provider compliance
- Document chain of responsibility
```

**2. Evaluate new tools:**
Before contracting any legal software with AI, verify:
- CE conformity certification (if high risk)
- Registration in EU database
- Provider's transparency policies
- Contractual liability clauses

### Professional Liability

The AI Act introduces **shared liability** between developers and professional users. Lawyers using AI systems for:

- **Contract analysis:** Must supervise and validate conclusions
- **Legal research:** Must verify accuracy of citations
- **Automated drafting:** Must review and approve generated content

> "Human oversight is not optional: it's a legal and ethical requirement. The lawyer is always responsible for the final outcome of the work, regardless of the tools used." — Madrid Bar Association, Best Practices Guide 2026

### Data Protection and Confidentiality

The intersection between **AI Act and GDPR** is especially relevant for the legal sector:

- Data processed by AI remains subject to GDPR
- Professional secrecy must be maintained even when using AI tools
- Contracts with AI providers must include data processing clauses as processors (DPA)
- Clients must be informed when their data is processed with AI

## Opportunities for the Legal Sector

### 1. New Advisory Services

The AI Act creates demand for specialized services:

**AI Compliance:**
- AI Act compliance audits
- Algorithmic impact assessments
- Implementation of AI governance systems

**Technology contracts:**
- Review of contracts with AI providers
- Negotiation of liability clauses
- Design of internal AI use policies

**Litigation and defense:**
- Representation in AI Act non-compliance sanctions
- Defense of rights of those affected by automated decisions
- Challenge of discriminatory AI systems

### 2. Responsible Operational Efficiency

Firms that adopt AI in a compliant manner can gain competitive advantages:

- **Cost reduction:** Automation of repetitive tasks (initial contract review, document search)
- **Greater accuracy:** Detection of problematic clauses, risk identification
- **Better service:** Faster responses, more thorough analysis

### 3. Market Differentiation

Demonstrating AI Act compliance can be a differentiating factor:

- Certifications of best practices in AI use
- Transparency with clients about tools used
- Public policies on ethics in legal AI

## Practical Recommendations

### For Law Firms and Advisories

**Short term (0-3 months):**
1. Complete inventory of tools with AI components
2. Risk classification according to AI Act
3. Verification of critical provider compliance
4. Basic team training on AI Act

**Medium term (3-12 months):**
1. Development of internal AI use policy
2. Update of client contracts (AI use clauses)
3. Implementation of human oversight protocols
4. External compliance audit

**Long term (12+ months):**
1. AI compliance certifications
2. Development of specialized AI Act services
3. Organizational culture of responsible AI

### For In-House Legal Departments

**Priorities:**
- Evaluate AI systems used by the company (not just in legal)
- Coordinate with compliance, IT, and data protection
- Establish AI governance committees
- Prepare for possible regulatory inspections

## Sanctions and Enforcement

Non-compliance with the AI Act carries **significant fines**:

- Up to **35 million euros** or **7% of annual global turnover** (whichever is higher) for serious infringements
- Up to **15 million euros** or **3% of turnover** for minor non-compliance
- Up to **7.5 million euros** or **1.5% of turnover** for incorrect information to authorities

In Spain, the **Spanish AI Supervision Agency (AESIA)**, created in 2025, is the competent authority to supervise compliance.

## Conclusion

The AI Act is not a barrier to innovation, but a **framework that guarantees responsible and ethical use of AI** in the legal sector. Firms and professionals who anticipate these obligations, far from seeing the regulation as a burden, can turn it into a competitive advantage.

The key lies in balance: leveraging AI's potential to improve efficiency and quality of legal service, while ensuring human oversight, transparency, and respect for clients' fundamental rights.

**The future of the legal sector is hybrid:** lawyers and AI working together, each contributing their strengths, under a clear regulatory framework that protects all ecosystem actors.
