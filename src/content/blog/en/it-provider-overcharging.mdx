## The Uncomfortable Question Few People Ask

A few weeks ago, a client showed us their monthly "website maintenance" bill: **€480 per month** for a static 5-page website that hadn't been touched in 8 months. No blog, no e-commerce, no integrations. It just existed.

When we asked what that maintenance included, they looked at us with a "isn't that normal?" expression.

It's not.

And it's not their fault. Most business owners don't know what the technology they use should actually cost. They trust that their provider is honest. Most are. But some take advantage of that trust.

This article gives you the tools to detect it before your budget bleeds for another year.

## Signal 1: The "Maintenance" That Doesn't Maintain Anything

Legitimate technical maintenance includes:
- Security updates
- Backups (and verification that they actually work)
- Uptime monitoring
- Bug fixes
- Technical support when something breaks

**What is NOT maintenance:**
- "Premium hosting" when you're on a €5/month shared server
- "Monthly SEO optimization" without reports or metrics
- "Software licenses" that are actually free (WordPress, open source plugins)
- "Domain management" that costs €12/year at any registrar

**Real 2026 benchmarks:**
- Static website (5-10 pages): €50-150/month (hosting + basic backups)
- CMS website (managed WordPress): €150-400/month (including updates, security, support)
- Complex web application: €500-2,000/month (depending on traffic and complexity)

If you're paying more and they can't explain exactly what they do each month, ask. If the answer is vague, you have your answer.

## Signal 2: The Development That Never Ends

A project with defined scope should have:
- Clear delivery dates
- Milestones with verifiable deliverables
- A closed budget (or justified variations in writing)

**Red flags:**
- "It's more complex than we thought" appears every two months
- The initial budget has doubled "due to unforeseen issues"
- You've been waiting 6 months for a feature that was supposed to take 2 weeks
- Every meeting generates new invoices without concrete deliverables

**The reality:** Good developers know how to estimate. Bad ones estimate low to win the project and then charge you for the overrun.

## Signal 3: The Intellectual Property They Never Hand Over

This is a classic that costs companies thousands.

**Scenario:** You contract a development. You pay. The project "works." But when you want to change providers... you don't have access to the code, you don't have the server keys, you don't have the database.

**What you should ALWAYS have:**
- Access to the code repository (GitHub, GitLab, etc.)
- Administrator credentials for all services
- Basic technical documentation
- Intellectual property rights to the code developed for you

If your provider tells you "don't worry, we manage everything," **that's a warning sign, not reassurance**.

## Signal 4: The "Expert" Who Only Sells What They Know How to Do

A good consultant recommends the best solution for your problem. A bad consultant sells you the only solution they know how to implement.

**Real examples:**
- They sell you a native app when a PWA would have cost 1/10 and covered your needs
- They put you on AWS when your traffic fits on a €20/month VPS
- They propose custom development for something a €50/month SaaS solves
- They force you to use their favorite tech stack even if it's not appropriate

**The test:** Ask "why would we NOT use [cheaper/simpler alternative]?" If they don't have a solid technical answer, they're selling, not advising.

## Signal 5: The Invoice Nobody Understands

Technology invoices should be as clear as your electricity bill:
- Detailed concept
- Hours worked (if time-based)
- Associated deliverable
- Unit price

**Red flags in billing:**
- "Various technical services" without breakdown
- Hours always rounded up (1.2h = 2h billed)
- "Infrastructure" costs without specifying what infrastructure
- Support included in the contract that they then bill separately

**Golden rule:** If you don't understand the invoice, don't pay it until they explain it line by line.

## Signal 6: Vendor Lock-in

This is the most sophisticated and dangerous technique. It makes you so dependent on them that changing providers is so painful you'd rather stay and keep paying.

**Common techniques:**
- Using proprietary or uncommon technologies
- Not documenting anything
- Not delivering credentials
- Building "custom" integrations that only they understand
- Hosting everything on their servers without access for you

**How to protect yourself:**
- Demand full access from day 1 (write it in the contract)
- Use standard and popular technologies
- Request documentation as a mandatory deliverable
- Maintain independent backups

## Signal 7: The Price That Doesn't Resemble Anything in the Market

We're not talking about "the cheapest is the best." We're talking about proportionality.

**Spain market benchmarks 2026:**
- Junior developer hour: €30-50
- Senior developer hour: €80-120
- Strategic consulting hour: €120-200
- Standard website maintenance: €150-500/month
- Landing page development: €2,000-8,000
- Complex web app development: €25,000-100,000+

If you're being charged double without justifying the differential value, ask why. If the answer is "because we're the best," ask for verifiable references.

## What to Do If You Detect These Signals

### Step 1: Document everything
Save all invoices, emails, contracts. If there are discrepancies, you need proof.

### Step 2: Request an independent audit
A third party can evaluate in hours whether what you're being charged makes sense. It costs less than you think and can save you thousands.

### Step 3: Review the contract
Look for intellectual property clauses, code access, termination conditions. Many companies are surprised when they read what they signed.

### Step 4: Renegotiate or change
If the provider isn't transparent when you ask, they never will be. Your company's technology is too important to trust someone who hasn't earned that trust.

## How to Choose Well from the Start

**Before signing with a new provider:**

1. **Ask for references** and call them. Not emails, calls.
2. **Demand a detailed quote** broken down by phases and deliverables
3. **Write it in the contract:** code access, intellectual property, documentation
4. **Define payment milestones** tied to deliverables, not dates
5. **Ask about technologies** and verify they are standard
6. **Start with a small project** to evaluate before committing long-term

## Conclusion

Technology shouldn't be a black box where you put money in and hope something good comes out. You have the right to understand what you're buying, how much it costs, and why.

Good IT providers know this and explain it without issue. Bad ones hide behind technical jargon and "trust."

**Your budget is your responsibility. But it's also your right to protect it.**
