## Introduction

Two years after its entry into force, **Regulation eIDAS 2.0** has radically transformed the digital identity landscape in Europe. In May 2026, the deployment of the **European Digital Identity Wallet (EUDI Wallet)** is no longer a technical promise: it is an operational reality that is redefining how law firms manage **KYC** (Know Your Customer), **qualified electronic signature**, and **notarial processes**.

This article provides a practical analysis of what has changed with eIDAS 2.0, how the EUDI Wallet works, and what implications it has for legal practice in Spain.

## 1. What is eIDAS 2.0 and the EUDI Wallet?

### The regulatory framework

Regulation (EU) 2024/1183, known as **eIDAS 2.0**, reformed the original 2014 framework in May 2024 with an ambitious objective: to provide all EU citizens and residents with an **interoperable, secure, and sovereign digital identity**.

The central element is the **EUDI Wallet**: a digital application that allows users to store and share identity credentials, verified attributes, and official documents (such as ID cards, academic degrees, or professional licenses) in a decentralized and encrypted manner.

### Technical framework in 2026

As of May 2026, the EUDI ecosystem presents the following operational characteristics:

| Component | Status in 2026 | Legal Impact |
|-----------|----------------|--------------|
| **Large Scale Pilots (LSP)** | Results published and in production | Validated cross-border use cases |
| **Reference Architecture** | Version 1.5+ stable | Standardization for providers |
| **eIDAS Network** | 22 Member States connected | Mutual recognition of identities |
| **QES (Qualified Electronic Signature)** | Interoperable with EUDI Wallet | Valid signature across EU territory |
| **PID (Personal Identification Data)** | National deployment ongoing | Spain integrates DNI 4.0 into the Wallet |

## 2. Impact on Spanish legal practice

### 2.1. KYC and client onboarding

The **client identification and verification process** has traditionally been one of the most costly bottlenecks for law firms. With the EUDI Wallet, this workflow is transformed:

**Before eIDAS 2.0:**
- Physical presentation of ID card or passport
- Manual document verification
- Scanning, storage, and destruction of copies
- Risk of identity fraud

**With eIDAS 2.0 (2026):**
- The client shares their **PID (Personal Identification Data)** from the EUDI Wallet
- The firm receives cryptographically verified attributes
- No need to store copies of physical documents
- Identity proof is **audited, traceable, and revocable**

### 2.2. Qualified Electronic Signature (QES)

Qualified electronic signature under eIDAS 2.0 reaches a key operational maturity in 2026. Spanish **Trust Service Providers (TSPs)** now integrate their signature services with the EUDI Wallet, enabling:

- Contract signing with **unified identity proof**
- Legal validity equivalent to handwritten signature throughout the EU
- Complete traceability of the signature act (who, what, when, how)
- Immediate revocation in case of credential compromise

> **Practical note:** Firms operating cross-border no longer need to validate foreign electronic signatures through apostille or legalization. The mutual recognition of the eIDAS Network automates this.

### 2.3. Digital notary and enforceable acts

The **Spanish Notarial Council** has worked since 2024 on integrating the EUDI Wallet for:

- **Granting of public deeds** via Wallet identification
- **Notarial certifications** of electronic signatures
- **Digital notarial powers of attorney** with EU scope
- **Protests of electronic documents** in accordance with the Regulation

In 2026, leading Spanish notaries already allow clients to appear **remotely** via secure videoconference + EUDI Wallet, reducing notarial processing times from days to minutes.

## 3. Obligations and compliance for law firms

### 3.1. New responsibilities as a Relying Party

When a firm requests identity data through the EUDI Wallet, it acts as a **Relying Party** under eIDAS 2.0. This implies:

- **Minimum attribute request:** Only ask for strictly necessary data (minimization principle)
- **Informed consent:** Inform the user about the purpose of accessing their credentials
- **Transaction security:** Implement authentication protocols compliant with eIDAS architecture
- **Audit logging:** Maintain logs of identity transactions

### 3.2. Data protection and GDPR

Using the EUDI Wallet does not exempt compliance with the **GDPR**. Firms must consider:

| Aspect | Requirement |
|--------|-------------|
| Legal basis | Legitimate interest or legal obligation compliance (Law 10/2010 anti-money laundering) |
| Right of access | The data subject can request which attributes have been queried |
| Right to erasure | Transaction logs have defined retention periods |
| DPIA | Likely required for large-scale identification systems |
| DPO | Coordination with the Data Protection Officer for new flows |

## 5. Pending challenges in 2026

Despite the progress, operational challenges remain:

### National deployment fragmentation

Not all Member States have reached the same maturity level. Spain is at the forefront alongside Germany, the Netherlands, and the Nordic countries, but operations with lagging jurisdictions still require alternative mechanisms.

### Legacy system interoperability

Many firms maintain **practice management systems (ERP/PMS)** that were not designed to integrate decentralized credentials. Adoption requires middleware or platform upgrades.

### Client adoption

The EUDI Wallet depends on citizens downloading and configuring it. In May 2026, penetration exceeds 40% in Spain, but firms must maintain **alternative pathways** for clients who do not yet use it.
