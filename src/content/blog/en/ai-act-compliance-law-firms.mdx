## Introduction

The **EU Artificial Intelligence Regulation (AI Act)**, in force since 2024 and fully applicable in 2026, establishes an unprecedented regulatory framework for the use of AI systems. For law firms and legal departments adopting generative AI tools, compliance with this regulation is not optional: it's a legal obligation.

In this article, we analyze the specific obligations of the AI Act for the legal sector, implementation deadlines, and best practices to ensure regulatory compliance.

## What is the AI Act and why does it affect law firms?

The AI Act classifies AI systems according to their risk level:

- **Unacceptable risk:** Prohibited (e.g., social scoring systems)
- **High risk:** Strict requirements (e.g., decision-making systems in sensitive areas)
- **Limited risk:** Transparency obligations
- **Minimal risk:** No specific requirements

**Where do legal AI tools fit?** Most systems used by law firms (contract analysis, legal research, automated drafting) are classified as **limited risk**, but those involved in decisions significantly affecting fundamental rights may be considered **high risk**.

## Specific obligations for law firms

### 1. Transparency and client information

Law firms must clearly inform clients when using AI systems in service delivery:

- **What the AI does:** Explain which tasks it performs (document analysis, precedent search, etc.)
- **Limitations:** Indicate that output requires human supervision
- **Final decision:** Clarify that the lawyer makes the final decision

**Example information clause:**

> "In providing our services, we use artificial intelligence tools to optimize document analysis and legal research. All results generated by these tools are reviewed and validated by qualified lawyers before use."

### 2. Risk management and impact assessment

For high-risk systems, the AI Act requires:

```yaml
Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment:
  - Identify which rights may be affected
  - Assess the necessity and proportionality of the system
  - Implement mitigation measures
  - Document the entire process
```

**When is it necessary?** If the AI system participates in:
- Decisions on admissibility of claims
- Risk assessment in criminal proceedings
- Predictive systems for judicial outcomes
- Automated decision-making affecting rights

### 3. Human oversight (Human-in-the-loop)

The AI Act requires high-risk systems to be subject to **effective human oversight**:

- **Mandatory review:** A lawyer must review all outputs before use
- **Intervention capacity:** Ability to modify or reject AI suggestions
- **Training:** Staff must be trained to understand AI limitations

> **Case study:** An AI system suggests a procedural strategy based on case law. The lawyer must verify the applicability of cited precedents, evaluate consistency with the specific case, and decide if the strategy is appropriate.

### 4. Data protection and professional confidentiality

The intersection between the AI Act and GDPR generates specific obligations:

- **Data minimization:** Only use data strictly necessary to train or use the AI
- **Confidentiality:** Ensure client data is not used to train external models
- **Vendor contracts:** Specific clauses on data use and GDPR compliance

**Security checklist:**
- [ ] Is data processed locally or sent to external servers?
- [ ] Does the vendor guarantee they won't use data to train models?
- [ ] Is there a signed data processing agreement (DPA)?
- [ ] Have pseudonymization measures been implemented where possible?

## Implementation deadlines

The AI Act establishes a gradual timeline:

| Date | Obligation |
|------|-----------|
| August 2024 | Prohibition of unacceptable risk systems |
| August 2025 | Transparency obligations for generative AI models |
| **March 2026** | **Full applicability for high-risk systems** |
| August 2027 | Complete application to existing systems |

**We are at the critical moment:** Law firms must have their compliance measures implemented **now**.

## Best practices for law firms

### 1. Conduct an AI systems inventory

Document all AI tools your firm uses:

```markdown
| Tool | Provider | Function | Risk classification |
|------|----------|----------|---------------------|
| ContractAI | LegalTech Inc | Contract analysis | Limited risk |
| CasePredict | JurisSoft | Judgment prediction | High risk |
| LexSearch | ProprioAI | Legal search | Minimal risk |
```

### 2. Implement an internal AI use policy

Key elements:

- **Ethical principles:** Commitment to responsible AI use
- **Review procedures:** Who and how to review AI outputs
- **Mandatory training:** All staff must understand limitations and risks
- **Responsibility:** Designate an AI Act compliance officer

### 3. Establish quality controls

Implement verification mechanisms:

- **Double review:** AI outputs reviewed by at least one senior lawyer
- **Periodic audits:** Quarterly review of cases where AI was used
- **Accuracy metrics:** Track error rates of tools

### 4. Client communication

Be proactive in communication:

- **Contract clauses:** Include information about AI use in service contracts
- **Informed consent:** For sensitive cases, obtain express consent
- **Right to object:** Allow clients to request 100% human review

## Non-compliance penalties

The AI Act establishes significant fines:

- **Up to €35 million** or 7% of global turnover (whichever is higher) for prohibitions of unacceptable risk systems
- **Up to €15 million** or 3% of turnover for non-compliance with high-risk obligations
- **Up to €7.5 million** or 1.5% of turnover for non-compliance with transparency obligations

For medium and small firms, even the smaller fines can be devastating.

## Conclusion: The opportunity of proactive compliance

Compliance with the AI Act should not be seen as a burden, but as a **competitive advantage**:

- **Differentiation:** Corporate clients increasingly value ethical and regulated use of AI
- **Risk reduction:** Avoid sanctions and reputational damage
- **Quality improvement:** Implemented controls improve legal service quality
