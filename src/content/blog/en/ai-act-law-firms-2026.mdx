## Introduction: The AI Act Is Here

The **EU Artificial Intelligence Regulation** (AI Act) began progressive enforcement from 2024, and in March 2026 we're at a crucial point: many of its obligations are already applicable and law firms must urgently assess their compliance status.

If your firm uses AI tools for contract analysis, legal research, document management, or drafting assistance, this article will help you understand what the regulation requires and how to adapt practically.

## What Is the AI Act?

The AI Act is the world's first comprehensive regulatory framework for artificial intelligence systems. Its goal: ensure that AI deployed in the EU is **safe, transparent, and respectful of fundamental rights**.

### Risk-Based Approach

The regulation classifies AI systems into four categories based on risk level:

1. **Unacceptable Risk**: Prohibited (e.g., social scoring systems)
2. **High Risk**: Strict compliance requirements
3. **Limited Risk**: Transparency obligations
4. **Minimal Risk**: No specific obligations

## AI Systems in Law Firms: Which Category Applies?

### High Risk: Specific Cases

An AI system is considered **high risk** if used for:
- Evaluation of access to essential services (including critical legal services)
- Administration of justice (judicial assistance in decisions affecting people)

**Practical example**: If your firm uses AI to automatically evaluate case viability or recommend strategies affecting clients' fundamental rights, it could be classified as high risk.

### Limited Risk: Most Common

Most current LegalTech tools fall into this category:
- Customer service chatbots
- Generative AI writing assistants (GPT, Claude, etc.)
- Document analysis tools
- AI-enhanced legal search systems

**Main obligation**: **Transparency**. You must inform users and clients when they interact with AI.

### Minimal Risk: Basic Tools

- Spell checkers
- Basic searches without machine learning
- Traditional management software

These tools don't require specific AI Act compliance.

## Practical Obligations for Law Firms

### 1. AI Systems Inventory

**Immediate action**: Create a registry of all tools using AI:

```markdown
| Tool | Provider | Use | Risk Category |
|------|----------|-----|---------------|
| CaseAnalyzer AI | Provider X | Contract analysis | Limited |
| LegalBot | Provider Y | Client service | Limited |
| DocuSearch Pro | Provider Z | Doc search | Minimal |
```

### 2. Client Transparency

If you use generative AI (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.) for:
- Document drafts
- Case summaries
- Preliminary research

**You must clearly inform**:
> "This document/analysis has been prepared with artificial intelligence assistance and reviewed by a licensed attorney."

### 3. Documentation and Traceability

For **high-risk** systems (if applicable):
- Maintain decision logs
- Document training and validation
- Establish human oversight mechanisms
- Conduct impact assessments

### 4. Providers and Liability

**Key question**: Who is responsible?

- If you use a commercial tool → The **provider** is responsible for technical compliance
- If you train/customize the system → There may be **shared liability**
- If you deploy your own AI → You are the **primary responsible party**

**Recommendation**: Demand AI Act compliance certificates from your LegalTech providers.

## Common Use Cases and How to Comply

### Case 1: GPT Writing Assistant

**Classification**: Limited risk

**Compliance**:
- ✅ Inform client that AI is used
- ✅ Mandatory human review of all output
- ✅ Don't input confidential data without protection measures

### Case 2: Predictive Litigation Analysis

**Classification**: Potentially high risk

**Compliance**:
- ✅ Formal risk assessment
- ✅ Dataset and model documentation
- ✅ Human oversight mechanism
- ✅ Client complaint procedure

### Case 3: Initial Consultation Chatbot

**Classification**: Limited risk

**Compliance**:
- ✅ Clear notice "You are talking to an AI assistant"
- ✅ Human contact option always available
- ✅ Doesn't make binding legal decisions

## Penalties: What Are the Risks?

The AI Act provides for significant fines:
- Up to **€35 million** or **7% of annual turnover** (whichever is higher) for serious violations
- Up to **€15 million** or **3%** for incorrect information to authorities
- Up to **€7.5 million** or **1.5%** for other violations

**Reality for small/medium firms**: Authorities prioritize serious cases and large providers, but the **reputational risk** of non-compliance is significant.
