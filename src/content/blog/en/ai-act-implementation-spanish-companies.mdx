## Introduction

The **European Union Artificial Intelligence Regulation (AI Act)** represents the world's most ambitious regulatory framework for AI. Following its entry into force, Spanish companies face a complex landscape of obligations and compliance deadlines. This article provides a practical guide to navigate this new regulatory reality.

## What is the AI Act and Why Does it Matter?

The AI Act is the world's first comprehensive legal framework for artificial intelligence. Unlike GDPR, which regulates personal data, the AI Act focuses on **AI systems** and their impact on fundamental rights, safety, and democracy.

### Main objectives of the regulation

- **Protect fundamental rights** of European citizens
- **Guarantee the safety** of AI systems
- **Foster responsible innovation** in the European single market
- **Establish a harmonized framework** that prevents regulatory fragmentation

## Classification of AI Systems by Risk Level

The AI Act adopts a **risk-based approach**, categorizing systems into four levels:

### 1. Unacceptable Risk (Prohibited)

Systems that pose a clear threat to people's safety, livelihoods, or rights:

- Government social scoring systems
- Subliminal manipulation of behavior
- Exploitation of vulnerabilities of specific groups
- Real-time biometric identification in public spaces (with limited exceptions)

> **Important:** Use of these systems is **completely prohibited** in the EU, with penalties of up to €35 million or 7% of global turnover.

### 2. High Risk

Systems that can significantly affect safety or fundamental rights. Include applications in:

- **Human resources management:** Employee selection and evaluation systems
- **Essential services:** Access to education, public services, insurance
- **Law enforcement:** Credibility assessment of evidence or testimony
- **Migration and borders:** Document authenticity verification
- **Administration of justice:** Assistance in investigation and interpretation of facts

**Obligations for high-risk systems:**

1. Robust risk management system
2. Training data governance and quality
3. Comprehensive technical documentation
4. Transparency and provision of information to users
5. Effective human oversight
6. Accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity
7. Registration in EU database

### 3. Limited Risk

Systems with specific transparency obligations:

- Chatbots and virtual assistants
- Content generation systems (deepfakes, synthetic texts)
- Biometric categorization systems

**Main obligation:** Clearly inform users they are interacting with AI.

### 4. Minimal Risk

Most AI applications fall into this category (video games, spam filters, etc.). They have no specific AI Act obligations, though they remain subject to other regulations (GDPR, sectoral regulations).

## Concrete Steps for Compliance

### Step 1: Inventory and Classification (March-June 2026)

**Immediate action:** Conduct a **complete inventory** of all AI systems your company uses or develops.

For each system, determine:

- What is its purpose and functionality?
- What risk category does it fall into?
- Who is the provider? Are they established in the EU?
- Who is responsible for deployment?

```markdown
Example system record:
- Name: Customer Service ChatBot
- Provider: TechCorp SL
- Category: Limited Risk
- Use: Customer interaction on website
- Responsible: Digital Department
- Compliance status: Pending - Requires AI notice
```

### Step 2: Compliance Gap Assessment

For **high-risk** systems, evaluate:

- Is there complete technical documentation?
- Has conformity assessment been performed?
- Is there an implemented risk management system?
- Is there a human oversight mechanism?
- Does training data meet quality requirements?

**Recommendation:** Hire external specialized audit for critical systems.

### Step 3: Implementation of Technical and Organizational Measures

**For high-risk systems:**

1. **Comprehensive technical documentation**
   - Detailed description of system and its purpose
   - Technical design specifications
   - Information about training data and methodology
   - Validation and testing results

2. **Risk management system**
   - Identification of known and foreseeable risks
   - Risk estimation and evaluation
   - Mitigation measures
   - Continuous review process

3. **Data governance**
   - Compliant data management practices
   - Representative, bias-free datasets
   - Documented statistical characteristics

4. **Mandatory registration**
   - Registration in European high-risk AI systems database
   - Updates upon substantial changes

**For limited risk systems:**

- Implement clear AI interaction notices
- Label AI-generated content (texts, images, videos)
- Maintain records of synthetic content generation

### Step 4: Training and Awareness

Compliance is not just technological:

- **Train personnel** on AI Act obligations
- **Designate responsible parties** for AI regulatory compliance
- **Establish processes** for approving new AI systems
- **Create culture** of responsible AI in the organization

## Critical Deadlines for 2026-2027

The AI Act has a **progressive implementation schedule**:

| Date | Obligation |
|------|-----------|
| **August 2026** | Prohibition of unacceptable risk systems |
| **February 2027** | Obligations for general-purpose AI systems |
| **August 2027** | Full application for high-risk systems |

**Recommendation:** Don't wait for deadlines. The market is already demanding compliance.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

The penalty regime is similar to GDPR:

- **Level 1 (serious violations):** Up to €35M or 7% global turnover
- **Level 2 (obligation violations):** Up to €15M or 3% global turnover
- **Level 3 (incorrect information):** Up to €7.5M or 1.5% global turnover

National data protection authorities and sectoral authorities will act as supervisory authorities.

## Generative AI and General-Purpose Models

**Generative AI** (ChatGPT, Claude, Midjourney, etc.) deserves special attention:

**Obligations for foundation model providers:**

- Transparency about capabilities and limitations
- Complete technical documentation
- Copyright compliance policy
- Public summary of training data

**Additional obligations for "systemic risk" models:**

- Systemic risk assessment and mitigation
- Robust adversarial testing
- Serious incident tracking
- High-level cybersecurity

**For business users:**

Even if your company doesn't develop these models, **you are responsible** for how you use them. Implement:

- Acceptable use policies for generative AI
- Human review of generated content
- Labeling of synthetic content
- Training in bias and hallucination detection

## Integration with GDPR and Other Regulations

The AI Act **does not replace** GDPR, but complements it:

### Overlapping areas

- **Transparency:** GDPR requires information about automated decisions; AI Act adds specific requirements
- **Data subject rights:** Both recognize right to human intervention
- **Impact assessments:** GDPR's DPIA can be integrated with AI Act conformity assessment

### Integration best practices

1. **Joint assessment:** Conduct DPIA and AI Act conformity assessment simultaneously
2. **Unified documentation:** Create a documentation framework covering both regulations
3. **Shared responsibility:** DPO can coordinate with AI Act compliance officer

## Final Recommendations for Spanish Companies

### 1. Act now, don't wait

The B2B market already demands compliance. Corporate clients and public administrations will request conformity certifications.

### 2. Prioritize high-risk systems

Focus resources on critical systems. A limited-risk chatbot can wait; an employee selection system cannot.

### 3. Collaborate with providers

If you use third-party systems, demand compliance documentation. Include AI Act clauses in contracts.

### 4. Invest in training

Technical-legal knowledge will be a competitive differentiator. Build multidisciplinary teams.

### 5. Consider the AI Act as an opportunity

Compliance is not just a cost; it's a competitive advantage over non-European competitors and a guarantee of trust for customers.

## Conclusion

The AI Act marks the beginning of a new era in artificial intelligence governance. For Spanish companies, it represents both a compliance challenge and an opportunity to lead in responsible AI.

The key to success lies in addressing compliance in a **proactive, structured, and strategic** manner. Organizations that integrate these principles into their digital DNA will not only avoid penalties but build sustainable competitive advantages.
