## Introduction

Since September 2025, the **Data Act Regulation** (Regulation (EU) 2023/2854) has been fully applicable across all European Union member states. Eight months later, in May 2026, law firms and technology companies are in the thick of adapting to a framework that redefines who can access data, how it can be shared, and under what conditions.

Unlike the GDPR, which focuses on privacy and consent, the Data Act regulates the **data economy**: it prevents data hoarding, guarantees portability between cloud providers, and establishes a clear framework for data exchange between businesses and with public authorities. For law firms, it represents both a compliance obligation and a strategic advisory opportunity.

## 1. What Is the Data Act and Who Does It Affect?

The Data Act applies to three main groups:

| Group | Description | Examples |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **Manufacturers of connected products** | IoT devices that generate data | Wearables, vehicles, smart appliances |
| **Cloud service providers** | Cloud computing platforms | AWS, Azure, Google Cloud |
| **Data holders** | Entities that accumulate usage data | SaaS platforms, marketplaces |

### Territorial Scope

The Regulation affects any company offering products or services in the European market, regardless of its registered office. This includes US providers, Asian providers, and, of course, Spanish companies.

## 2. Five Pillars of the Data Act

### 2.1. Right to Access Personal and Non-Personal Data

The user of a connected product has the right to **access the data generated** by their use, both personal and non-personal. The manufacturer must facilitate access free of charge, in real time, and in a machine-readable format.

**Legal implication:** Software license contracts and IoT device terms of use must be reviewed to remove clauses that limit this right.

### 2.2. B2B Data Sharing with Trusted Third Parties

When a user authorizes a third party to access their data, the data holder (manufacturer or platform) **is obliged to share it** under fair, reasonable, and non-discriminatory (FRAND) conditions.

**Critical point:** The third party cannot use that data to develop competing products with the original product or service. This competitive safeguard must be drafted with precision in sharing agreements.

### 2.3. Cloud Portability and Elimination of Vendor Lock-In

Cloud service providers must facilitate the **migration of data and applications** to competitors. This includes:

- Interoperable and exportable formats
- Elimination of discriminatory economic charges for exit
- Documented transition tools

**Legal implication:** Traditional cloud contracts with high egress fees or proprietary formats are directly contrary to the Regulation.

### 2.4. B2G Data Sharing in Public Emergencies

In public emergency situations (pandemics, natural disasters, large-scale cyberattacks), companies are obliged to share data with public authorities. The mechanism must be foreseen and access must be provided immediately.

### 2.5. Prohibition of Unfair Contract Terms

The Data Act includes a blacklist of **contractual clauses considered unfair**, similar to Directive 93/13/EEC but specific to the digital environment. Examples:

- Exemption from liability for data loss
- Unilateral prohibition on exporting data
- Disproportionate indemnification clauses in favor of the provider

## 3. Relationship with GDPR: Complementarity, Not Substitution

A common mistake is to treat the Data Act as a modification of the GDPR. It is not. Both regulations **coexist and complement each other**:

| Aspect | GDPR | Data Act |
|--------|------|----------|
| **Objective** | Protect fundamental rights | Facilitate the data economy |
| **Scope** | Personal data | Personal and non-personal data |
| **Legal basis** | Consent, legitimate interest | Legal obligation of manufacturer/provider |
| **Sharing** | Requires legal basis and transparency | Mandatory technical and contractual mechanism |
| **Enforcement** | DPA, supervisory authorities | Authorities designated by each member state |

**Practical advice:** When a client requests to share personal data through an authorized third party, the law firm must verify both Data Act compliance (technical mechanism) and GDPR compliance (legal basis, data subject information).

## 4. Action Guide for Law Firms

### 4.1. IoT Contract Audit

Review client contracts for manufacturers to ensure:

- [ ] Users are informed of their data access right
- [ ] An API or accessible download interface is provided
- [ ] No clauses exclude portability to third parties
- [ ] Data is provided in a structured, machine-readable format

### 4.2. Cloud Contract Review

Cloud service agreements must include:

- [ ] Reasonable and transparent exit (egress) fees
- [ ] Commitment to interoperability with open standards
- [ ] Documented data migration procedure
- [ ] Maximum deadlines for data return/deletion post-contract

### 4.3. Drafting B2B Sharing Agreements

When a client acts as a data holder, the agreement with the third party must define:

- **Permitted purposes:** Explicit use, prohibition on developing competing products
- **Security measures:** Encryption, pseudonymization, access control
- **Retention periods:** Destruction or return upon termination
- **Compensation:** The Data Act allows reasonable compensation for direct costs, but not data monopolization

### 4.4. Compliance Strategy for Business Clients

For companies operating in multiple European jurisdictions, we recommend:

1. **Appoint an internal Data Act officer** (may coincide with the DPO, but with differentiated functions)
2. **Create a data register** generated by connected products and its classification
3. **Update privacy policies** to reflect the right of access to non-personal data
4. **Review cloud provider contracts** every 12 months

## 5. Sanctions and Non-Compliance Risks

Each member state must designate a supervisory authority. In Spain, the **AEPD** or a new sectoral authority is expected to assume these powers. Sanctions include:

- Administrative fines proportional to the severity
- Obligations to cease practices contrary to the Regulation
- Civil liability for damages arising from contractual non-compliance

Additionally, reputational risk is significant: in a market where data transparency is valued by consumers and investors, Data Act non-compliance can translate into lost B2B contracts.

## 6. Business Opportunities for Law Firms

The Data Act generates demand for specialized advice in:

- **Technology due diligence** in M&A operations: verifying that the target complies with portability and access obligations
- **Contractual litigation:** claims for unfair clauses or refusal of data access
- **Integrated compliance:** programs that combine GDPR, Data Act, NIS2, and DORA
- **Drafting IoT and cloud contracts:** a technical niche with high fee valuation

## Conclusion

The Data Act is not a minor regulation: it is the economic pillar of the European digital single market. For law firms advising technology, industrial, or digital service companies, mastery of this Regulation has become an **essential competence**.

The key to success lies in acting proactively: reviewing existing contracts, training commercial and technical teams, and establishing compliance processes before the supervisory authority initiates inspection proceedings.
