## Introduction

The European Union's **Artificial Intelligence Act (AI Act)** has entered its critical implementation phase in 2026. For Spanish law firms, this isn't just another regulatory change: it represents a fundamental transformation in how they must evaluate, implement, and manage AI tools in their professional practice.

In this article, we analyze the specific obligations affecting the legal sector and provide a practical roadmap for regulatory compliance.

## What is the AI Act and Why Does It Matter Now?

The AI Act is the world's first comprehensive regulatory framework for artificial intelligence. Approved in 2024, it establishes a risk-based classification system that determines compliance obligations based on how AI systems are used.

**Key implementation timeline for 2026:**
- February 2026: Prohibitions on high-risk AI systems
- August 2026: Obligations for general-purpose AI systems
- February 2027: Full obligations for all high-risk systems

Law firms must act **now** to be prepared.

## Risk Classification: Where Does Your Firm Fit?

The AI Act classifies AI systems into four categories:

### 1. Unacceptable Risk (Prohibited)

Includes systems for cognitive manipulation, social scoring by public authorities, and real-time biometric identification in public spaces (with exceptions).

**Legal impact:** Minimal for law firms, but relevant in cases defending affected clients.

### 2. High Risk

This is where law firms need to pay maximum attention. High-risk systems are those that:

- **Make decisions about people** (personnel selection, credit assessment)
- **Administer essential services** (justice, migration)
- **Affect fundamental rights**

**Examples in the legal context:**
- Judgment prediction systems
- AI for candidate selection in legal departments
- Litigation risk analysis tools that directly influence strategic decisions

### 3. Limited Risk (Transparency)

Most generative AI tools used by law firms fall here:
- ChatGPT for drafting
- Document summarization systems
- Virtual assistants for clients

**Main obligation:** Inform users they are interacting with AI.

### 4. Minimal Risk

Basic automation tools, spam filters, spell checkers.

**Obligations:** None specific.

## Specific Obligations for Law Firms

### For High-Risk Systems

If your firm develops or implements high-risk systems, you must:

**1. Conformity Assessment**
```
Document:
├── Intended purpose of the system
├── Training data (origin, quality, biases)
├── Accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity
├── Effective human oversight
└── Transparency for users
```

**2. Registration in the EU Database**

All high-risk systems must be registered in the EU public database before market placement.

**3. Quality Management Systems**

Implement documented processes for:
- Data governance
- Post-market monitoring
- Incident management

**4. Human Oversight**

> "AI must be a support tool, never a substitute for professional legal judgment."

Systems must be designed to allow effective human intervention in critical decisions.

### For Limited-Risk Systems

The transparency obligation requires:

**Common use cases in law firms:**

```markdown
❌ Incorrect:
"We have drafted your contract."

✅ Correct:
"We have used AI to generate a contract draft, 
which has been reviewed and adapted by our legal team."
```

**In client communications:**
- Inform when using chatbots or virtual assistants
- Declare AI use in automatically generated documents
- Maintain records of which tools are used and for what purpose

## Professional Ethics: Beyond the AI Act

The Spanish Bar Association is developing specific protocols. Key points:

### Confidentiality and Professional Secrecy

**Problem:** Cloud tools like ChatGPT process data on external servers.

**Solution:**
- Use enterprise versions with privacy guarantees
- Anonymize information before processing with AI
- Evaluate on-premise alternatives for sensitive data

### Professional Liability

The lawyer is **always responsible** for the final result, regardless of tools used.

**Best practices:**
- Never send AI outputs directly to clients without review
- Document the human review process
- Train the team on AI limitations and biases

### Digital Competence

Professional associations are integrating AI competence as a continuing education requirement.

## Compliance Roadmap: 5 Practical Steps

### Step 1: Tool Audit (March-April 2026)

Inventory all AI systems used by the firm:

```
Tool      | Provider   | Use          | Risk Classification
----------|-----------|--------------|--------------------
ChatGPT   | OpenAI    | Drafts       | Limited Risk
LexisAI   | LexisNexis| Research     | Limited Risk
DocuSign  | DocuSign  | Signatures   | Minimal Risk
```

### Step 2: Risk Assessment (May 2026)

For each high or limited-risk tool:
- Review terms of service regarding AI Act compliance
- Request certifications from the provider
- Evaluate alternatives if no guarantees exist

### Step 3: Transparency Protocols (June 2026)

Create communication templates:
- Clauses for legal service contracts
- Notices for chatbots and virtual assistants
- Disclaimer for AI-generated documents

### Step 4: Internal Training (July-August 2026)

Train the team on:
- AI Act obligations
- Responsible use of AI tools
- Review and oversight protocols

### Step 5: Documentation and Monitoring (Ongoing)

Establish:
- AI use log per case/client
- Incident reporting protocol
- Biannual tool and compliance review
