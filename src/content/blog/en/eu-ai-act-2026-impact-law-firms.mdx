## Introduction

One year before the full entry into force of the European Union's Artificial Intelligence Regulation (AI Act), Spanish law firms face a new regulatory paradigm that transforms both their internal operations and the advice they provide to clients. In this article, we analyze the current state of implementation and the specific obligations for the legal sector.

## The AI Act: Implementation Status in 2026

**Regulation (EU) 2024/1689** on Artificial Intelligence establishes a harmonized framework for the development, marketing, and use of AI systems in the European Union. In March 2026, we are in the crucial phase of applying obligations for high-risk systems, with active supervision from national competent authorities.

### Risk-Based Classification

The AI Act classifies AI systems into four categories:

1. **Unacceptable Risk**: Prohibited (e.g., social scoring, subliminal manipulation)
2. **High Risk**: Require conformity assessment and registration
3. **Limited Risk**: Transparency obligations
4. **Minimal Risk**: No specific restrictions

For law firms, it is especially relevant to understand which systems they use internally or recommend to clients that may be classified as high-risk.

## AI in Legal Practice: Which Systems Are High-Risk?

### Internal Firm Systems

Most AI tools used internally by law firms are **NOT** considered high-risk:

- ✅ **Minimal/limited risk**: Drafting assistants, contract analysis, legal research, document management
- ⚠️ **Transparency required**: Customer service chatbots (obligation to inform that it's AI)

However, there are exceptions:

- ❌ **Potentially high-risk**: AI systems that make automated decisions in judicial or administrative proceedings

### Client Advisory

Law firms must assess whether their clients develop, market, or use high-risk AI systems:

**Critical sectors where AI may be high-risk:**
- Human resources (automated personnel selection)
- Financial services (algorithmic credit scoring)
- Education (automated student assessment)
- Administration of justice (judicial support systems)
- Critical infrastructure management

## Compliance Obligations for Law Firms

### 1. Technology Due Diligence

Law firms must implement **AI audit** processes to:

```markdown
✓ Inventory all AI systems used internally
✓ Classify each system according to AI Act risk level
✓ Document the legal basis and purpose of each system
✓ Assess GDPR compliance (especially for personal data)
✓ Review contracts with legal tech software providers
```

### 2. Client Transparency

When using AI in the provision of legal services:

- **Actively inform** the client about the use of AI tools
- **Explain the role** of AI in document generation or analysis
- **Maintain human oversight** over AI-generated outputs
- **Document** the human review and validation process

> "AI can optimize legal research, but the final legal judgment must always be human and properly reasoned." — Recommendation of the Spanish General Council of Lawyers

### 3. Training and Internal Governance

Modern law firms establish:

- **AI and Ethics Committee**: Internal body to evaluate new tools
- **Usage protocols**: Clear guidelines on what can/cannot be done with AI
- **Continuous training**: Team capacity-building in legal AI and its limitations
- **Decision log**: Documentation of risk assessments and measures adopted

## Client Advisory: New Practice Area

The AI Act creates significant opportunities for specialized law firms:

### AI Act Compliance Services

Clients developing or using AI systems need:

1. **Initial risk assessment**: Is my system high-risk?
2. **Conformity assessment**: Certification process for high-risk systems
3. **Technical documentation**: Preparation of required documentation
4. **EU database registration**: Registration of high-risk systems
5. **Supplier contracts**: Review of liability and compliance clauses
6. **Incident management**: Notification protocols to competent authorities

### Litigation and Defense

An increase is expected in:

- **Enforcement proceedings** for AI Act non-compliance (fines up to €35M or 7% of turnover)
- **Administrative appeals** against supervisory authority decisions
- **Civil litigation** for damages caused by defective AI systems

## Interaction with Other Regulations

The AI Act does not operate in a regulatory vacuum. Law firms must consider the interaction with:

### GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)

- AI systems processing personal data must comply with **both** regulations
- **Impact assessments**: If the AI Act requires conformity assessment AND the system processes personal data, a DPIA (Data Protection Impact Assessment) may also be necessary
- **Data subject rights**: Transparency, explainability, not to be subject to automated decisions

### AI Liability Directive

Directive (EU) 2024/2853 on AI liability facilitates damage claims:

- **Presumption of causality** in certain cases
- **Facilitated burden of proof** for injured parties
- **Joint and several liability** between developers and users in certain circumstances

### Digital Services Act (DSA)

For platforms offering online legal services with AI components:

- Transparency obligations about recommendation systems
- AI-assisted content moderation
- Independent audits (very large platforms)

## Practical Recommendations for Law Firms

### Immediate Action (Q2 2026)

1. **AI Audit**: Conduct complete inventory of AI tools used
2. **Internal policy**: Draft firm AI usage policy
3. **Basic training**: Informational session for entire team on AI Act
4. **Contract review**: Update engagement templates to include AI clauses

### Medium Term (2026-2027)

1. **Specialization**: Consider creating an AI and technology compliance department or practice
2. **Certifications**: Obtain certifications in AI system auditing
3. **Expert network**: Collaborate with engineers, data scientists, and technology auditors
4. **Continuous updates**: Monitor guidelines and interpretative criteria from the European Commission and AEPD

### Business Opportunities

Law firms that position themselves early can capture:

- **Preventive advisory** to companies developing AI
- **Compliance as a Service** for AI startups
- **Technology due diligence** in M&A transactions
- **Corporate training** on AI Act and GDPR
- **Representation** before competent authorities

## Conclusion

The EU AI Act represents one of the most significant regulatory changes of the last decade for the legal sector. Law firms that understand its implications early and develop expertise in this area will be better positioned to:

1. **Optimize** their own operations with compliant AI
2. **Effectively advise** clients in regulated sectors
3. **Capture** new business lines in a high-demand area
4. **Mitigate** reputational and enforcement risks

The digital transformation of the legal sector is unstoppable, but it must be **responsible, ethical, and law-abiding**. The AI Act is not a barrier, but a framework that ensures technological innovation respects fundamental European values.
