## Introduction

The European Union's **Artificial Intelligence Regulation** (AI Act) came into force in 2024, but 2026 marks the beginning of its practical application. After two years of preparation, companies and legal professionals now face concrete obligations that will transform the use of AI systems in Europe.

In this article, we analyze the main regulatory developments, compliance deadlines in force in 2026, and the strategies that Spanish companies are implementing to adapt to this new regulatory reality.

## The AI Act: Context and Risk Classification

The European Regulation establishes a **risk-based approach**, classifying AI systems into four categories:

### 1. Unacceptable Risk (Prohibited)

Systems that manipulate human behavior, exploit vulnerabilities, or implement state social scoring. Their use has been completely prohibited since August 2024.

### 2. High Risk (Strict Regulation)

Includes AI systems for:
- **Personnel selection and human resources management**
- **Credit assessment and financial scoring**
- **Judicial and law enforcement systems**
- **Critical infrastructure management**
- **Education and professional training**

These systems require conformity assessments, external audits, and comprehensive technical documentation. **In 2026, companies operating these systems must have already completed their first audits.**

### 3. Limited Risk (Transparency)

Chatbots, virtual assistants, and deepfake content generators must **clearly inform** users that they are interacting with AI. Transparency obligations have been fully enforceable since February 2026.

### 4. Minimal Risk (No Special Regulation)

Spam filters, AI-powered video games, and other low-impact systems have no specific obligations under the AI Act.

## Main Obligations in 2026

### For AI System Providers

Developers and companies commercializing high-risk systems must:

1. **Implement risk management systems:** Continuous risk identification throughout the system's lifecycle.

2. **Ensure data governance:** Representative training sets, free from discriminatory biases, and properly documented.

3. **Prepare complete technical documentation:** Including model architecture, training data, test results, and human oversight measures.

4. **Establish logging systems:** Traceability of all relevant decisions made by the system.

5. **Provide clear information to users:** Instruction manuals, system capabilities and limitations.

```python
# Example of logging implementation for AI Act compliance
import logging
from datetime import datetime

class AIActLogger:
    def __init__(self, system_name):
        self.system_name = system_name
        self.logger = logging.getLogger(f"AIAct.{system_name}")
    
    def log_decision(self, input_data, output, confidence, user_id):
        """Log AI system decisions according to AI Act"""
        log_entry = {
            "timestamp": datetime.utcnow().isoformat(),
            "system": self.system_name,
            "user_id": user_id,
            "input_hash": hash(str(input_data)),
            "output": output,
            "confidence": confidence,
            "human_review_required": confidence < 0.85
        }
        self.logger.info(log_entry)
        return log_entry
```

### For Professional Users (Companies)

Organizations implementing high-risk AI systems must:

1. **Use systems according to instructions:** Follow provider guidelines and maintain adequate human oversight.

2. **Monitor operation:** Continuous monitoring and reporting of serious incidents.

3. **Preserve automatic logs:** For periods allowing compliance audits (generally 6 months to 5 years depending on the sector).

## Impact on the Legal Sector

### Predictive Analytics and Dispute Resolution

AI systems that predict judicial outcomes or suggest legal strategies fall into the **high-risk** category. Law firms using these tools must:

- Verify that the provider complies with the AI Act
- Maintain human review of all recommendations
- Document decisions based (even partially) on AI outputs

### Contract Review and Analysis

AI systems for legal document analysis are generally considered **limited risk**, but companies must:

> **Warn clients when a contract has been reviewed (wholly or partially) by AI systems, especially if it affects critical decisions.**

### Technology Due Diligence

Lawyers specializing in technology compliance are developing new service lines:

- **AI Act audits:** Compliance review for high-risk systems
- **AI provider due diligence:** Regulatory risk assessment in acquisitions or contracting
- **Internal policy design:** Implementation of governance frameworks for responsible AI use

## Practical Cases: Spanish Companies in 2026

### Case 1: Fintech and Credit Scoring

A Spanish P2P lending startup implemented an AI-based credit assessment system. After the AI Act came into force, it performed:

- External conformity audit (€45,000)
- Model retraining eliminating detected biases
- Implementation of human appeal system for negative decisions
- Update of terms and conditions explaining AI use

**Result:** Compliance achieved, but 18% increase in operational costs.

### Case 2: Human Resources and Personnel Selection

An HR consultancy used AI for initial CV filtering. The system was classified as high-risk under the AI Act:

- Investment in detailed technical documentation
- Implementation of human oversight in every decision
- Specific training for recruiters on system limitations
- Detailed recording of all system recommendations

**Result:** Greater transparency, although 25% slowdown in initial processes.

## Sanctions and Penalty System

The AI Act establishes fines proportional to the severity of the infringement:

| Infringement | Maximum Fine |
|--------------|--------------|
| Use of prohibited systems | **€35 million or 7% global turnover** |
| Non-compliance with high-risk obligations | **€15 million or 3% global turnover** |
| Incorrect information to authorities | **€7.5 million or 1.5% global turnover** |

**In Spain, the Spanish Agency for the Supervision of Artificial Intelligence (AESIA)** is the competent authority to supervise compliance.

## Compliance Strategies for 2026

### 1. AI Systems Inventory

Create a complete register of all AI systems used in the organization, classifying them by risk level.

### 2. Provider Assessment

Verify that all high-risk system providers comply with the AI Act and request conformity documentation.

### 3. Internal Training

Train employees on:
- AI system limitations
- Need for human oversight
- Escalation procedures when AI shows low confidence

### 4. Governance Policies

Establish internal AI committees to oversee:
- Fundamental rights impact assessments
- Decisions on acquiring new AI systems
- Response to incidents and system failures

### 5. Periodic Audits

Implement quarterly or semi-annual reviews of high-risk system operation, with special attention to possible biases or deviations.

## Future Perspectives

The AI Act is just the beginning. The European Commission is already working on:

- **Harmonized standards:** Sector-specific technical standards (expected for 2027)
- **European certifications:** Conformity seals facilitating cross-border trade
- **Voluntary codes of conduct:** For minimal risk systems wanting to demonstrate best practices

Additionally, 2026 case law is beginning to form the first precedents on Regulation interpretation, especially in gray areas such as risk categorization.
