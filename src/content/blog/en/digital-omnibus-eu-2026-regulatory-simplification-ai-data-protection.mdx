## Introduction

In November 2025, the **European Commission** published the **"Digital Omnibus Package,"** a set of proposals promising to transform the digital regulatory landscape of the European Union. This package aims to simplify and harmonize multiple existing legal frameworks, including the **GDPR**, the **ePrivacy Directive**, the **Data Act**, the **Data Governance Act**, the **NIS2 Directive**, and the **AI Act**.

For law firms and compliance officers advising European companies, understanding these changes is crucial. This article provides an in-depth analysis of the Digital Omnibus implications and how it will affect legal practice in 2026.

## Context: Why is the EU Simplifying its Digital Regulation?

### The Draghi Report and European Competitiveness

In 2024, the **Draghi Report** on European competitiveness identified that Europe's digital regulatory framework had become excessively fragmented, complex, and burdensome for industry. With the rapid rise of **artificial intelligence** and the proliferation of digital regulations in Europe, the Commission faced:

- **Competitive pressures** from the U.S. and China
- **Need for high-quality data** to develop AI in Europe
- **Regulatory overload** hindering innovation
- **Fragmentation** between different legal frameworks

The response: the **Digital Omnibus Package**, published on November 19, 2025, as part of the new **European Data Union Strategy**.

### Shift in Regulatory Approach

The package represents a significant "course correction" in Brussels' approach to digital regulation. Less than a decade after GDPR implementation - considered the gold standard worldwide for data protection - the Commission is reviewing some norms it deems excessively burdensome.

## Structure of the Digital Omnibus Package

The package is divided into two main components:

### 1. Digital Omnibus Regulation Proposal

Covers reforms in:
- **GDPR** - General Data Protection Regulation
- **ePrivacy Directive** - Electronic privacy and cookies
- **Data Act** - Data access and use
- **Data Governance Act** - Data governance
- **NIS2 Directive** - Cybersecurity

### 2. Digital Omnibus on AI Regulation Proposal

Focused specifically on:
- **AI Act** - Artificial Intelligence Regulation

This separation is due to the urgency of acting on AI Act deadlines expiring in August 2026.

## Current Status of the Legislative Process

### Key Timeline

- **November 19, 2025**: Publication of the Digital Omnibus package
- **March 13, 2026**: The European Council adopts its position
- **March 26, 2026**: The European Parliament adopts its position
- **Current**: Negotiations between Council, Parliament, and Commission for the final version

### Institutional Positions

The Council adopted its position on March 13, 2026, reflecting some of the concerns raised by the **EDPB and EDPS Joint Opinion** (European data protection authorities). Three days later, on March 26, the European Parliament adopted its own position.

Both institutions are now negotiating with the Commission to agree on the final version of amendments to the AI Act.

## Key Points of AI Act Proposals

### 1. Extension of Compliance Deadlines for High-Risk AI

The AI Act classifies AI systems by risk level. "High-risk" systems are subject to strict compliance requirements.

**Proposal**: Extend compliance deadlines to give companies that aren't yet ready more time.

**Implication for law firms**: Clients developing or deploying high-risk AI will have more time to adapt, but need strategic advice on how to manage this extension.

### 2. Extension of Grace Period for AI Transparency

AI transparency requirements include:

- Clear information obligations to users
- Labeling of AI-generated content
- Disclosure when users interact with AI systems

**Proposal**: Extend the grace period to comply with these requirements.

**Implication for law firms**: Clients must prepare for transparency obligations, but with a more flexible timeline.

### 3. Use of Special Category Data for Bias Detection

GDPR classifies certain data as "special categories" requiring enhanced protection (e.g., health data, political opinions, sexual orientation).

**Proposal**: Allow use of this data to **detect and correct biases** in AI systems, under specific conditions.

**Implication for law firms**: New GDPR exception requiring careful risk-benefit analysis for clients.

### 4. Simplification of Registration Obligations

The AI Act requires mandatory registrations for certain AI systems.

**Proposal**: Simplify and rationalize these obligations to reduce administrative burden.

**Implication for law firms**: Less bureaucracy for clients, but continued monitoring of which systems require registration.

## Reforms to GDPR and Other Data Frameworks

### GDPR Simplification

Although GDPR reforms are at an earlier stage of the legislative process, proposals include:

- **Reduction of administrative burdens** for small and medium enterprises
- **Harmonization of procedures** between data protection authorities
- **Greater clarity on requirements** for international transfers
- **Simplification of DPIAs** (Data Protection Impact Assessments)

**Implication for law firms**: Clients, especially SMEs, could benefit from simplified requirements, but need to stay updated on which changes are implemented and when.

### Harmonization with ePrivacy and Data Act

The goal is to create a more coherent framework that:

- Eliminates contradictions between GDPR and ePrivacy
- Facilitates data sharing under the Data Act
- Simplifies compliance for companies operating under multiple regimes

## Opportunities for Law Firms

### Strategic Advisory During Transition Period

The period from now until final implementation offers unique opportunities:

- **Transition planning**: Help clients prepare for changes
- **Impact analysis**: Assess how reforms will affect each client
- **Compliance optimization**: Identify new flexibilities to reduce costs

### AI and Data Specialization

The Digital Omnibus increases demand for:

- **AI-specialist lawyers** who understand the AI Act and its reforms
- **Data protection experts** navigating the complexities of modified GDPR
- **Cybersecurity consultants** familiar with simplified NIS2

### Litigation and Regulatory Defense

The reforms will generate:

- **Legal uncertainty** during the transition period
- **Potential litigation** over interpretation of new rules
- **Defense in sanction proceedings** under modified frameworks

## Risks and Challenges

### Temporary Uncertainty

Until the final version is agreed, companies face:

- **Confusion about current vs. future requirements**
- **Decision to wait or act** under current rules
- **Risk of double investment** in systems that later change

### Multi-Level Compliance

Companies must manage:

- **Current rules** while reforms are implemented
- **Transitions between legal** frameworks
- **Temporal differences** in implementation by country

### Protection of Fundamental Rights

Organizations like Amnesty International have expressed concern that simplification could:

- **Dilute protections** of fundamental rights
- **Prioritize competitiveness** over privacy
- **Create regulatory loopholes** that corporations exploit

## Next Steps and Timeline

### Estimated Calendar

**Q2 2026**: Trilogue negotiations (Council, Parliament, Commission)  
**Q3 2026**: Final agreement on reformed AI Act text  
**Q4 2026**: Publication and entry into force  
**2027**: Gradual implementation according to extended deadlines

### Immediate Actions for Law Firms

1. **Monitor negotiations**: Follow trilogue progress
2. **Identify affected clients**: Map clients using AI or processing data
3. **Prepare impact analysis**: Assess potential impact per client
4. **Develop templates**: Create advisory documents for when reforms are approved

## Conclusion

The **Digital Omnibus Package** represents one of the most significant changes in the European digital regulatory landscape since GDPR implementation. For law firms, it's not just a business opportunity, but a responsibility to help clients navigate this transition period.

Regulatory simplification can bring real benefits in terms of competitiveness and efficiency, but also poses risks to data protection and fundamental rights. Law firms must strike a balance between helping clients leverage new flexibilities and defending the protection standards Europe has built over decades.
