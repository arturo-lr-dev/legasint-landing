## Introduction

Spring 2026 marks a critical inflection point in the European Union's cybersecurity regulation. Following the **European Commission breach** in March 2026, where the ShinyHunters group exfiltrated over 350GB of data from AWS accounts, the EU has accelerated its regulatory approach with the simultaneous implementation of four main regulatory frameworks: **NIS2**, **DORA**, **CRA** and the proposed **Cybersecurity Act 2.0**.

For law firms advising regulated companies, this represents both a challenge and a significant business opportunity.

## 1. The Threat Context That Drove the Changes

### The European Commission Breach (March 2026)

On March 24, 2026, attackers accessed the European Commission's AWS cloud accounts and exfiltrated over 350GB of data before being blocked. The ShinyHunters extortion group claimed responsibility. The Commission confirmed the breach on March 30, making it the most significant EU institutional compromise of the year.

**Data compromised:**
- Mail server dumps
- Databases
- Confidential documents
- Contracts

This incident exposed a structural vulnerability: public-facing cloud infrastructure operated without the access controls and credential hygiene that NIS2 and DORA are designed to mandate.

### First EU Cyber Sanctions of 2026

On March 16, 2026, the EU Council imposed restrictive measures against three entities —Integrity Technology Group, Anxun Information Technology (China), and Emennet Pasargad (Iran)— and two individuals for cyber attacks against European institutions.

These are not background events —they are the enforcement context every IT leader and compliance officer needs to understand now.

## 2. NIS2: January 2026 Amendments

### Key Proposed Changes

On January 20, 2026, the European Commission introduced changes that clarify:

- **Jurisdiction:** Improved coordination between national authorities
- **Scope:** Expansion of covered sectors (now 18 sectors)
- **Certification obligations:** New requirements for digital service providers

### Who is Affected

NIS2 significantly expands the scope compared to the original NIS1 Directive. As a general rule, it applies to medium-sized and larger entities in 18 sectors:

- Energy
- Food
- Digital infrastructure
- Chemicals
- Space
- Machinery
- And others...

### Implementation Status

Member States were required to transpose NIS2 by 2024, but several missed this deadline. In Germany, the NIS2 implementation law was published on 5 December 2025. Covered entities in Germany were required to register by 6 March 2026; currently, only around one third of entities subject to registration have done so.

**Risks for law firms:**
- Clients who haven't yet registered
- Uncertainty about specific technical obligations
- Lack of alignment between national frameworks

## 3. DORA: Active Compliance Since January 2025

### Current Status

Fully applicable since January 17, 2025, with BaFin and other national regulators conducting audits throughout 2026.

### Key Obligations for Financial Entities

| Area | Requirement |
|------|-------------|
| **Risk management** | Cyber risk framework approved by board |
| **Incidents** | Notification within 24 hours |
| **Testing** | TLPT and annual resilience testing |
| **Third parties** | Digital third-party risk management |

### Opportunities for Law Firms

Law firms can assist with:
- Risk management policy review
- Audit preparation for BaFin (or other regulators)
- Incident management and notification
- Digital vendor assessment (ICT third-party risk)

## 4. Cyber Resilience Act (CRA): Upcoming Deadlines

### The CRA's Objective

The Cyber Resilience Act targets the cybersecurity of products with digital elements. Unlike NIS2 —which focuses on network and information systems of entities— the CRA's objective is to ensure cybersecurity of certain hardware and software products themselves.

### Who is Affected

Manufacturers of software or hardware products with digital elements, particularly products connected to the internet, but also standalone software.

### Critical Timelines

- **Entry into force:** 10 December 2024
- **Main obligations:** From 11 December 2027
- **Reporting obligations:** From 11 September 2026

### Reporting Requirements Starting September 2026

Starting September 11, 2026, manufacturers must report:
- **Actively exploited vulnerabilities:** Within 24 hours
- **Cybersecurity incidents:** Classification and impact analysis
- **Product recalls:** When necessary due to cybersecurity risk

## 5. Cybersecurity Act 2.0: January 2026 Proposal

### Proposed Changes

In January 2026, new plans were presented under the "Cybersecurity Act 2.0" label. These focus on:

1. **Adjusting existing rules**, including the NIS2 Directive
2. **Updating European cybersecurity certification frameworks** to improve technical implementation
3. **Changing the role of ENISA**, in particular regarding incident reporting

### Impact on Law Firms

Law firms should be prepared to:
- Advise on changes in product certification
- Guide clients through regulatory transitions
- Interpret new ENISA jurisprudence

## 6. Critical Entities Resilience Directive (CER)

### Complement to NIS2

The CER Directive creates a framework to strengthen the resilience of essential service providers against a range of threats. It complements NIS2 by focusing on **physical resilience** to risks such as natural disasters, terrorism and sabotage.

The underlying recognition: cybersecurity and physical security are deeply interconnected.

### Covered Sectors

CER covers essential service providers in eight sectors:
- Energy
- Water
- Transport
- Finance
- Health
- Food
- Waste management
- IT/telecommunications

### Implementation Timelines

- **Entry into force:** 16 January 2023
- **Transposition:** 17 October 2024 (several countries missed deadline)
- **Germany:** Implementation law (KRITIS-Dach-Gesetz) entered force on 17 March 2026
- **Registration:** First registration obligations for affected entities by 17 July 2026

## 7. Regulatory Convergence: Four Simultaneous Frameworks

### The Overlap Challenge

Spring 2026 marks a convergence: the January 20 NIS2 amendments and CSA2 proposal, active DORA enforcement by national regulators, and the September 11 CRA reporting deadline approaching fast.

**For businesses**, the challenge isn't just understanding one regulation. It's navigating an entire ecosystem of overlapping requirements.

### Overlap Example

A financial sector company must simultaneously comply with:

| Regulation | Main Focus | Key Requirement |
|-------------|------------|------------------|
| **NIS2** | Information system resilience | Technical and organizational measures |
| **DORA** | Financial operational resilience | TLPT + annual testing |
| **CRA** | Product security | Vulnerability reporting from Sept 2026 |
| **CER** | Physical resilience | Physical continuity plans |
