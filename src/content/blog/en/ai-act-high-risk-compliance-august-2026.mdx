## Introduction

Exactly two months from **August 2, 2026**, thousands of European companies still have not completed their preparation for AI Act compliance on high-risk AI systems. From that date, AI systems classified as "high-risk" must meet strict technical and organizational requirements under penalties of up to **7% of global annual turnover**.

For law firms, this deadline represents a historic opportunity: clients need urgent advice to understand if they are affected, what they must do, and how to document it before it is too late.

## 1. What is a high-risk AI system?

**Annex III** of the AI Act lists the high-risk categories. The most relevant for the legal and business sectors include:

- **Biometric systems:** Remote identification, categorization of persons, emotion recognition.
- **Critical infrastructure management:** Systems in transport, water, gas, electricity.
- **Education and training:** Assessment, admission, plagiarism detection systems.
- **Employment:** Recruitment, performance evaluation, promotion.
- **Access to essential services:** Credit scoring, risk assessment, insurance.
- **Administration of justice:** Systems influencing individual judicial decisions.
- **Migration and asylum:** Visa or asylum application assessment.

### Is your client affected?

Key questions to determine scope:
1. Does it use AI to make decisions affecting individuals in any of these areas?
2. Is it an AI software provider selling to regulated sectors?
3. Is it an employer using AI in HR processes?

If the answer to any of these questions is yes, the August 2 deadline applies directly.

## 2. Obligations for providers (developers)

**Providers** of high-risk systems — those who develop or market the AI — must comply before August 2 with the following requirements:

### Risk management system

- Identify and analyze known and reasonably foreseeable risks.
- Estimate and evaluate risks that may emerge during intended use.
- Evaluate emerging risks based on post-implementation data.
- Adopt appropriate mitigation measures and document them.

### Data quality and datasets

- Ensure training, validation, and testing datasets are relevant, representative, and free from systematic errors.
- Document data limitations, potential biases, and preprocessing decisions.
- Respect copyright and data protection laws during collection.

### Transparency and documentation

- Draft clear, complete instructions for use tailored to the recipient.
- Prepare the **EU Declaration of Conformity**.
- Prepare the conformity assessment (self-assessment or with a notified body).
- Register the system in the **EUAIA database** before market placement.

### Human oversight

- Design systems to be supervised by naturally competent persons.
- Do not replace human oversight with a second AI or automated system.
- Document who supervises, how, and with what capacity for intervention.

### Accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity

- Ensure the system is accurate, robust, and secure under real conditions of use.
- Implement measures against errors, failures, inconsistencies, and adversarial attacks.
- Prevent cybersecurity vulnerabilities that could compromise functioning.

## 3. Obligations for deployers (enterprise users)

**Deployers** — companies that use high-risk AI systems — have lighter but equally strict obligations:

### Fundamental rights impact assessment

- Conduct an impact assessment before putting the system into service.
- Review it if conditions of use, data volume, or geographic scope change.
- Consider effects on privacy, non-discrimination, freedom of expression, and other rights.

### Transparency towards data subjects

- Inform individuals that they are interacting with an AI system (Art. 50).
- Inform when AI is used for decisions that significantly affect them.
- Provide clear information on logic, significance, and envisaged consequences.

### Operational monitoring

- Monitor system operation continuously.
- Retain automatic logs for the necessary period (minimum according to sector).
- Report serious incidents to the provider and, where appropriate, to national authorities.

### Data subject rights

- Facilitate exercise of access, rectification, and erasure rights.
- Allow individuals to submit opinions, challenge decisions, and obtain meaningful explanations.
- Do not obstruct the exercise of these rights through interface designs or bureaucratic processes.

## 4. Practical guide for law firms: the next 60 days

### Weeks 1-2: Impact diagnosis

- [ ] Complete inventory of all AI systems in use, testing, or development.
- [ ] Classification by risk level: prohibited, high-risk, limited, minimal.
- [ ] Role identification: are we providers, deployers, distributors, or importers?
- [ ] Determination of specific deadlines according to role and system type.

### Weeks 3-4: Technical and legal assessment

- [ ] Review of training datasets: quality, representativeness, bias, licenses.
- [ ] Technical audit of accuracy, robustness, and error resistance.
- [ ] Cybersecurity assessment of the system and its deployment environment.
- [ ] Contractual review with AI providers: who assumes responsibility as provider?

### Weeks 5-6: Regulatory documentation

- [ ] Drafting of technical and user instructions for use.
- [ ] Preparation of the EU Declaration of Conformity.
- [ ] Conformity assessment (internal or with a notified body, if applicable).
- [ ] Internal human oversight policy: roles, training, escalation procedures.

### Weeks 7-8: Registration, training, and go-live

- [ ] Registration of the system in the EUAIA database.
- [ ] Training of the human oversight team and end users.
- [ ] Establishment of incident notification channels and responsible persons.
- [ ] Final testing, log validation, and documented launch.

## 5. Penalties and non-compliance risks

The AI Act establishes a progressive and severe penalty system designed to be deterrent:

| Violation | Maximum penalty |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Use of prohibited AI (Art. 5) | 35 million € or 7% of global annual turnover |
| Non-compliance with high-risk obligations | 15 million € or 3% of global annual turnover |
| Administrative infringements (information, etc.) | 7.5 million € or 1% of global annual turnover |

**Additional risk:** Non-compliance can generate civil liability, irreversible reputational damage, loss of sectoral licenses, and collective actions by affected consumers or employees.
