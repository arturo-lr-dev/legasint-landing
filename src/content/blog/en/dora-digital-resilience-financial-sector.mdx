## Introduction

Regulation (EU) 2022/2554, known as **DORA (Digital Operational Resilience Act)**, has been fully applicable since **January 17, 2025** and represents a milestone in the regulation of digital operational resilience in the European financial sector.

This regulation affects more than twenty categories of financial entities and establishes strict requirements to prevent, withstand, respond to, and recover from incidents related to information and communication technologies (ICT).

For **law firms** advising the financial sector, understanding DORA is essential: many clients will need legal support to comply with these new obligations.

## What is the DORA Regulation?

DORA is the most comprehensive European regulatory framework for ICT risk management in the financial sector. Its main objectives are:

- **Harmonize** digital resilience rules across the EU
- **Strengthen** response capabilities against cyberattacks
- **Supervise** critical ICT service providers
- **Establish** incident reporting standards

### Differences from other regulations

| Aspect | DORA | NIS2 | GDPR |
|--------|------|------|------|
| **Sector** | Financial | Essential sectors | All |
| **Focus** | ICT resilience | Network security | Personal data |
| **Supervisor** | EBA/ESMA/EIOPA | National agencies | DPAs |
| **Application** | January 2025 | October 2024 | May 2018 |

## Who must comply with DORA?

The scope of application of the Regulation (Article 2) includes more than **twenty categories** of financial entities:

### Obligated entities

- ✅ Credit institutions (banks)
- ✅ Investment firms
- ✅ Payment and electronic money institutions
- ✅ Insurance and reinsurance companies
- ✅ Fund management companies
- ✅ Market infrastructures (exchanges, clearing houses)
- ✅ Crypto-asset service providers
- ✅ Financial data service providers

### Responsibility over ICT providers

Although DORA expressly mentions **external ICT service providers**, the primary responsibility for compliance lies with financial entities.

Article 28 and following establish that entities must:

```markdown
- [ ] Maintain a complete register of ICT agreements
- [ ] Identify providers supporting critical functions
- [ ] Include minimum clauses on security and auditing
- [ ] Manage data localization
- [ ] Control subcontracting
```

When a provider is designated as **"critical"**, it becomes subject to the supervision regime under Chapter V, coordinated by EBA, ESMA, or EIOPA.

## Five pillars of DORA compliance

### 1. ICT risk management

The ICT risk management framework must be **robust, coherent, and traceable**. Articles 5 to 14 establish minimum requirements:

**Mandatory elements:**
- Identification of essential assets
- Continuous risk assessments
- Updated security policies and controls
- Continuity and recovery plans
- Periodic resilience testing

### 2. ICT incident management

Articles 17 to 23 regulate the **management and reporting of major incidents**. Entities must:

- Classify incidents by severity
- Notify the corresponding financial supervisor
- Coordinate with GDPR when personal data is affected
- Document lessons learned

**⚠️ Important:** Data protection authorities have reiterated that personal data breaches must be reported within **72 hours**, requiring coordination between DORA and GDPR reporting procedures.

### 3. Operational resilience testing

Entities must conduct **periodic tests** including:

- Vulnerability assessments
- Source code analysis
- Performance testing
- Threat-led penetration testing (TLPT) for significant entities

### 4. Third-party ICT risk management

One of the most complex aspects of DORA is **ICT supply chain oversight**:

**Minimum contractual requirements:**
- Audit rights
- Clear service levels (SLA)
- Exit clauses
- Data localization in the EU (preferable)
- Subcontractor notification

### 5. Information sharing

DORA encourages **threat intelligence sharing** among financial entities on a voluntary but incentivized basis.

## Penalties for non-compliance

Article 50 of the Regulation requires Member States to establish **"effective, proportionate, and dissuasive"** sanctions.

### Sanctioning regime

| Authority | Sector | Type of sanction |
|-----------|--------|------------------|
| Central Bank | Credit institutions | Fines up to 10% turnover |
| Securities Commission | Capital markets | Activity restrictions |
| Insurance Regulator | Insurance | Director disqualification |

### Accumulation of sanctions

If the incident also constitutes a **personal data breach**, DORA sanctions may accumulate with those under GDPR, multiplying financial and reputational risk.

**Combined risk scenario:**
```
Major ICT incident + Personal data breach =
  DORA: Up to 10% annual turnover
  GDPR: Up to €20M or 4% global turnover
  Total potential: Combined sanctions + reputational damage
```

## DORA-GDPR intersection: Key points

For law firms advising on **data protection**, understanding how both frameworks relate is fundamental:

### Notification coordination

| Requirement | DORA | GDPR |
|-------------|------|------|
| Notification deadline | Based on severity | 72 hours |
| Destination authority | Financial supervisor | DPA |
| Minimum content | Technical + impact | Data affected + measures |

### Practical recommendation

Design a **unified notification procedure** that:
1. Classifies the incident (ICT, personal data, or both)
2. Activates corresponding notifications in parallel
3. Documents for both regulators
4. Coordinates external communication
