## Introduction

The **NIS2 Directive** (Directive (EU) 2022/2555) has completely transformed the cybersecurity landscape in Europe and, especially, in Spain. Although the October 2024 transposition deadline has passed, Spain is still working on the final approval of the Cybersecurity Coordination and Governance Law. This scenario creates a unique situation of regulatory uncertainty and, at the same time, **significant opportunities** for law firms advising the financial sector and other affected entities.

This article analyzes the current status of NIS2 implementation in Spain, the key obligations for financial entities, and how law firms can position their advisory services in this context.

## 1. Current Status of Transposition in Spain

### Deadlines Missed and Delays

The official NIS2 transposition deadline was **17 October 2024**. As of today (April 2026), Spain has not yet completed this process:

- **Status**: The Cybersecurity Coordination and Governance Law remains in draft phase
- **Publication**: Not yet published in the BOE (Boletín Oficial del Estado - Official State Gazette)
- **Effectiveness**: Once published, it will enter into force the following day
- **European pressure**: The European Commission has already initiated infringement proceedings against Spain

This situation creates a peculiar scenario: the Directive is binding at the European level, but the definitive national legal framework has not yet been approved. However, entities have both a **moral and strategic obligation** to prepare and assess their situation.

### The Cybersecurity Coordination and Governance Law

The Spanish draft transposes NIS2 with some local particularities:

- **Maintenance of ENS**: The National Security Scheme (ENS) is maintained as a technical reference
- **Specific Compliance Profile**: A specific profile is provided to demonstrate NIS2 compliance based on ENS
- **Strengthened governance**: Cybersecurity governance is reinforced
- **Board responsibility**: Emphasizes that cybersecurity must be a strategic matter

## 2. Key Innovations of NIS2

### Expansion of Sectors and Entities

Unlike its predecessor (NIS1), NIS2 **significantly expands** the number of sectors and affected entities:

**Traditional sectors (NIS1)**:
- Energy
- Transport
- Water
- Banking
- Health

**New sectors (NIS2)**:
- Public administration
- Digital infrastructures
- Postal and courier services
- Waste management
- Advanced manufacturing
- Food production and distribution

**Entity classification**:
- **Essential entities**: Larger size, significant impact, more requirements
- **Important entities**: Smaller size, but still subject to compliance

### Board Responsibility: Paradigm Shift

This is perhaps the **most important innovation** of NIS2:

**Explicit board responsibility**:
- Management bodies must approve cybersecurity measures
- Solidary liability in case of infringements
- Cultural change: cybersecurity is not just a technical issue, but strategic

**Specific board obligations**:
- Approve cybersecurity policy
- Ensure sufficient resources
- Supervise implementation of measures
- Participate in incident simulation exercises

## 3. Obligations for the Financial Sector

### Classification in the NIS2 Context

The financial sector is predominantly classified as an **essential entity** under NIS2, implying stricter requirements:

**Criteria for essential entity**:
- More than 250 employees OR
- Annual turnover exceeding 50 million euros OR
- Total balance sheet exceeding 43 million euros

Most banks, savings banks, insurance companies, and significant financial entities meet at least one of these criteria.

### Mandatory Cybersecurity Measures

NIS2 establishes a **harmonized framework** of minimum cybersecurity requirements:

1. **Cybersecurity risk management**
   - Comprehensive risk analysis
   - Risk-based measures
   - Regular assessments

2. **Corporate governance**
   - Appointment of a cybersecurity officer
   - Mandatory staff training
   - Board-approved cybersecurity policy

3. **Supply chain protection**
   - Security assessment of providers
   - Security clauses in contracts
   - Third-party auditing

4. **Business continuity**
   - Updated continuity plan
   - Regular backups
   - Recovery procedures

5. **Incident notification**
   - Very strict deadlines (24 hours for initial notification)
   - Detailed report within 72 hours
   - Public notification when appropriate

### Reinforced Supervision and Sanctions

**New features of the sanctioning regime**:
- Fines of up to 10 million euros or 2% of global annual turnover (whichever is higher)
- Stricter and more frequent supervision
- Solidary liability of the board
- Possible license revocation in serious cases

## 5. Practical Guide for Financial Sector Clients

### Step 1: Initial Assessment

**Immediate actions**:
- [ ] Identify if the entity is classified as "essential" or "important"
- [ ] Appoint a cybersecurity officer if one does not exist
- [ ] Assess current compliance status with ENS
- [ ] Conduct a gap analysis against NIS2

### Step 2: Governance Adaptation

**Necessary changes**:
- Ensure the board formally approves cybersecurity measures
- Incorporate cybersecurity into the board of directors' agenda
- Establish regular supervision procedures

### Step 3: Technical Implementation

**Priority measures**:
- Review and update cybersecurity policies
- Establish risk management processes
- Implement supply chain controls
- Prepare incident notification procedures

### Step 4: Documentation and Evidence

**Required documentation**:
- Approved cybersecurity policy
- Documented risk assessments
- Staff training records
- Evidence of implemented controls
- Business continuity plan

## 6. Special Situation: Regulatory Uncertainty

### Strategy in the Absence of Definitive Law

**Recommended approach**:
1. **Don't wait for the law**: Start preparation now to avoid last-minute scrambling
2. **Align directly with NIS2**: Use the Directive text as a guide
3. **Prepare for ENS**: Since the draft maintains it as a reference
4. **Document decisions**: Keep evidence of measures adopted

**Advantages of proactive action**:
- Demonstrate diligence to supervisors
- Avoid non-compliance fines
- Gain competitive advantage
- Prepare for other frameworks (DORA, etc.)

## 7. Integration with Other Regulatory Frameworks

### Relationship with the National Security Scheme (ENS)

The ENS will continue to be **the technical reference** in Spain:
- The Spanish draft maintains the ENS
- A "Specific Compliance Profile" for NIS2 is foreseen
- Entities already aligned with ENS are not starting from zero

### Coordination with DORA

The **DORA Regulation** (Digital Operational Resilience Act) is specific to the financial sector:
- Both are complementary
- DORA focuses on digital operational resilience
- NIS2 establishes broader cybersecurity requirements
- Law firms can help integrate both requirements

## Conclusion

The implementation of NIS2 in Spain is in a complex situation: delays in transposition, but clear obligations at the European level. For law firms, this represents a **significant opportunity** to offer high-value advisory services, especially to the financial sector.

The key to success will be:
- **Acting proactively**: Don't wait for the definitive law
- **Focus on governance**: Board responsibility is the most important change
- **Practical approach**: Help clients implement measures, not just understand the regulation
- **Framework integration**: Coordinate NIS2 with ENS, DORA, and other requirements
