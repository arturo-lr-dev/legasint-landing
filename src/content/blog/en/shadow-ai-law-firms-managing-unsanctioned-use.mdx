## Introduction

In 2026, the adoption of artificial intelligence tools in the legal sector has shifted from being an exception to becoming the norm. However, a significant portion of this adoption occurs **outside the control of law firms**: employees using ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or specialized tools without formal authorization, without compliance review, and frequently **without understanding the implications for confidentiality and data protection**.

This phenomenon, known as **Shadow AI**, represents one of the most serious operational risks facing law firms today. Unlike traditional software, which requires IT installation and approval, generative AI is accessible from any browser, eliminating the technical barriers that historically contained Shadow IT.

## 1. What Is Shadow AI?

Shadow AI is defined as the use of artificial intelligence tools by employees **without the approval, supervision, or knowledge** of the IT department or law firm management.

### Typical Scenarios in Law Firms

| Situation | Tool Used | Primary Risk |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| Drafting pleadings and complaints | ChatGPT, Claude | Leakage of confidential client data |
| Contract summary and analysis | Gemini, Copilot | Data storage on third-party servers |
| Legal document translation | DeepL, Google Translate | Data processing outside the EU |
| Case law research | Perplexity, Consensus | Training of models with sensitive data |
| Email correction and enhancement | Grammarly, Notion AI | Recording of privileged professional communications |

The fundamental difference between Shadow AI and authorized use lies in the **absence of governance**: there is no Data Processing Agreement (DPA), no risk assessment, no traceability of what information has left the firm, and no guarantee that data is not used to train third-party models.

## 2. Risks Specific to Law Firms

### 2.1. Violation of Attorney-Client Privilege

The Spanish Bar Association's Code of Conduct and professional college regulations establish the **duty of secrecy** regarding all information obtained in the exercise of the profession. When a lawyer enters the facts of a case, litigation strategy, or identifiable client data into a public chatbot, they are disclosing confidential information to a technological third party over which the firm has no contractual control.

This constitutes a **serious violation of professional secrecy**, subject to disciplinary sanctions by the bar association and potentially generating civil liability to the client.

### 2.2. GDPR Non-Compliance

The General Data Protection Regulation imposes strict obligations on the processing of personal data:

- **Lawfulness of processing** (Art. 6): sending personal data to an AI provider without a valid legal basis is illegal.
- **Processors** (Art. 28): requires a contract with specific guarantees. Most Shadow AI tools lack a signed DPA.
- **Data minimization** (Art. 5.1.c): employees typically share complete documents rather than anonymized fragments.
- **Security of processing** (Art. 32): sending data to uncontrolled servers, often outside the EEA, constitutes a security breach.

A personal data leak resulting from Shadow AI use can trigger the **obligation to notify the supervisory authority** within 72 hours and communicate with affected data subjects.

### 2.3. Hallucinations and Professional Liability

Generative AI tools can produce **incorrect, invented, or legally unsustainable information**. If an employee uses an AI response without expert supervision to prepare a pleading, advise a client, or draft a contractual clause, the firm may incur **professional liability** for negligence or malpractice.

Documented cases of judicial hallucinations — where AI cites case law or doctrine that does not exist or is misquoted — are especially dangerous in litigation where the firm's reputation and the client's interests are at stake.

### 2.4. Loss of Intellectual Property and Know-How

When an employee uses AI to draft innovative contractual clauses, original litigation strategies, or specialized legal analyses, the question arises of **who holds the rights** to those outputs. Some AI providers reserve rights over input data or derived models, which may compromise the firm's competitive know-how.

## 3. How to Detect Unauthorized AI Use

### 3.1. Technical Indicators

The IT department or the firm's technology partner can implement technical controls that do not invade workplace privacy but do identify risks:

- **DNS and TLS traffic inspection**: identification of frequent connections to OpenAI, Anthropic, Google AI APIs, or unapproved AI services.
- **Proxy log analysis**: recurrent visits to chat.openai.com, claude.ai, perplexity.ai during working hours.
- **DLP (Data Loss Prevention) tools**: alerts when data with patterns of ID numbers, case numbers, sensitive legal terms, or client names leave the corporate network towards unauthorized domains.
- **Document metadata review**: some word processors and collaborative platforms insert metadata that may indicate the use of AI assistants.

### 3.2. Organizational and Quality Indicators

- Employees producing unusually high-quality documents in record time without explanation.
- Inconsistencies in writing style between different documents from the same lawyer.
- References to case law, doctrine, or regulations that do not exist or are misquoted (AI hallucinations).
- Reluctance to share the drafting process or sources consulted for a document.
- Unexpected legal conceptual errors in work from experienced lawyers.

## 4. Governance Framework: From Prohibition to Smart Management

### 4.1. Total Prohibition Is Counterproductive

Many law firms react to Shadow AI with **absolute prohibition policies**. This strategy is ineffective: employees continue to use AI but hide it even more, increasing the risk because the use becomes completely opaque and there are no channels to report problems.

The solution is a **governance model based on three risk levels**:

| Level | Permitted Tools | Supervision Required | Permitted Data |
|-------|----------------|---------------------|----------------|
| **Level 1: Free use** | Local tools, offline AI, general search engines | Low | Public data, internal training, generic templates |
| **Level 2: Controlled use** | Enterprise cloud tools with signed DPA | Medium: review by supervisor | Anonymized data, non-identified cases, reference documents |
| **Level 3: Restricted use** | On-premise AI or deployed in firm's private cloud | High: partner review before submission | Real client data, litigation strategies, confidential information |

### 4.2. Mandatory AI Use Policy

Every law firm should have an AI use policy approved by the governing body, including at minimum:

1. **Catalog of approved tools**: whitelist of providers with validated DPA, security certification, and guarantee of no data use for training.
2. **New tool request procedure**: documented process for employees to request evaluation of new AI tools.
3. **Data classification**: matrix defining what type of information can be processed with each tool level.
4. **Human review obligation**: every AI output used for client work must be reviewed, verified, and approved by a lawyer.
5. **Mandatory and periodic training**: awareness courses on Shadow AI risks, hallucinations, and regulatory compliance.
6. **Sanctions for non-compliance**: clear consequences for unauthorized use of unapproved tools with sensitive data.

### 4.3. Technical and Legal Provider Evaluation

Before approving any AI tool for use in the firm, the compliance team must verify:

- ✅ **GDPR-compliant DPA**: standard contractual clauses if the provider is outside the EU, or Adequacy Decisions.
- ✅ **No-training guarantee**: contractual certification that input data is not used for fine-tuning or model improvement.
- ✅ **Server location**: preferably EU or European Economic Area; if not, valid international transfer guarantees.
- ✅ **Security certifications**: ISO 27001, SOC 2 Type II, or equivalent recognized standards.
- ✅ **Right to audit**: ability to inspect security processes and data treatment practices.
- ✅ **AI Act compliance**: if the tool is classified as a high-risk AI system, verification of conformity.

## 5. Practical Implementation Guide

### Step 1: Inventory and Risk Assessment

```markdown
- [ ] Conduct anonymous employee survey on current AI tool usage
- [ ] Analyze network traffic to identify connections to unauthorized AI services
- [ ] Inventory all AI tools already formally contracted by the firm
- [ ] Assess risks for each identified tool: data processed, provider, location
- [ ] Document real use cases and employee motivations
```

### Step 2: Governance Policy Design

```markdown
- [ ] Draft AI use policy with input from legal, IT, and compliance departments
- [ ] Define the three usage levels and catalog of approved tools per level
- [ ] Approve policy by governing body or partnership board
- [ ] Communicate policy to all employees with presentation session
- [ ] Obtain individual signed acknowledgment from each employee
```

### Step 3: Technical Implementation and Controls

```markdown
- [ ] Configure DLP to alert when sensitive data leaves towards unauthorized domains
- [ ] Block, where technically feasible, access to unapproved tools from corporate network
- [ ] Deploy approved Level 2 and 3 tools with SSO authentication and usage logging
- [ ] Configure internal APIs or gateways for controlled use of AI models
- [ ] Establish secure channels for employees to request new tools
```

### Step 4: Training, Culture, and Continuous Improvement

```markdown
- [ ] Organize training sessions on Shadow AI, risks, and internal policy
- [ ] Conduct prompt engineering workshops for approved tools
- [ ] Periodically communicate success stories, avoided errors, and lessons learned
- [ ] Designate an "AI champion" or AI responsible in each department
- [ ] Review policy and tool catalog every 6 months
```

## 6. Comprehensive Compliance Checklist

### Before Implementation

- [ ] Complete Shadow AI inventory conducted in the firm
- [ ] Formal AI use policy approved by governing body
- [ ] All catalog providers evaluated and approved legally and technically
- [ ] GDPR-compliant DPAs signed with each approved provider
- [ ] Team trained on risks, policy, and authorized tools

### During Operation

- [ ] AI tool usage logs activated and reviewed quarterly
- [ ] DLP system configured and tested for sensitive data
- [ ] Human review documented for all AI outputs used for clients
- [ ] Internal compliance audit conducted semi-annually
- [ ] Incident reporting channel operational and known to all employees

### Incident Response

- [ ] Defined action protocol if unauthorized use with sensitive data is detected
- [ ] Notification procedure to supervisory authority if personal data leak is confirmed
- [ ] Impact assessment on client confidentiality and attorney-client privilege
- [ ] Incident documentation for firm's professional liability insurance
- [ ] Corrective measures implemented and policy review if necessary

## Conclusion

Shadow AI is not merely a technological problem: it is a **governance, culture, and leadership problem**. Law firms that attempt to completely prohibit AI use fail because the barrier to entry is too low; those that ignore the phenomenon expose themselves to GDPR sanctions, violations of professional secrecy, civil liability claims, and irreparable reputational damage.

The winning strategy consists of **acknowledging reality**, **educating the team** about real risks, **approving secure tools** that meet compliance standards, and **supervising use** with a balanced combination of technical, organizational, and cultural measures.

In an increasingly competitive legal market, the ability to leverage AI safely and compliantly is becoming a **differentiating competitive advantage**. Firms that master AI governance will deliver better service, faster, and with less risk.
