## Introduction

The **European Union's Artificial Intelligence Regulation (AI Act)** has evolved from a draft legal text into an operational reality that is transforming how law firms work. In March 2026, with much of its provisions already in force, it's time to analyze its real impact on legal practice.

As a consultancy specialized in digital transformation of the legal sector, at **Legasint** we've witnessed firsthand how this regulation is reconfiguring not only the use of AI tools, but also compliance strategies and the delivery of legal services.

## The AI Act: Essential Recap

The AI Act establishes a **risk-based regulatory framework** for artificial intelligence systems in the EU. Systems are classified into four categories:

1. **Unacceptable risk**: Prohibited (e.g., social scoring systems)
2. **High risk**: Require conformity assessment and continuous monitoring
3. **Limited risk**: Transparency obligations
4. **Minimal risk**: Free use, no specific obligations

For law firms, the key lies in correctly identifying which category their AI tools fall into.

## Impact on Law Firm Operations

### 1. Mandatory Technology Due Diligence

**Before the AI Act**, many firms adopted AI tools without exhaustive analysis of their implications. **Now**, each tool must be evaluated:

```
AI Act evaluation checklist:
✓ What risk category does the system fall into?
✓ Does it meet transparency requirements?
✓ What data does it process and under what legal basis?
✓ Is there adequate human oversight?
✓ Does the provider comply with the AI Act?
✓ Do we have complete system documentation?
```

Firms are creating internal **"AI Systems Registers"**, documenting each tool used, its purpose, risk level, and implemented control measures.

### 2. High-Risk Systems in Legal Practice

Some AI uses in the legal field may be classified as **high risk**, especially when they:

- Are used to assess the admissibility of evidence
- Assist in judicial decision-making
- Process sensitive personal data at scale
- Are employed in predictive analysis affecting fundamental rights

**Practical case**: A firm using AI to automatically analyze contracts in M&A due diligence processes must:

1. Document the system's functioning
2. Implement qualified human oversight
3. Maintain logs of all AI-suggested decisions
4. Ensure explainability of recommendations
5. Allow human review of all critical conclusions

### 3. Transparency with Clients

The AI Act imposes **transparency obligations** that firms must communicate to their clients:

> "When a law firm uses AI systems in providing legal services, it must inform the client in a clear and understandable manner about the use of these tools, their capabilities and limitations."

This has led many firms to:

- Update their service agreements
- Create specific clauses about AI use
- Develop AI policies accessible to clients
- Implement informed consent where necessary

### 4. New Business Lines: AI Act Advisory

Paradoxically, the AI Act has created a **new practice area** for law firms. Companies developing or deploying AI systems need specialized advice on:

- Risk classification of their systems
- Conformity assessments
- Technical and legal documentation
- Ongoing compliance strategies
- Incident management and notifications

Firms with experience in technology law, data protection, and compliance are leading this new specialization.

## Practical Challenges We're Seeing

### Challenge 1: Interpretation of the Regulation

The AI Act is a complex text with numerous **indeterminate legal concepts**. When exactly does a contract analysis system become "high risk"? National authorities are issuing guidelines, but uncertainty persists.

### Challenge 2: AI Supply Chain

Many firms use SaaS (Software as a Service) tools for document management, legal research, or data analysis. The AI Act makes the **"deployer"** of the system responsible, which means firms must:

- Verify provider compliance
- Obtain contractual guarantees
- Maintain supply chain documentation
- Monitor changes in tools used

### Challenge 3: Resources and Knowledge

Most firms, especially small and medium-sized ones, **lack technical expertise** to conduct deep evaluations of AI systems. This is generating:

- Demand for specialized consultancies (like Legasint)
- Collaboration between firms to share resources
- Intensive training of professionals in AI and law
- Creation of hybrid roles: "Legal Tech Compliance Officer"

## Opportunities Offered by the AI Act

Despite the challenges, the AI Act also presents **opportunities**:

### 1. Competitive Differentiation

Firms that implement AI Act compliance proactively and transparently can differentiate themselves with clients who value responsible innovation.

### 2. Internal Process Improvement

The obligation to document and monitor AI systems is forcing many firms to **digitize and optimize** their work processes, with benefits beyond mere compliance.

### 3. Positioning as Experts

Firms that develop AI Act expertise position themselves as leaders in one of the most important regulatory areas of this decade.

## Practical Recommendations for Law Firms

Based on our experience advising firms on their AI Act adaptation, we recommend:

1. **Tools audit**: Conduct a complete inventory of all digital tools incorporating AI
2. **Risk assessment**: Classify each system according to the AI Act framework
3. **Internal policies**: Develop a clear and binding AI use policy
4. **Training**: Educate the team on AI Act fundamentals and their practical implications
5. **Continuous monitoring**: Implement processes for monitoring tools used
6. **Contractual updates**: Review and update contracts with clients and suppliers
7. **External collaboration**: Consider support from specialized consultancies for complex technical evaluations
