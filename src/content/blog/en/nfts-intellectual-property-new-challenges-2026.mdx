## Introduction

The rise of **NFTs (Non-Fungible Tokens)** has transformed the digital art market, but also created a complex legal labyrinth regarding **intellectual property** and **copyright**. In 2026, law firms advising artists, creators, and digital companies face new challenges that require deep understanding of both traditional law and blockchain technology.

This article analyzes the main legal challenges, the current regulatory framework in Spain and the EU, and how law firms can effectively advise their clients in this constantly evolving ecosystem.

## 1. What is an NFT and What is NOT

### Technical Definition

An **NFT** is a unique, non-replicable digital asset registered on a **blockchain** (typically Ethereum). Its uniqueness is guaranteed by a cryptographic identifier that makes it non-fungible: it cannot be exchanged for another identical one.

### The Big Misconception: NFT ≠ Intellectual Property

**Common error:** Many NFT buyers believe that purchasing the token gives them ownership of the associated digital content (the artwork, audio, video).

**Legal reality:** Buying an NFT **does NOT automatically transfer** copyright or exploitation rights of the digital content. What is acquired is a certificate of authenticity and possession of the token, but the underlying content remains subject to applicable intellectual property laws.

> **Case study:** In 2021, a digital artist sued a marketplace for selling NFTs of her work without authorization. The court determined that the NFT sale did not imply transfer of copyright.

## 2. Legal Framework in Spain and the EU

### Intellectual Property: Moral Rights vs. Economic Rights

Spain's **Royal Legislative Decree 1/1996** on Intellectual Property establishes two types of rights:

#### Moral Rights (Inalienable)
- **Right of attribution:** Recognition as author
- **Right of integrity:** Oppose modifications that damage the work
- **Right of disclosure:** Decide if and how the work is made public
- **Right of withdrawal:** Withdraw the work from the market (prior compensation)

#### Economic Rights (Transferable)
- **Reproduction right:** Fix the work in any medium
- **Distribution right:** Make the work available to the public
- **Communication right:** Make the work accessible to the public by any means
- **Transformation right:** Translate, adapt, modify the work

### Exploitation Rights and Smart Contracts

A **smart contract** associated with an NFT can define license terms, but **CANNOT derogate** from inalienable legal rights:

```markdown
✅ CAN define:
- Percentage of resale royalties
- Scope of usage license
- Specific commercial restrictions

❌ CANNOT eliminate:
- Author's moral rights
- Distribution rights (exhaustion of right on first sale)
- Minimum legal protections established by law
```

### Directive on Copyright and the Digital Single Market

The **Directive (EU) 2019/790** introduces changes relevant to NFTs:

- **Text and data mining exception:** Allows using protected works for AI training (with exceptions)
- **New exceptions for educational and research purposes**
- **Obligations for content-sharing platforms:** Expanded liability

### MiCA (Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation)

The **MiCA Regulation (EU) 2023/1114**, fully applicable since late 2024, classifies NFTs:

- **Exempt from MiCA:** NFTs that are "genuinely unique and non-fungible"
- **Subject to MiCA:** NFT collections with similar characteristics (treated as crypto-assets)

**Implications for law firms:**
- NFT marketplaces operating as "crypto-assets" require registration as crypto-asset service providers (CASP)
- KYC/AML, governance, and information disclosure obligations

## 3. Intellectual Property Challenges with NFTs

### 1. Copyright Infringement in Minting

The most common problem: **"right-click minting"** - anyone can create an NFT of a protected work without the author's authorization.

**Defense strategies for authors:**
- **Work registration in SGAE, VEGAP or similar entities** for proof of authorship
- **Use of time-stamping on blockchain** as evidence of priority
- **Terms of service on marketplaces:** Infringement notices (DMCA-style)
- **Smart contracts with authorization lists:** Only whitelisted wallets can create official NFTs

### 2. The Problem of Digital Exhaustion

The **exhaustion principle** (Art. 18 LPI): when the copyright holder distributes a physical copy of their work in the market, their right to control its resale is exhausted.

**The digital challenge:** Does this apply to NFTs?

- **Court of Justice of the EU (UsedSoft vs Oracle case, 2012):** Exhaustion can apply to digital copies sold with perpetual license.
- **Current tension:** NFT resales do not necessarily transfer rights over the underlying work, creating a legal vacuum.

### 3. Licenses vs. Rights Assignments

Creators must be clear about what is being transferred:

| Type | What it grants | Duration | Recommended for |
|------|----------------|----------|-------------------|
| **NFT sale without license** | Only the token (no rights over work) | Permanent | Collectors, investors |
| **Personal use license** | Non-exclusive right to use/display | Permanent or limited | Digital art buyers |
| **Commercial license** | Right to commercially exploit the work | Perpetual or limited | Companies, brands |
| **Total rights assignment** | All rights (moral rights remain with author) | 70 years post-mortem + 5 years | Purchases by brands, studios |

### 4. Automated Royalties and Resale Rights

NFTs allow implementing **resale royalties** automatically via smart contract. In Europe:

- **Right of follow-up (Droit de Suite):** Art. 26 LPI grants authors of original artworks an inalienable right to 5% of resale price.
- **European harmonization:** Directive 2001/84/CE (applicable throughout the EU).

**The challenge:** How do automated NFT royalties interact with the traditional droit de suite?

**Court of Justice of the EU position (2025):** Royalties via smart contract can complement, but not replace, the legal droit de suite when dealing with original artworks.

## 4. Protection for Creators and Law Firms

### 1. Smart Contract Framework

Law firms can advise on drafting **terms of service** that integrate into the smart contract:

```markdown
{
  "license": {
    "type": "non-commercial personal use",
    "scope": "visualization and display",
    "prohibitions": [
      "modification of work",
      "distribution without separate license",
      "creation of derivatives without authorization"
    ],
    "royalties_resale": "5% automatic",
    "droit_de_suite_eu": "applicable per Art. 26 LPI"
  }
}
```

### 2. Work Protection Strategies

#### For Authors/Creators
1. **Preventive registration** in SGAE/VEGAP before minting
2. **Digital watermarking** visible on the work (as evidence)
3. **Inclusion of authorship metadata** in original files
4. **Clear usage terms** on the marketplace and smart contract
5. **Monitoring** infringements with blockchain scraping tools

#### For Buyers/Investors
1. **Verification of authorship** before purchase (direct contact with artist)
2. **Review of license terms** in the smart contract
3. **Verification of NFT history** on blockchain explorers
4. **Due diligence** on the provenance of the work

### 3. Litigation and Dispute Resolution

#### Remedies
- **IP infringement actions** before Commercial Courts
- **Infringement notices** to marketplaces (takedowns)
- **Civil lawsuits** for damages
- **Criminal complaints** when intellectual property crimes are involved

#### Competent Jurisdiction
- **Commercial Courts** for civil IP litigation
- **National Court** for international scope or criminal cases
- **Court of Justice of the EU** for interpretation of directives and regulations

## 5. Recommended Practices for Law Firms

### 1. Preventive Advice

Before a client creates or invests in NFTs:
- **Copyright review** of the work
- **Ownership analysis** of the content (freelancers, collaborators)
- **Identification of infringement risks** (use of Creative Commons licensed material, stock images, etc.)
- **Drafting contracts** with collaborators explicitly defining rights over NFTs

### 2. Contracts and Smart Contracts

Law firms can:
- **Draft terms of service** for marketplaces
- **Structure licenses** that integrate into smart contracts
- **Define scope of royalties** and resale rights
- **Establish dispute resolution clauses** (international arbitration vs. national courts)

### 3. Due Diligence in Transactions

For clients buying NFTs:
- **Provenance verification** (provenance research)
- **Analysis of ownership chains** on blockchain
- **Review of transaction history** to detect fraud
- **Evaluation of regulatory risks** (especially in crypto-asset projects that are actually NFT collections)

### 4. Training and Awareness

Educate clients about:
- **Difference between token and copyright** rights
- **Importance of work registration** before minting
- **Infringement risks** in unauthorized minting
- **Tax obligations** (VAT, income tax in Spain; crypto-asset reporting in the EU)

## 6. Trends and Future (2026-2028)

### 1. Interoperability and Cross-Chain

**Multi-chain NFTs** (existing on multiple blockchains simultaneously) raise questions:
- Which law applies? That of each jurisdiction where the blockchain operates?
- How are copyright rights enforced when a work exists on multiple chains?

### 2. Dynamic and Generative NFTs

NFTs that **evolve over time** or are generated with AI:
- Who is the author when an AI modifies the original work?
- How are intermediate versions protected?

### 3. DAOs and Co-creation

**Decentralized Autonomous Organizations** that create art collectively:
- How are copyright rights distributed among multiple anonymous creators?
- What role do smart contracts play in rights management?

### 4. Evolving Regulation

- **EU crypto-asset directive:** Next post-MiCA review (expected for 2027)
- **Digital droit de suite harmonization:** Ongoing discussions on NFT adaptation
- **Legal recognition of IP tokens:** Proposals for blockchain registration as complementary proof

## 7. Practical Checklist for Clients

### For Creators Wanting to Create NFTs

- [ ] **Register work** in SGAE/VEGAP before minting
- [ ] **Verify ownership** of all content (including design elements, audio, software)
- [ ] **Define license terms** that integrate into the smart contract
- [ ] **Include watermark** or watermark on visible work
- [ ] **Establish royalty percentage** for resales
- [ ] **Document authorship** with additional evidence (timestamped email, contracts with collaborators)
- [ ] **Consult with law firm** on IP and tax implications

### For Investors and Buyers

- [ ] **Verify authorship** with the original creator (not just the seller on marketplace)
- [ ] **Review license terms** in the smart contract
- [ ] **Check history** of NFT on blockchain explorers (Etherscan, PolygonScan)
- [ ] **Evaluate provenance** of the work (ownership chain)
- [ ] **Understand rights granted** (usage license vs. rights assignment)
- [ ] **Consider regulatory risks** (especially for NFT collections that might be considered crypto-assets by MiCA)
- [ ] **Consult with law firm** for high-value due diligence

### For Marketplaces and Platforms

- [ ] **Implement infringement notification system** (takedowns)
- [ ] **Require clear terms of service** that recognize authors' moral rights
- [ ] **Verify whitelists** for official creators
- [ ] **Comply with MiCA** if operating as crypto-asset provider
- [ ] **Establish KYC/AML policies** per European regulations
- [ ] **Maintain transaction records** for audits

## Conclusion

NFTs represent an unprecedented opportunity for digital creators and the art market, but also pose complex legal challenges that law firms must address with specialized knowledge.

**The key to success in 2026 lies in:**
1. **Contractual clarity:** Explicitly defining what rights are transferred (or not) with each NFT
2. **Prevention:** Work registration and due diligence before minting or investment
3. **Specialization:** Understanding both traditional intellectual property and blockchain technology
4. **Constant updates:** Following regulatory evolution in the EU and Spain

Law firms that develop expertise in this niche will be well-positioned to advise artists, creators, digital companies, and marketplaces in an ecosystem that will continue to grow and evolve.
