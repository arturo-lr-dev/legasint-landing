## Introduction

In 2026, the European Union is building something unprecedented in the legal world: the **European Legal Data Space (ELDS)**, an interoperable ecosystem that will make legal data from across the EU available to citizens, lawyers, judges, and businesses. Legislation, case law, procedural metadata, and judicial statistics will no longer be scattered across 27 national systems but will form a common data space that is accessible, reusable, and governed by European rules.

This project is not a technological utopia. It stems from the **Data Governance Act** (applicable since September 2023) and the **European Data Strategy** of 2020, and is now materializing with concrete infrastructure, funding, and technical architecture. For Spanish law firms, the ELDS represents both a competitive opportunity and an adaptation challenge.

## What is the European Legal Data Space?

The ELDS is one of the **Common European Data Spaces** designated by the European Commission, specifically focused on the domain of legislation and case law. Its goal is to provide legal data that is easily accessible, reusable, and interoperable across the EU.

### Data it will include

| Data type | Examples | Expected availability |
|-----------|----------|----------------------|
| Legislation | Directives, regulations, transposed national laws | 2025-2026 |
| Case law | CJEU rulings, national supreme court decisions | 2026-2027 |
| Procedural metadata | Litigation statistics, resolution times | 2026-2028 |
| Open legislative data | Bills, parliamentary debates | 2025-2026 |
| Legal resources | Legal doctrine, training, legal tech tools | 2027+ |

### Guiding principles

- **Technical and semantic interoperability**: structured data with common metadata
- **Non-discriminatory access**: same rules for large firms, startups, and citizens
- **Reusability**: licenses that allow the development of products and services
- **Privacy and data protection**: compatibility with GDPR and procedural regulations

## Architecture and governance

### Technical infrastructure

The ELDS is built on the **building blocks** of the Digital Europe programme and the **Data Spaces Support Centre (DSSC)**. The architecture includes:

- **Interoperable middleware**: SIMPL (Smart Open-source Middleware) to connect national systems
- **Common data models**: standardized legal ontologies for legislation and case law
- **Standardized APIs**: programmatic access to data for legal tech and applications
- **European registry**: centralized catalog of protected and open legal datasets

### Governance

ELDS governance is structured at three levels:

1. **European level**: The European Commission coordinates through the **European Data Innovation Board (EDIB)**, established under the Data Governance Act
2. **Sectoral level**: Specific consortia such as the ELDS define access, quality, and format rules
3. **National level**: Each Member State designates contact points and adapts its judicial infrastructure

## Impact on the Spanish legal sector

### For law firms

**Opportunities:**

- **Pan-European legal research**: access to case law from 27 jurisdictions without language barriers (with integrated automatic translation)
- **Comparative analysis**: compare sectoral regulations between countries for cross-border operations
- **Legal tech products**: develop tools that consume ELDS data via API
- **International due diligence**: verify regulatory compliance in multiple jurisdictions

**Risks and challenges:**

- **Unfair competition**: large firms with data ingestion capabilities may dominate the market
- **Data quality**: heterogeneity of national judicial systems may generate inconsistencies
- **Information overload**: more data does not always mean better information without filtering tools

### For courts and administrations

- **Judicial transparency**: comparable statistics between countries on resolution times, case types
- **Improved public policies**: evidence-based data to reform judicial processes
- **Procedural interoperability**: facilitate mutual recognition of judicial decisions

### For citizens

- **Access to justice**: know their rights and relevant precedents without intermediaries
- **Empowerment**: compare how similar cases are resolved in different countries
- **Participation**: access to bills and legislative debates in real time

## Practical use cases

### Case A: Cross-border M&A lawyer

A Spanish firm advises on the acquisition of a German company. Through the ELDS:

1. Consults German company law directly from their DMS
2. Accesses Bundesgerichtshof case law on exclusivity clauses
3. Compares with Spanish Supreme Court rulings on the same topic
4. Generates a regulatory risk report with pan-European coverage

### Case B: Legal tech startup

A startup develops an AI assistant for contracts. Using the ELDS:

- Accesses updated sectoral directives via API
- Trains its model with structured case law from multiple countries
- Offers compliance checks in 27 jurisdictions without maintaining 27 databases

### Case C: Judge in international litigation

A Spanish judge resolves a case with elements in Italy and France:

- Consults relevant CJEU and national court case law
- Verifies notification procedures and recognition of decisions
- Accesses comparative statistics on enforcement times

## Regulatory framework: Data Governance Act and beyond

The ELDS does not exist in a vacuum. It articulates with:

| Regulation | Relationship with ELDS |
|------------|------------------------|
| **Data Governance Act** | General framework for data governance and intermediaries |
| **Data Act** | IoT data access and cloud portability (complementary) |
| **GDPR** | Protection of personal data in legal datasets |
| **eIDAS 2.0** | Digital identity for authenticated access |
| **AI Act** | Regulation of AI systems that consume ELDS data |

### Privacy considerations

Not all legal data is public. The ELDS must balance:

- **Transparency**: anonymized rulings and open legislation
- **Protection**: personal data in proceedings, especially in family or minors matters
- **Professional secrecy**: information protected by the lawyer's duty of confidentiality
