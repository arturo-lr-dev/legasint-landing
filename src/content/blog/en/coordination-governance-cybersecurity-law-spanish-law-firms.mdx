## Introduction

On January 14, 2025, the Spanish Council of Ministers approved the **Draft Law on Coordination and Governance of Cybersecurity**, a regulatory framework that transposes the NIS2 Directive into Spanish law and establishes new obligations for law firms. This law is not just a technical update: it represents a **paradigm shift** in how legal organizations must manage information security.

In this article, we analyze the specific impacts of this regulation for Spanish law firms, compliance deadlines, and the immediate actions they need to take to adapt.

## 1. What is the Coordination and Governance of Cybersecurity Law?

### Origin and Objectives

The new law responds to two imperatives:
- **Transposing NIS2 Directive (EU) 2022/2555**, which expands the scope of cybersecurity regulations in the European Union
- **Updating the Spanish framework**, which dates back to 2011 (Law 8/2011), facing much more sophisticated digital threats

### Main Innovations Compared to Law 8/2011

| Aspect | Law 8/2011 | New Law 2026 |
|---------|------------|----------------|
| **Subjective scope** | Critical operators and essential services | Expanded to critical sectors and numerous essential service sectors |
| **Notification deadlines** | 24 hours | 72 hours + complete report in 30 days |
| **Sanctions** | Up to €1M | Up to **€10M or 2% global turnover** |
| **Supervision** | INCIBE + CNPIC | INCIBE + CNPIC + new sectoral authorities |

## 2. Does It Affect Law Firms?

### Yes, and Directly

Law firms are included as **obliged entities** for several reasons:

1. **Processing of sensitive personal data**: Client information, litigation, confidential matters
2. **Relevance to the Spanish economy**: Large firms (>50 employees) are considered "large establishments"
3. **Digitalization of services**: Most firms use cloud tools, document management, and electronic communications

### Criteria for Obligation

According to the draft bill, a firm is obliged if it meets ANY of these criteria:
- **Size**: >50 employees OR annual revenue >€10M OR total assets >€10M
- **Strategic importance**: Essential services for the national economy
- **Availability**: Service interruption would have significant impact

**Important note**: Medium and small firms should also apply **proportionate cybersecurity measures**, even if not directly in scope.

## 3. Specific Obligations for Law Firms

### 3.1. Technical and Organizational Security Measures

#### Mandatory measures:

1. **Information security policy** (Art. 21)
   - Documented, updated, and approved by management
   - Includes roles, responsibilities, and procedures

2. **Risk management** (Art. 22)
   - Cybersecurity risk assessment
   - Risk treatment plan
   - Minimum annual review

3. **Supply chain security** (Art. 24)
   - Assessment of digital service providers
   - Cybersecurity clauses in contracts

4. **Data encryption** (Art. 25)
   - Encryption at rest and in transit for sensitive data
   - Robust cryptographic key management

5. **Access control** (Art. 26)
   - Multi-factor authentication (MFA) mandatory
   - Principle of least privilege
   - Immediate revocation of access

### 3.2. Security Incidents

#### New notification deadlines:

| Incident type | Deadline | Authority |
|---------------|----------|-----------|
| **Preliminary alert** | 72 hours | INCIBE (or sectoral authority) |
| **Complete report** | 30 days | INCIBE + competent authority |
| **Notification to affected parties** | Without unjustified delay | If high risk to rights |

#### Use cases in law firms:

**Case 1: Ransomware in document management system**
```
- Immediate action: Isolate systems, do not pay ransom
- Notification: To INCIBE within 72h with details
- Measures: Restoration from backups, forensic analysis
- Communication: To clients if personal data is affected
```

**Case 2: Unauthorized access to client portal**
```
- Immediate action: Revoke compromised credentials
- Notification: To INCIBE if there is availability impact
- Measures: Log audit, mandatory MFA
- Prevention: Review password policies
```

### 3.3. Training and Awareness

Article 27 requires firms to:
- Provide **specific cybersecurity training** to all employees
- Document training delivered
- Periodically update content (minimum annually)

**Recommended modules:**
- Phishing and social engineering
- Password management and MFA
- Secure handling of confidential documents
- Incident response procedures

## 4. Digital Service Providers and Third Parties

### Shared responsibility

The law introduces the concept of **"shared responsibility"** in the supply chain:

1. **Provider assessment**
   - Due diligence before contracting
   - Assessment of cybersecurity measures
   - Verification of certifications (ISO 27001, ENS, etc.)

2. **Contractual clauses**
   - Explicit security obligations
   - Incident notification deadlines
   - Audit rights

3. **Sub-processors**
   - Information about subcontractors
   - Prior consent for change of subcontractor
   - Guarantee of equivalent measures

### Practical cases of critical third parties:

| Provider | Risk | Required measures |
|----------|-------|-------------------|
| **Cloud document manager** | Data breach | E2E encryption, DPA, audits |
| **Video conferencing tools** | Interception | E2E encryption, retention policies |
| **Billing platform** | Service interruption | SLA, backup, redundancy |
| **Hosting service** | DDoS, intrusion | WAF, CDN, 24/7 monitoring |

## 5. Sanctions and Sanctioning Regime

### Sanctions scale

| Infraction | Fine amount | Basis |
|------------|-------------|-------|
| **Minor** | €10,000 - €300,000 | Procedure failures |
| **Serious** | €300,000 - €2,000,000 | Non-compliance with security measures |
| **Very serious** | €2,000,000 - €10,000,000 | Serious incidents not notified, lack of cooperation |

### Aggravating factors:

- Reiteration of infractions
- Lack of cooperation with authorities
- Significant impact on essential services
- Intent or gross negligence

**Important**: Sanctions can be added to those under GDPR (Art. 83), creating considerable financial risk for non-compliant firms.

## 6. Implementation Guide for Law Firms

### Phase 1: Diagnosis (Month 1)

```
✅ Initial audit
   - Review of current security measures
   - Identification of critical assets
   - Gap analysis with the new law

✅ Firm classification
   - Does it meet criteria for obliged entity?
   - Which regulated sectors does it serve?

✅ Provider inventory
   - Complete list of contracted digital services
   - Risk assessment by provider
```

### Phase 2: Planning (Month 2)

```
✅ Security policy development
   - Formal document approved by management
   - Includes procedures and roles

✅ Risk assessment
   - Identification of threats and vulnerabilities
   - Business impact analysis
   - Risk matrix

✅ Budget and resources
   - Allocation of technical and human resources
   - Contracting external services if necessary
```

### Phase 3: Implementation (Months 3-6)

```
✅ Technical measures
   - Deployment of MFA in all systems
   - Implementation of data encryption
   - Backup and disaster recovery

✅ Organizational measures
   - Designation of cybersecurity team
   - Incident response procedures
   - Business continuity plan

✅ Training
   - Initial training for all staff
   - Scheduling periodic sessions
   - Documentation of attendance and content
```

### Phase 4: Continuous Improvement (Ongoing)

```
✅ Monitoring
   - Centralized security logs
   - 24/7 monitoring (if applicable)
   - Periodic risk review

✅ Internal audits
   - Annual compliance audits
   - Penetration testing
   - Incident simulations

✅ Updates
   - Annual policy review
   - Update measures according to new threats
   - Incorporation of lessons learned
```

## 8. Frequently Asked Questions

### Are small firms exempt?

Not completely. Although not in **direct scope**, they must apply proportionate cybersecurity measures, especially if they process personal data from clients (GDPR also applies).

### When does the law come into force?

The law is in parliamentary approval phase (April 2026). Publication in BOE expected in **Q2 2026** and entry into force with an adaptation deadline of **12-18 months** for most obligations.

### Is it compatible with GDPR?

Totally. The law integrates with the data protection framework. In case of conflict, data protection rules prevail (especially for health data, minors' data, etc.).

### What if I use only free software?

Use of free software does not exempt from compliance. Security obligations are **technologically neutral**: free or proprietary software, they must meet the same standards.

### Do I need a DPO (Data Protection Officer)?

If you already have a DPO for GDPR, they should also assume cybersecurity functions. If not, consider a **CSO (Chief Security Officer)** or dedicated team.
