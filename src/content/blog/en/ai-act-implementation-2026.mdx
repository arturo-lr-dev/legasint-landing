## Introduction

March 2026 marks a crucial moment in artificial intelligence regulation in Europe. With the **EU AI Act** in full implementation phase, legal professionals face new challenges and opportunities. This article analyzes the practical implications and steps that law firms and legal departments must take to ensure regulatory compliance.

## The AI Act: Current Implementation Status

The AI Regulation, approved in 2024, established a risk-based regulatory framework for artificial intelligence systems. In 2026, we are in the critical transition period where organizations must:

### Key Deadlines in 2026

- **High-Risk Systems:** Obligations for high-risk AI systems are coming into force, requiring conformity assessments and exhaustive technical documentation.
- **Prohibited Practices:** Prohibitions on manipulative AI systems or social scoring are already effective.
- **General Purpose Systems:** Large language models and generative systems are under specific regulatory scrutiny.

## Impact on the Legal Sector

### 1. Use of AI in Legal Practice

Law firms using AI tools for contract analysis, legal research, or predictive analytics must assess whether their systems are classified as high-risk. This includes:

- **Judicial decision support systems:** Classified as high-risk under the AI Act
- **Case law analysis tools:** Require transparency about their operation
- **Legal chatbots:** Must comply with transparency obligations toward users

### 2. Client Advisory

Lawyers are becoming essential advisors for companies developing or implementing AI systems. Emerging practice areas include:

- **AI Act Compliance:** Audits and risk assessments
- **AI Contracts:** Specific liability clauses and guarantees
- **Data Protection and AI:** Intersection with GDPR and new regulations
- **Intellectual Property:** Rights over AI-generated outputs

### 3. Liability and Litigation

The liability framework is evolving with two complementary directives:

- **AI Liability Directive:** Facilitates claims for damages caused by AI systems
- **Product Liability Directive (updated):** Includes software and AI systems

## Practical Obligations for Law Firms

### Internal AI Systems Assessment

Every firm using AI tools must:

1. **Inventory AI systems in use:** From management software to legal analysis tools
2. **Classify risk level:** Determine if any system is high-risk
3. **Document processes:** Maintain records of how they are used and what decisions they support
4. **Implement transparency:** Inform clients when AI is used in their advisory

### Team Training

AI literacy has become an essential competency:

- Understand capabilities and limitations of AI tools
- Know specific legal obligations of the AI Act
- Identify algorithmic biases and ethical risks
- Maintain independent professional judgment

### Due Diligence Protocols

When advising clients on AI:

```markdown
**AI Act Compliance Checklist:**

□ System classification (minimal/limited/high-risk/prohibited)
□ Conformity assessment (for high-risk)
□ Technical documentation and activity logging
□ Fundamental rights impact assessment
□ Transparency measures and human oversight
□ Risk management system
□ Data quality assessment
□ Post-market monitoring plan
```

## Coordination with GDPR

The AI Act does not replace GDPR but complements it. AI systems processing personal data must comply with **both** regulations:

- **Impact Assessments:** DPIA (GDPR) + Risk Assessment (AI Act)
- **Transparency:** Right to information (GDPR) + Transparency obligations (AI Act)
- **Supervision:** Data protection authorities + AI authorities
- **Penalties:** Up to €20M or 4% turnover (GDPR) + Up to €35M or 7% (AI Act)

## Current Practical Challenges

### 1. Lack of Definitive Criteria

Although the Regulation is approved, many technical specifications and standards are under development. Firms must:

- Monitor European Commission publications
- Follow national authority guidelines
- Participate in sector associations to share best practices

### 2. Shortage of Qualified Professionals

Demand for AI and law experts exceeds supply. Strategies:

- Continuous training of existing team
- Interdisciplinary collaborations with engineers and data scientists
- Networks with other firms to share knowledge

### 3. Cost of Compliance

For high-risk systems, compliance can be costly. Considerations:

- Cost-benefit assessment of each AI tool
- Prioritization of critical systems
- Exploration of compliance-as-a-service solutions

## Strategic Recommendations

### For Law Firms

1. **Conduct an immediate audit** of all AI tools in use
2. **Designate an AI officer** within the firm
3. **Develop internal policies** on ethical and legal use of AI
4. **Invest in continuous training** for the team
5. **Create service offerings** specific to AI Act advisory

### For In-House Legal Departments

1. **Map AI use** across the entire organization
2. **Coordinate with IT and compliance** for comprehensive assessments
3. **Establish interdisciplinary committees** for AI governance
4. **Prepare budgets** for AI compliance in 2026-2027
5. **Consider external advisory** for specialized areas
