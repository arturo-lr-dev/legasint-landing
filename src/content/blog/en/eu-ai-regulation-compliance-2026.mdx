## Introduction

The **European Union's Artificial Intelligence Regulation** (AI Act) has entered into force, and European companies have a limited timeframe to adapt their AI systems. In this article, we analyze concrete obligations and offer a practical guide to ensure regulatory compliance.

## The Four Risk Levels

The AI Act classifies AI systems according to their risk level for fundamental rights:

### 1. Unacceptable Risk (Prohibited)

Systems **completely banned** in the EU:

- **Behavioral manipulation** causing physical or psychological harm
- **Social scoring** by public authorities
- **Real-time biometric identification** in public spaces (except security exceptions)
- **Exploitation of vulnerabilities** of specific groups (age, disability)

> **Penalty:** Up to €35 million or 7% of annual global turnover.

### 2. High Risk

Systems that **require conformity assessment** before market placement:

- **Personnel selection and worker management**
- **Access to education and vocational training**
- **Essential services** (credit, insurance)
- **Law enforcement** (evidence evaluation, crime prediction)
- **Migration management** and border control
- **Administration of justice**

#### Obligations for high-risk systems:

1. **Continuous risk management system**
2. **Data governance** (representative datasets, bias-free)
3. **Complete technical documentation**
4. **Automatic event logging**
5. **Transparency** to users
6. **Effective human oversight**
7. **Accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity**

### 3. Limited Risk

Systems with **transparency obligations**:

- **Chatbots and conversational AI:** Clearly inform users they're interacting with a machine
- **Deepfakes and generated content:** Mandatory labeling
- **Biometric categorization systems:** User notification
- **Emotion detection:** Disclosure of use

### 4. Minimal Risk

Systems **without specific obligations** (majority of AI applications): video games, anti-spam filters, basic assistants.

## Practical Steps for Compliance

### Phase 1: AI Systems Audit (March-June 2026)

**Immediate action:** Inventory all AI systems used in your organization.

```yaml
# Inventory template
System: [Name]
Provider: [Internal/External]
Purpose: [Description]
Risk category: [High/Limited/Minimal]
Data processed: [Data type]
Affected users: [Number and type]
Compliance status: [Compliant/In progress/Non-compliant]
```

**Key questions:**

- What AI systems are we currently using?
- Who is the provider (internal/external)?
- What decisions does the system automate?
- Does it affect fundamental rights?
- Do we process biometric or sensitive data?

### Phase 2: Risk Classification

For each identified system:

1. **Verify if prohibited** → Suspend immediately
2. **Assess if high-risk** → Initiate compliance process
3. **Identify transparency obligations** → Implement labeling
4. **Document classification** → Legal justification

### Phase 3: Technical Measures Implementation

#### For high-risk systems:

**a) Mandatory technical documentation:**

- General system description
- Training data and methodology
- Performance metrics and limitations
- Cybersecurity measures
- Human oversight procedures

**b) Conformity assessment:**

Options depending on system type:

- **Internal self-assessment** (most cases)
- **Third-party evaluation** (notified bodies)
- **Special procedures** (critical systems)

**c) CE marking:**

Once assessment is passed, the system can bear CE marking and be registered in the European database.

### Phase 4: Governance and Internal Training

**Designate responsible parties:**

- **AI DPO** (specialized Data Protection Officer)
- **Multidisciplinary compliance team** (legal, technical, ethical)
- **Human oversight supervisors**

**Mandatory training:**

- Technical staff: AI development best practices
- Legal department: AI Act interpretation
- End users: bias and error detection
- Executives: governance and accountability

## Practical Cases: Particularly Affected Sectors

### Legal Sector: LegalTech

AI tools for lawyers may be classified as **high-risk** if they:

- Predict judicial outcomes
- Analyze evidence in criminal proceedings
- Assist in custody or adoption decisions

**Solution:** Implement explicit human oversight, document limitations, ensure algorithmic transparency.

### Human Resources

AI systems for **personnel selection** are automatically high-risk.

**Specific obligations:**

- Training data without gender, race, or age biases
- Explainability of decisions
- Candidates' right to know about AI use
- Possibility to challenge automated decisions

### Banking and Finance

**Credit and solvency assessment** systems using AI: high-risk.

**Additional requirements:**

- Transparency in criteria used
- No discrimination by origin, gender, or zip code
- Right to explanation of decisions
- Periodic audits

## Penalties and Liability Regime

The AI Act establishes a **strict sanctioning regime**:

| Infringement                    | Maximum Penalty                             |
|---------------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| Use of prohibited systems       | €35M or 7% of annual global turnover        |
| High-risk non-compliance        | €15M or 3% of annual global turnover        |
| Incorrect information           | €7.5M or 1.5% of turnover                   |

**Joint liability:**

- AI system **providers**
- **Importers** (if provider is outside the EU)
- **Distributors** (under certain conditions)
- **Users** (if they substantially modify the system)

## Upcoming Regulatory Milestones (2026-2027)

**April 2026:**
- Publication of first list of notified bodies for assessment

**August 2026:**
- Entry into force of prohibitions (unacceptable risk systems)

**February 2027:**
- Full application of AI Act
- Mandatory registration in European database
- Start of inspections and audits by national authorities

## Conclusion: The Advantage of Early Compliance

Beyond avoiding penalties, complying with the AI Act offers **competitive advantages**:

1. **Customer trust:** Transparency and ethics as differentiator
2. **Risk reduction:** Fewer biases, fewer claims
3. **Market access:** Requirement to operate in the EU
4. **Preparation for future regulations:** Leadership in AI governance

The AI Regulation is not just a legal obligation, but an opportunity to build **fairer, more transparent, and effective** systems.
