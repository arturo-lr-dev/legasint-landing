## Introduction

The **digital transformation of the Administration of Justice** in Spain has reached a tipping point in 2026. After years of incremental development, digital justice systems have evolved from pilot projects to operational infrastructures that shape the daily practice of law and procurator services. This article analyzes the current state of judicial digitalization, the key tools available, and the practical challenges faced by law firms.

## 1. The Current Regulatory Framework

### Law 18/2011 and Its Evolution

The legal foundation for digital justice in Spain still rests on **Law 18/2011**, which regulates the use of information and communication technologies in the Administration of Justice. However, 2026 has brought a wave of regulatory developments that update its practical application:

- **New technical rules** for electronic filing of documents
- **Updated system for mandatory** telematic notifications
- **Integration with European digital identity** (eIDAS 2.0)
- **Interoperability framework** between courts and registries

### eIDAS 2.0 in Judicial Practice

The full entry into force of the **eIDAS 2.0 Regulation** at the beginning of 2026 has substantially changed how lawyers, procurators, and citizens access judicial systems. European digital identity wallets are now valid for authentication on LexNET and other platforms, expanding options beyond the traditional DNIe and representative certificates.

## 2. LexNET: Status and Features in 2026

### What Works Today?

**LexNET** remains the backbone of electronic judicial communication. As of May 2026, these are the operational features with acceptable stability:

| Feature | Status | Notes |
|---------|--------|-------|
| Document filing | ✅ Operational | PDF/A and XML formats accepted |
| Lawyer-to-lawyer communication | ✅ Operational | Attachments up to 50MB included |
| Telematic notifications | ✅ Operational | Mandatory for professionals |
| Access to court decisions | ⚠️ Partial | Available in some jurisdictions |
| Case status inquiries | ⚠️ Partial | Integrated in some courts |
| Video conferencing for hearings | 🔧 Pilot | Expanding in civil jurisdictions |

### Persistent Problems

Despite advances, professionals still encounter **operational frictions**:

- **Hourly unavailability:** Maintenance windows that cut access during business hours
- **Inconsistency between jurisdictions:** What works in Madrid doesn't always work in Barcelona
- **Signature validation:** Recurring issues with certificates from some providers
- **Limited interoperability:** Difficulty crossing data between LexNET and law firm management systems

## 3. Electronic Proceedings by Practice Area

### Civil Proceedings

**Civil proceedings** is where digitalization has advanced the most. In 2026, the vast majority of first instance courts accept:

- Electronic filing of claims and responses
- Documentary evidence through indexed digital attachments
- Expert appointments with telematic communication
- Electronic summons to parties

However, **in-person judicial auctions** remain the general rule, although courts specialized in insolvency proceedings already operate with integrated electronic auction platforms.

### Criminal Proceedings

In **criminal proceedings**, digitalization faces greater restrictions due to the nature of the process:

- **Fast-track trials:** Mostly digital in the pre-trial phase
- **Misdemeanors:** Telematic management in many provinces
- **Summary proceedings:** Mixed, depending on the court location
- **Ordinary trials:** In-person hearings maintained in the vast majority

Documentary evidence can be submitted electronically in the pre-trial phase, but **investigation procedures** still require physical presence.

### Labor and Administrative Litigation

Both jurisdictions have experienced a **notable digital acceleration** in 2026:

- **Labor jurisdiction:** Claims, applications, and appeals almost entirely telematic in some autonomous communities
- **Administrative litigation:** Contentious-administrative appeals and economic-administrative claims with high digitalization
- **Cassation appeals:** Specific platforms for preparing appeals before the Supreme Court

## 4. Remote Access and Distributed Legal Practice

### The Borderless Law Firm

One of the most transformative effects of digital justice has been the **delocalization of professional practice**. A lawyer based in Valencia can represent a client before a court in Bilbao without traveling, filing documents telematically and participating in hearings by video conference when technology allows.

This has generated:

- **Reduced operational costs** for multi-jurisdictional firms
- **Greater competitiveness** between firms from different geographies
- **New coordination needs** for distributed teams
- **Cybersecurity challenges** when accessing judicial systems from non-corporate networks

### Judicial Video Conferencing

2026 has consolidated **video conferencing as a procedural tool** in certain scenarios:

- **Party declarations** in minor civil proceedings
- **Expert ratification** when there is no controversy over their appointment
- **Urgent precautionary measures hearings**
- **Gender violence proceedings** (with specific privacy protocols)

However, the **main oral hearing** remains in-person in the majority of proceedings, especially in criminal cases.

## 5. Practical Challenges for Law Firms

### Training and Technological Adaptation

Digital justice obligates **continuous training** that many firms underestimate:

- Periodic LexNET updates that modify workflows
- New format and document structure requirements
- Changes in notification procedures and deadline computation
- Integration of advanced electronic signature tools

### Adapted Document Management

Law firm management systems must **integrate with digital justice**:

- Automatic generation of documents in formats accepted by LexNET
- Document indexing for electronic attachments
- Complete traceability of telematic communications
- Specific backups of procedural documentation

### Cybersecurity in Judicial Access

Accessing LexNET from personal devices, public Wi-Fi networks, or while traveling introduces **risk vectors** that firms must mitigate:

- Mandatory use of corporate VPNs for remote access
- Network segmentation between judicial systems and internal systems
- Multi-factor authentication at all access points
- Screen lock and automatic session closure policies

## 6. The Horizon: What's Next?

### Artificial Intelligence in Justice

2026 has seen the **first AI pilots in the Spanish Administration of Justice**:

- **Automatic classification** of documents by subject matter and jurisdiction
- **Support systems** for drafting resolutions (NOT automated decisions)
- **Predictive analysis** of caseload for resource allocation
- **Chatbots** for basic procedural information to citizens

These developments raise **guarantee questions** that law firms must closely monitor, especially regarding **Article 22 GDPR** on automated decisions.

### Blockchain for Electronic Evidence

Some pilot courts are experimenting with **blockchain registries** for:

- Timestamping of telematically filed documents
- Chain of custody for digital evidence
- Document integrity verification in appeals

Although far from generalization, it represents a relevant innovation line.

## Conclusion

**Spanish digital justice in 2026** is an operational reality with both bright spots and shadows. Systems work for most ordinary procedures, but full interoperability, hourly stability, and consistency between jurisdictions remain pending objectives.

For law firms, the key lies in **proactive adaptation**: investing in training, integrating management systems with judicial platforms, and maintaining cybersecurity standards appropriate for handling sensitive data in telematic environments.

Digitalization is no longer an option but a **condition of professional practice**. Firms that integrate these tools smoothly will gain clear competitive advantages in efficiency, geographic reach, and service quality.
