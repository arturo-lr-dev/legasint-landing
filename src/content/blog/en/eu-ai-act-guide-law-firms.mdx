## Introduction

The **European Union Artificial Intelligence Regulation** (AI Act) represents the world's most comprehensive regulatory framework for AI systems. For Spanish law firms using or planning to implement artificial intelligence tools, understanding their obligations is essential to avoid penalties that can reach up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover.

In this article, we analyze the practical implications of the AI Act for the legal profession in Spain, identifying which systems require special attention and what measures must be adopted to ensure regulatory compliance.

## The Risk-Based Classification System

The AI Act establishes four risk categories for AI systems:

### 1. Unacceptable Risk (Prohibited)

Systems prohibited under any circumstances:
- Subliminal manipulation of behavior
- Exploitation of vulnerabilities of specific groups
- Social scoring by public authorities
- Real-time biometric identification systems in public spaces (with limited exceptions)

**Impact on law firms:** Minimal, as these uses are not typical of legal practice.

### 2. High Risk

AI systems requiring conformity assessment and strict compliance:
- Systems supporting judicial decisions
- AI used in credit assessment and scoring
- Systems for managing and operating critical infrastructures
- Recruitment and employee evaluation tools

**Impact on law firms:** **CRITICAL**. Many LegalTech tools could fall into this category.

### 3. Limited Risk

Systems with transparency obligations:
- Chatbots and virtual assistants
- Content generation systems (deepfakes)
- Biometric categorization systems

**Impact on law firms:** Moderate. Requires clearly informing clients when they interact with AI.

### 4. Minimal Risk

Systems without specific obligations beyond good practices.

## AI Applications in Law Firms: Where Do You Stand?

### Common Tools and Their Classification

| Tool | Typical Use | Likely Classification | Obligations |
|------|-------------|----------------------|-------------|
| **Predictive case law analysis** | Estimate success probabilities | High Risk | Conformity assessment, technical documentation |
| **Automated contract review** | Due diligence, clause detection | High/Limited Risk | Depends on automation level |
| **Client service chatbots** | Initial consultation, appointments | Limited Risk | Mandatory transparency |
| **Meeting transcription** | Court hearing documentation | Minimal Risk | Good practices |
| **Intelligent document management** | Automatic classification | Minimal/Limited Risk | Depends on criticality |

### Special Case: Predictive LegalTech

Tools that **predict judicial outcomes** or **recommend procedural strategies** will likely be considered **high risk** because they:

1. Support decisions affecting fundamental rights
2. Can significantly influence access to justice
3. Their use could condition the client's defense strategy

**Specific obligations:**
- Documented risk management system
- Representative and unbiased training datasets
- Complete technical documentation
- Registration in the European high-risk systems database
- Effective human oversight (human-in-the-loop)
- Demonstrable robustness, accuracy, and cybersecurity

## Immediate Obligations for Law Firms

### 1. AI Systems Inventory

**Action:** Create a register of all AI tools used in the firm.

**Information to document:**
- System name and provider
- Purpose and use cases
- Data processed (especially personal data)
- Level of automation (assistance vs. autonomous decision)
- Estimated risk classification

### 2. Vendor Assessment

**Key questions for LegalTech providers:**
- Have you classified the system according to the AI Act?
- Are you conducting conformity assessment (if applicable)?
- Do you provide complete technical documentation?
- How do you ensure the absence of bias?
- What human oversight mechanisms are included?
- Is it registered in the EU database (if high risk)?

### 3. Transparency Policies

**Implement:**
- Clear notices when clients interact with AI
- Information about AI use in the legal process
- Specific clauses in service contracts
- Informed consent when necessary

**Example clause:**
> "This law firm uses artificial intelligence tools for [specific purpose]. All decisions are subject to review and direct supervision by licensed attorneys. The use of these technologies does not replace human professional judgment."

### 4. Training and Digital Competence

**Mandatory training plan:**
- Understand the basic functioning of AI systems used
- Identify limitations and potential biases
- Know when to distrust AI recommendations
- Properly document AI-assisted decisions

## Coordination with GDPR

The AI Act **does not replace** GDPR but complements it. Law firms must ensure:

### Regulatory Compatibility

| Aspect | GDPR | AI Act |
|--------|------|--------|
| **Legal basis** | Consent, legitimate interest, legal obligation | Not directly applicable |
| **Transparency** | Clear information about processing | Information about AI use |
| **Automated decisions** | Art. 22: Right not to be subject to solely automated decisions | Mandatory human oversight in high risk |
| **Impact assessment** | DPIA when high risk exists | Conformity assessment in high-risk AI |
| **Rights** | Access, rectification, erasure, etc. | Right to explanation in high-risk systems |

### Practical Recommendation

Conduct a **joint GDPR-AI Act assessment** for systems that process personal data using AI:

```markdown
1. Identify personal data processed
2. Determine legal basis (GDPR)
3. Classify risk level (AI Act)
4. Assess need for DPIA (GDPR)
5. Assess need for conformity assessment (AI Act)
6. Document technical and organizational measures
7. Establish human oversight procedures
8. Define incident management protocol
```

## Liability and Penalties

### Sanctioning Regime

The AI Act establishes administrative fines of up to:
- **€35 million** or **7% of global turnover** for use of prohibited systems
- **€15 million** or **3% of turnover** for breach of high-risk system obligations
- **€7.5 million** or **1.5% of turnover** for inaccurate information to authorities

### Professional Liability

Beyond administrative sanctions, improper use of AI can generate:
- **Civil liability** for damages caused to clients
- **Deontological liability** before the Bar Association
- **Criminal liability** in cases of gross negligence
- **Loss of reputation** and client trust
