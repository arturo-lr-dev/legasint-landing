## Introduction

The **European Union's Artificial Intelligence Regulation** (AI Act) came into force in 2024, and in 2026 we find ourselves in full implementation phase. For legal professionals, this is not just another regulation: it represents a fundamental shift in how we advise clients on technology use and how we manage our own practices.

In this article, we explore the current state of AI Act implementation and offer a practical guide to ensure regulatory compliance in the legal sector.

## The Regulatory Framework: Where Are We in 2026?

### Implementation Timeline

The AI Act established a staggered entry into force schedule:

- **February 2024**: Initial entry into force
- **August 2024**: Prohibitions on high-risk AI systems
- **February 2026**: Full applicability of obligations for general-purpose AI systems
- **August 2026**: Complete applicability for all high-risk AI systems

We are currently at the critical moment where companies must have implemented their compliance systems for general-purpose systems and must prepare for full applicability in August.

### Risk Classification in the Legal Context

The Regulation establishes four risk categories:

**1. Unacceptable Risk (Prohibited)**
- Cognitive manipulation systems
- Social scoring by public authorities
- Real-time biometric identification (with exceptions)

**2. High Risk**
- AI systems for personnel selection
- Assessment of eligibility for social benefits
- Evidence analysis in judicial proceedings

**3. Limited Risk**
- Chatbots and virtual assistants
- Systems requiring transparency

**4. Minimal Risk**
- Spam filters
- AI-powered video games

## Direct Implications for the Legal Sector

### 1. AI Use in Legal Practice

Law firms are adopting AI tools for:

- **Document analysis**: Automated contract review
- **Legal research**: Precedent and doctrine search
- **Outcome prediction**: Predictive litigation analysis
- **Virtual assistants**: Chatbots for initial consultations

Many of these applications fall into the **limited risk** or **high risk** category, especially when used to make decisions that significantly affect people's rights.

### 2. Client Advisory

Lawyers must be prepared to advise their clients on:

- **Risk assessment** of their AI systems
- **Compliance obligations** according to classification
- **Required documentation** (records, impact assessments)
- **AI governance** and risk management systems

## Practical Compliance Obligations

### For Providers of High-Risk AI Systems

If your firm develops or implements AI systems for clients, you must:

**1. Establish a Risk Management System**
```
- Identification of known and foreseeable risks
- Risk estimation and evaluation
- Risk management measures
- Continuous testing and validation
```

**2. Data Governance**
- Representative training datasets
- Bias examination
- Data quality documentation

**3. Technical Documentation**
- Detailed system description
- Instructions for use
- Information on performance and limitations

**4. Registration in EU Database**
- All high-risk systems must be registered
- Updated information on conformity

**5. User Transparency**
- Clear information about capabilities and limitations
- Effective human oversight

### For Professional Users

If your firm uses AI tools developed by third parties:

- **Due diligence**: Verify that the provider complies with the AI Act
- **Impact assessment**: For decisions affecting fundamental rights
- **Human oversight**: Ensure final decisions are made by lawyers
- **Usage logging**: Document how AI tools are used

## Practical Cases: AI in Law Firms

### Case 1: Predictive Litigation Analysis

A firm implements an AI tool that predicts the probability of success in labor litigation.

**Classification**: High risk (employment decisions)

**Obligations**:
- Conformity assessment
- Mandatory human oversight
- Transparency with client about AI use
- Documentation of methodology and limitations

### Case 2: Chatbot for Initial Consultations

A firm launches a chatbot on its website that answers basic legal questions.

**Classification**: Limited risk

**Obligations**:
- Transparency: Inform that it is an AI system
- Disclaimer: Does not constitute legal advice
- Human referral for complex queries

### Case 3: Automated Contract Review

Tool that analyzes contracts and detects problematic clauses.

**Classification**: May be high risk if used for significant contractual decisions

**Obligations**:
- Bias assessment (does it favor certain types of clauses?)
- Lawyer oversight on all recommendations
- Documentation of cases where AI identified errors

## Sanctions and Compliance

The AI Act establishes significant penalties:

- **Violated prohibitions**: Up to €35 million or 7% of global turnover
- **Breached obligations**: Up to €15 million or 3% of global turnover
- **Incorrect information**: Up to €7.5 million or 1% of global turnover

> "The magnitude of the sanctions is comparable to GDPR. Firms must take AI Act compliance as seriously as data protection."

## Compliance Checklist for Law Firms

- [ ] **Inventory of AI systems** used or developed
- [ ] **Risk classification** of each system
- [ ] **Due diligence of AI tool providers**
- [ ] **Internal policies** on AI use in the firm
- [ ] **Team training** on the AI Act and its implications
- [ ] **Documentation of AI-assisted decisions**
- [ ] **Human oversight procedures**
- [ ] **Impact assessments** for high-risk systems
- [ ] **Response plan** for AI incidents
- [ ] **Periodic review** of compliance
