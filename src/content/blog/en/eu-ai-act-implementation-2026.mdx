## Introduction

The **European Union's Artificial Intelligence Regulation** (AI Act) entered into force in 2024, and in March 2026 we are in the midst of its gradual implementation process. For Spanish companies that develop or use AI systems, understanding legal obligations and preparing adequately is no longer optional: it is a mandatory regulatory requirement.

This article offers a practical guide on the AI Act's implications, compliance deadlines, and concrete measures that organizations must adopt.

## The Risk-Based Classification System

The AI Act establishes a risk-based approach that classifies AI systems into four categories:

### 1. Unacceptable Risk (Prohibited)

Systems that pose a clear threat to the safety, rights, and freedoms of individuals:

- **Subliminal manipulation** causing physical or psychological harm
- **Exploitation of vulnerabilities** of specific groups (age, disability)
- **Social scoring** by public authorities
- **Real-time remote biometric identification** in public spaces (with limited exceptions)

**Consequence:** Absolute prohibition. Companies must immediately cease any development or use of these systems.

### 2. High Risk

Systems that can significantly affect fundamental rights or safety. They include:

- **Critical infrastructure** (transport, water, energy)
- **Education and vocational training** (student assessment)
- **Employment** (recruitment, worker evaluation)
- **Essential public services** (allocation of social benefits)
- **Justice and democracy** (assistance in interpretation of facts and law)
- **Biometric systems** (identification, categorization of persons)

**Key obligations:**
- Robust risk management system
- Data governance and training data quality
- Exhaustive technical documentation
- Registration in EU database
- Effective human oversight
- Transparency and traceability
- Cybersecurity and accuracy

### 3. Limited Risk

Systems such as chatbots or synthetic content generators (deepfakes).

**Obligations:** Clear transparency. Users must know they are interacting with AI or viewing artificially generated content.

### 4. Minimal Risk

Most AI systems (spam filters, AI-powered video games, etc.).

**Obligations:** None specific, although voluntary codes of conduct are encouraged.

## Key Compliance Deadlines

The AI Act establishes a staggered entry into force:

- **February 2025** (6 months): Prohibition of unacceptable risk systems
- **August 2025** (12 months): Codes of conduct and governance
- **August 2026** (24 months): Obligations for general-purpose AI models
- **August 2027** (36 months): **Full compliance for high-risk systems**

In March 2026, organizations must be **finalizing their preparation** to comply with all high-risk system obligations in less than 18 months.

## Practical Steps for Compliance

### 1. AI Systems Audit

**Complete inventory:**
- Identify all AI systems in use or development
- Classify them according to risk level
- Document vendors, purpose, and data used

```plaintext
Example classification matrix:
AI System → Category → Risk Level → Required Action
Customer service chatbot → Limited risk → Transparency
CV screening → High risk → Full compliance
```

### 2. Conformity Assessment

For **high-risk** systems, it is mandatory:

- **Self-assessment** (most cases) or third-party certification
- Complete technical documentation (code, data, architecture)
- EU declaration of conformity
- CE marking where applicable

### 3. Data Management

The AI Act requires robust data governance practices:

- **Quality:** Relevant, representative, error-free data
- **Privacy:** Strict compliance with GDPR
- **Bias:** Measures to detect and mitigate discriminatory biases
- **Traceability:** Record of data origin and processing

> "Data is the fuel of AI, but poor data produces deficient and potentially illegal systems." - Recital 42 of the AI Act

### 4. Human Oversight

All high-risk systems must incorporate **human oversight** mechanisms:

- **Intervention capability:** Humans can stop or modify AI decisions
- **Anomaly detection:** Alerts when the system operates outside parameters
- **Adequate training:** Staff trained to interpret AI outputs

### 5. Transparency and Registration

Companies deploying high-risk systems must:

- **Register them** in the European AI systems database
- Provide **clear information** to affected users
- Maintain operation **logs** for defined periods
- Facilitate **access** to supervisory authorities

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

The AI Act provides for significant administrative fines:

- **Up to €35 million** or 7% of global annual turnover (use of prohibited systems)
- **Up to €15 million** or 3% of turnover (breach of high-risk obligations)
- **Up to €7.5 million** or 1.5% of turnover (inaccurate information to authorities)

The **higher amount** will be applied in each case.

## Implications for Law Firms

Law firms that use AI tools for:
- Predictive analysis of judgments
- Automated contract review
- AI-powered document management systems

Must assess whether their tools qualify as **high-risk** (assistance in interpretation of facts and law) and require their legal software vendors to provide corresponding compliance documentation.

### Legal Checklist for Law Firms

- [ ] Inventory of AI tools used
- [ ] Risk classification of each tool
- [ ] Review of contracts with legal software providers
- [ ] AI Act compliance clauses in new acquisitions
- [ ] Team training on responsible AI use
- [ ] Internal human oversight policies
- [ ] Documentation of AI-assisted decision-making processes

## Opportunities from Regulation

Beyond compliance, the AI Act creates opportunities:

### 1. Competitive Advantage

Companies that comply early can:
- Differentiate themselves as "AI Act compliant" providers
- Access public tenders requiring certification
- Generate trust in clients and partners

### 2. Process Improvement

AI Act requirements drive:
- **Better data quality** → More accurate decisions
- **Greater transparency** → Customer trust
- **Human oversight** → Reduction of costly errors

### 3. Responsible Innovation

The European regulatory framework promotes:
- Human-centered AI development
- Common technical standards
- Ecosystem of trust for investment
