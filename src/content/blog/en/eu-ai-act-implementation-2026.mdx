## Introduction

The **EU Artificial Intelligence Regulation (AI Act)**, approved in 2024, entered its gradual implementation phase in 2025. As of March 2026, we find ourselves at a critical juncture: law firms and companies must adapt their AI systems to new legal requirements before sanctions come into force.

This article offers a practical guide on how legal professionals can approach AI Act compliance, both to advise their clients and to review their own technological tools.

## The AI Act in 2026: Current Status

### Implementation Timeline

The AI Act establishes a staggered application schedule:

- **February 2025**: Prohibition of unacceptable high-risk AI systems
- **August 2025**: Obligations for general-purpose AI models
- **August 2026**: Complete requirements for high-risk AI systems (next critical milestone)
- **August 2027**: Full application of the regulation

We are now **five months away from the August 2026 deadline**, when organizations must demonstrate full compliance with requirements for high-risk systems.

### Risk Classification: The Foundation of Compliance

The AI Act classifies AI systems into four levels:

1. **Unacceptable Risk**: Prohibited (e.g., cognitive manipulation, social scoring)
2. **High Risk**: Require conformity assessment and continuous monitoring
3. **Limited Risk**: Transparency obligations
4. **Minimal Risk**: No specific requirements

For the legal sector, most tools fall into the limited or minimal risk categories, but AI applications in human resources management, predictive judicial analysis, or automated decision-making may be classified as **high risk**.

## Impact on Legal Practice

### 1. AI Tools in Law Firms

Spanish law firms increasingly use AI for:

- **Contract review**: Automated clause analysis and risk detection
- **Legal research**: Intelligent search for case law and precedents
- **Predictive analytics**: Estimation of litigation success probabilities
- **Due diligence**: Automation of corporate documentation review

**Critical point**: If a predictive analysis tool directly influences case decisions, it could be classified as high risk and require:
- Comprehensive technical documentation
- Risk management system
- Registration in EU database
- Effective human oversight
- Transparency with clients about AI use

### 2. Client Advisory

Lawyers specializing in technology compliance are experiencing a significant increase in inquiries about:

**AI audits**: Companies needing to map all their AI systems and classify them according to the AI Act's risk level.

**Vendor contracts**: Review of liability clauses when using third-party AI services, especially in cloud computing and SaaS.

**Internal policies**: Design of AI governance protocols that comply with the AI Act, GDPR, and specific sectoral regulations.

### 3. New Business Opportunities

The AI Act is generating new legal practice areas:

- **Legal AI Officer**: Continuous advisory for tech companies on AI compliance
- **Technology litigation**: Disputes related to AI system failures or biases
- **Intellectual property**: Questions about authorship and rights of AI-generated content
- **Civil liability**: Determination of responsibilities for damages caused by AI

## Compliance Checklist for Law Firms

### Step 1: AI Systems Inventory

Create an internal registry of all AI tools used:

- System name and provider
- Specific purpose
- Data processed
- Level of automation in decision-making
- Preliminary risk classification

### Step 2: High-Risk Assessment

For each system that could be classified as high risk:

```markdown
□ Does it make automated decisions affecting people's rights?
□ Is it used in HR management (selection, evaluation)?
□ Does it process sensitive data or data of minors?
□ Does it provide predictive analysis used in judicial decisions?
```

If you answer **YES** to any question, you must implement high-risk measures.

### Step 3: Technical Documentation

Request from your AI providers:

- System description and operation
- Training data used
- Measures to prevent bias
- Performance and accuracy metrics
- Human oversight protocols
- AI Act compliance certifications

**Legal alert**: The AI provider is primarily responsible for compliance, but the **deployer** (who uses the system) also has monitoring and use documentation obligations.

### Step 4: Team Training

Organize training sessions on:

- AI Act fundamentals
- How to identify high-risk systems
- Transparency obligations with clients
- Human oversight protocols
- Incident reporting procedures

### Step 5: Policy Updates

Review and update:

- **Privacy policy**: Include AI use and explain automated decisions
- **Terms of service**: Inform about AI tools used
- **Client contracts**: Consent and transparency clauses about AI
- **Compliance manual**: Integrate AI Act into risk management system

## Coordination with GDPR

The AI Act does not replace GDPR but **complements** it. AI systems processing personal data must comply with **both** regulations:

| Aspect | GDPR | AI Act |
|--------|------|--------|
| Legal basis | Consent, legitimate interest, etc. | Does not specify legal bases |
| Impact assessment | DPIA for high privacy risk | Risk assessment for high-risk AI |
| Individual rights | Access, rectification, deletion | Transparency, human oversight |
| Responsible party | Data controller | AI provider and deployer |

**Best practice**: Integrate the GDPR impact assessment (DPIA) with the AI Act risk assessment into a single compliance document.

## Sanctions and Enforcement

The AI Act establishes a significant penalty regime:

- **Up to €35M or 7% of global turnover** for violation of prohibitions
- **Up to €15M or 3% of turnover** for non-compliance with high-risk requirements
- **Up to €7.5M or 1.5% of turnover** for providing incorrect information

In Spain, the **Spanish Agency for AI Supervision (AESIA)** is the supervisory authority. Although it is still consolidating its inspection procedures, pilot audits in strategic sectors are expected to begin during 2026.

## Practical Recommendations

### For Law Firms

1. **Audit your tools before August 2026**: Don't wait until the last minute
2. **Establish a channel with your AI providers**: Request compliance documentation
3. **Implement real human oversight**: Not just formal, but effective
4. **Document everything**: The burden of proof lies with the system user
5. **Train your team**: Compliance requires everyone's awareness

### For Business Advisors

1. **Offer AI compliance auditing services**: High demand in 2026
2. **Develop contract templates**: Specific clauses for AI providers
3. **Create incident response protocols**: Procedures when AI fails
4. **Collaborate with technical experts**: The AI Act requires both technical and legal understanding
5. **Stay updated**: AESIA will publish guidelines and interpretative criteria

## Conclusion

The AI Act marks a before and after in European technological regulation. For Spanish law firms, 2026 is the year of action: it's not enough to know the regulation—concrete compliance measures must be implemented.

The good news is that AI Act compliance not only reduces legal risks but also improves the **quality and reliability** of AI systems, generating greater client trust and a competitive advantage in an increasingly technological market.

The message is clear: **well-regulated AI is better AI**. And the firms that lead this transition will be better positioned for the digital future of law.
