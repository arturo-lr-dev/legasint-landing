## Introduction

The **EU Artificial Intelligence Regulation (AI Act)** is now fully in force, and Spanish companies face the challenge of adapting their AI systems to one of the most comprehensive regulatory frameworks in the world. Unlike GDPR, which came with a two-year grace period, the AI Act has staggered implementation timelines based on each system's risk level.

In this article, we break down the practical obligations, key deadlines, and concrete actions your company must take to comply with the regulation.

## The Risk-Based Classification System

The AI Act classifies AI systems into four categories, each with different obligations:

### 1. Unacceptable Risk (Prohibited)

These systems have been **completely banned** since February 2, 2025:

- Social scoring systems by governments
- Subliminal manipulation causing harm
- Exploitation of vulnerabilities of specific groups
- Real-time biometric identification in public spaces (with limited exceptions)

**Required action:** If your company operates any of these systems, you must immediately cease use or risk fines of up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover.

### 2. High Risk (Heavy Regulation)

Systems that impact fundamental rights or safety, including:

- AI in personnel selection and worker management
- Credit scoring assessment
- Facial recognition systems (except real-time)
- AI in critical infrastructure
- Educational systems that determine access or evaluation

**Main obligations:**
- Documented risk management system
- Data governance and training data quality
- Exhaustive technical documentation
- Activity logs
- Transparency and user information
- Effective human oversight
- Robust cybersecurity

**Deadline:** August 2026 for systems already on the market; immediate for new systems.

### 3. Limited Risk (Transparency Obligations)

Includes chatbots, deepfakes, and recommendation systems:

**Main obligation:** Clearly inform users that they are interacting with AI.

```typescript
// Example of transparency implementation
const chatbotDisclaimer = {
  en: "This message has been generated by an AI assistant.",
  mandatory: true,
  displayOnFirstInteraction: true
};
```

**Deadline:** Already in force since February 2025.

### 4. Minimal Risk (No Specific Obligations)

Most AI systems (spam filters, AI in video games, etc.) fall into this category and have no specific obligations beyond general data protection and consumer regulations.

## 5-Step Action Plan

### Step 1: AI Systems Audit

**Timeline: March-April 2026**

Identify **all** AI systems your company uses, including:
- Internal software (HR, finance, operations)
- Customer-facing tools (chatbots, recommendations)
- Third-party integrated systems (SaaS using AI)

Create a documented inventory with:
- System name
- Provider/developer
- Purpose and scope of use
- Data processed
- Impact on individuals

### Step 2: Risk Classification

**Timeline: April-May 2026**

For each identified system, determine its risk category according to AI Act criteria. If in doubt, apply the **precautionary principle** and classify it in the higher category.

**Useful resources:**
- Complete list in Annex III of the AI Act
- Guidelines from the Spanish AI Supervisory Agency (AESIA)
- Consultations with specialized legal advisors

### Step 3: Provider Assessment

**Timeline: May-June 2026**

For third-party systems (especially SaaS):

1. **Request compliance documentation** from the provider
2. **Verify the risk classification** they declare
3. **Review contracts** to ensure liability clauses
4. **Confirm registration** in the European database of high-risk systems

> "As an end user of a high-risk system, you have supervisory responsibilities, even if the system is provided by a third party." - AESIA

### Step 4: Control Implementation

**Timeline: June-August 2026**

For **high-risk** systems, implement:

**A. Data Governance**
- Document origin and quality of training data
- Establish update and validation processes
- Implement bias and discrimination controls

**B. Human Oversight**
- Clearly define the human oversight role
- Train responsible personnel
- Establish intervention protocols

**C. Technical Documentation**
- Create complete technical datasheets
- Maintain version and change logs
- Document design decisions

**D. Logging and Traceability**

### Step 5: Registration and Communication

**Timeline: July-August 2026**

- **Register high-risk systems** in the EU database
- **Update privacy policies** mentioning AI use
- **Train employees** on new procedures
- **Communicate to customers/users** about AI systems (transparency)

## Penalties and Consequences of Non-Compliance

The AI Act establishes a tiered penalty regime:

| Violation | Maximum Penalty |
|-----------|----------------|
| Prohibited systems | €35M or 7% global turnover |
| Non-compliance with high-risk obligations | €15M or 3% global turnover |
| Incorrect information to authorities | €7.5M or 1.5% global turnover |

**Important:** These are **maximum** fines. Authorities consider company size, severity, intentionality, and cooperation.

## Practical Cases: Key Sectors

### Human Resources

If you use AI for:
- CV filtering
- Candidate evaluation
- Performance management
- Turnover prediction

**Your system is high-risk.** You must implement strict bias controls, mandatory human oversight in final decisions, and complete documentation.

### E-commerce and Retail

If you use AI for:
- Recommendation systems
- Dynamic pricing
- Customer service chatbots

**Limited to minimal risk.** The main obligation is transparency: inform users that they interact with AI or receive automated recommendations.

### Financial Services

If you use AI for:
- Credit scoring
- Fraud detection
- Automated advice

**High risk in scoring and advice.** Limited/minimal risk in fraud detection (depending on design). Requires case-by-case evaluation.

## Resources and Support

### National Authorities

- **AESIA** (Spanish AI Supervisory Agency): Competent authority in Spain
- **AEPD**: Coordination on data protection issues related to AI
- **Ministry of Economic Affairs and Digital Transformation**: Complementary regulations

### Practical Tools

The European Commission has published:
- Online self-assessment tool
- Technical documentation templates
- Sector-specific guidelines

### Legal Advice

Given the complexity of the AI Act, it is **highly recommended** to have specialized legal advice, especially if you:
- Operate high-risk systems
- Are a provider of AI systems
- Your company operates in multiple jurisdictions

## Conclusion

The AI Act represents a fundamental shift in how companies must manage artificial intelligence. Although the requirements may seem overwhelming, the phased implementation provides sufficient time to adapt, especially if you start now.

**The three key points to remember:**

1. **Act now:** The August 2026 deadline for high-risk systems is only five months away.
2. **Document everything:** Traceability and documentation are at the core of compliance.
3. **Invest in governance:** Solid management systems protect against penalties and improve the quality of your AI systems.

Companies that see the AI Act as an opportunity to improve their AI systems, rather than merely a regulatory burden, will emerge stronger with more robust, fair, and trustworthy systems.
