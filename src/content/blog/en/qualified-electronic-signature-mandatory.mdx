## Introduction

**Electronic signatures** have become an essential tool for law firms, notaries, and businesses in Spain and the European Union. However, confusion about when a qualified electronic signature is mandatory and when a lower-level signature suffices remains common. This article clarifies the different types of electronic signatures, their legal validity, and use cases.

## 1. Regulatory Framework: eIDAS and Spanish Law

### The eIDAS Regulation

**[Regulation (EU) No 910/2014](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX%3A32014R0910)**, known as eIDAS, establishes the European framework for electronic identification and trust services. This regulation defines three levels of electronic signature:

1. **Simple electronic signature**
2. **Advanced electronic signature**
3. **Qualified electronic signature**

### Spanish Legislation

In Spain, **Law 6/2020, of November 11**, regulating certain aspects of electronic trust services, adapted the eIDAS framework to national legislation. Additionally, **[Royal Decree 203/2021](https://www.boe.es/buscar/doc.php?id=BOE-A-2021-7355)** develops technical aspects of electronic signatures.

## 2. Types of Electronic Signatures and Their Characteristics

### Simple Electronic Signature

This is the lowest level of electronic signature. According to Article 3 of eIDAS, it is defined as:

> "data in electronic form which is attached to or logically associated with other data in electronic form and which is used by the signatory to sign."

**Examples:**
- Clicking "I Accept" on a website
- Signing with a stylus on a tablet
- PIN code on a terminal

**Legal validity:** Yes, but with lower probative force. Article 25.1 of eIDAS states that "an electronic signature shall not be denied legal effect and admissibility as evidence in legal proceedings solely on the grounds that it is in an electronic form."

### Advanced Electronic Signature

Article 26 of eIDAS establishes that an advanced electronic signature must meet the following requirements:

1. **Be uniquely linked to the signatory**
2. **Enable identification of the signatory**
3. **Be created using electronic signature creation data** that the signatory can, with a high level of confidence, use under their sole control
4. **Be linked to the data signed** in such a way that any subsequent change is detectable

**Examples:**
- Signature with a personal digital certificate (non-qualified)
- Advanced biometric signature
- Signature with OTP (One-Time Password)

**Legal validity:** Greater probative force than simple. Article 25.2 of eIDAS establishes that an advanced electronic signature meets the legal requirements of a signature in relation to data in electronic form.

### Qualified Electronic Signature

This is the highest level of electronic signature. Article 25.4 of eIDAS establishes that:

> "A qualified electronic signature shall have the equivalent legal effect of a handwritten signature."

**Additional requirements:**
- Must be an **advanced electronic signature**
- Created with a **qualified electronic signature creation device**
- Based on a **qualified electronic signature certificate**

**Examples:**
- Signature with electronic DNI
- Signature with a qualified certificate issued by accredited trust service providers (such as FNMT, Camerfirma, etc.)

## 3. When Is Qualified Electronic Signature Mandatory?

### Public Sector

In Spain, **Law 39/2015, of October 1, on the Common Administrative Procedure of Public Administrations** establishes in Article 14.2 that:

> "Public administrations shall be obliged to allow identification, authentication, and electronic signature through systems based on qualified electronic certificates."

**Cases of mandatory use in the public sector:**
- Filing of documents in electronic registers
- Grant applications
- Electronic public procurement
- Mandatory electronic notifications
- Filing of tax returns (for companies and professionals)

### Private Sector

In the private sector, qualified electronic signature is **not generally mandatory**, except when:

1. **The law expressly requires it** for certain legal acts
2. **The parties agree** to use qualified signature
3. **The regulations of a public registry** require it

**Specific cases where it is mandatory or highly recommended:**
- Notarial public deeds (qualified electronic signature has been valid since the reform of the Notarial Law Ordinance Regulation)
- Acts before property registrars
- Electronic wills (in some Autonomous Communities)
- Contracts with financial entities (due to sectoral regulatory requirements)

## 4. Comparative Legal Validity

| Signature Type | Probative Force | Equivalent to Handwritten | Technical Requirements |
|----------------|-----------------|---------------------------|------------------------|
| **Simple** | Low | No | Minimal |
| **Advanced** | Medium-High | No (unless agreed) | Certificate + exclusive control |
| **Qualified** | Maximum | Yes (Art. 25.4 eIDAS) | Qualified certificate + qualified device |

### Legal Presumptions

Qualified electronic signature enjoys **legal presumptions** that others do not:

- **Presumption of authorship:** The signature is presumed to belong to the certificate holder
- **Presumption of integrity:** The document is presumed not to have been altered since signing
- **Presumption of date:** The date of the signature is the one stated in the time stamp

## 5. Practical Guide by Sector

### For Law Firms

**Recommendations:**
- Use **qualified signature** for deeds, powers of attorney, and notarial documents
- Use **advanced signature** for client contracts, engagement letters, and internal documentation
- Use **simple signature** for policy acceptances, newsletters, and low-risk communications

**Recommended tools:**
- Electronic DNI (for qualified signature)
- Platforms such as DocuSign, Signaturit, or Lleida.net (for advanced signature)
- Integrated signature in document management system (for simple signature)

### For Notaries

Since the reform of the Notarial Law Ordinance Regulation (2020), notaries can:
- Authorize deeds with qualified electronic signature
- Issue authentic electronic copies
- Carry out electronic appearances

**Mandatory:** The notary's signature must always be qualified.

### For Companies

**Areas of application:**
- **HR:** Employment contracts (advanced signature sufficient in most cases)
- **Commercial:** Contracts with clients and suppliers (advanced signature)
- **Compliance:** Internal policies, codes of ethics (simple or advanced signature)
- **Finance:** Loan contracts, guarantees (qualified signature if required by the financial entity)

## 6. Electronic Signature in Court

### Probative Value

Spanish and European courts have been consolidating the validity of electronic signatures:

- **STS 59/2018:** Recognizes the validity of advanced electronic signature in banking contracts
- **STS 451/2019:** Values electronic signature as proof of contractual consent
- **Directive (EU) 2019/1153:** Facilitates access by investigative authorities to electronic records

### Challenges in Litigation

The main challenges when an electronic signature is challenged are:

1. **Proving the signatory's identity** (easier with qualified signature)
2. **Proving document integrity** (easier with advanced or qualified signature)
3. **Proving consent** (may require additional evidence with simple signature)

## 7. eIDAS 2.0 and the European Digital Identity Wallet

With the approval of the **[eIDAS 2.0 Regulation](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/eidas-regulation)** (2024), the landscape will evolve significantly:

- **European Digital Identity Wallet (EUDI Wallet):** Allows storing digital identity and signing documents from mobile
- **Qualified signature from smartphone:** Without the need for a physical cryptographic card
- **Cross-border recognition:** Greater harmonization between Member States

**Impact for law firms:**
- Clients will be able to sign in a qualified manner from their mobile
- Greater adoption of 100% digital processes
- New opportunities for remote services

## 8. Decision Checklist

### What type of signature do I need?

```
Does the law require qualified signature?
├── YES → Use qualified signature (eID, qualified certificate)
└── NO → Is it a high-risk or high-value document?
    ├── YES → Do the parties agree on qualified signature?
    │   ├── YES → Use qualified signature
    │   └── NO → Use advanced signature minimum
    └── NO → Is robust legal validity needed?
        ├── YES → Use advanced signature
        └── NO → Simple signature may suffice
```

### Key Questions

1. **What does sectoral law say?** (tax, commercial, labor...)
2. **What does the public registry regulation say?** (Property Registry, Commercial Registry...)
3. **What have the parties agreed?**
4. **What is the risk if the signature is challenged?**
5. **Is cross-border recognition needed?**

## Conclusion

Qualified electronic signature is **not always mandatory**, but it is the only one with the **same legal effect as a handwritten signature**. For law firms, the key lies in:

1. **Knowing the regulatory framework** applicable to each type of legal act
2. **Evaluating the risk** of each transaction
3. **Documenting the signing process** for future evidence
4. **Preparing for eIDAS 2.0** and the EUDI Wallet

The trend is clear: towards greater digitalization and recognition of electronic signatures. Law firms that master these signature levels will be able to offer more agile, efficient, and competitive services.
