## Introduction

In July 2026, the **Digital Omnibus** has become the most relevant regulatory topic for European businesses and law firms. Approved by the European Commission in late 2025, this legislative package aims to **simplify and harmonize** the EU's digital framework, reducing the compliance burden that has overwhelmed organizations since the GDPR came into force in 2018.

The goal? To move from an ecosystem of **overlapping and contradictory regulations** to a coherent framework where GDPR, AI Act, DORA, DSA and other digital regulations function as an integrated system rather than independent silos.

## 1. What is the Digital Omnibus?

### The problem it solves

Since 2018, the EU has approved multiple digital regulations:
- **GDPR** (2018): Data protection
- **DSA/DMA** (2024): Digital services and markets
- **AI Act** (2024/2025): Artificial intelligence regulation
- **DORA** (2025): Digital operational resilience
- **Data Act** (2025): Data sharing

Each with its own **deadlines, definitions, obligations and sanctions**. The result: businesses and law firms managing 5+ regulatory frameworks with overlapping but not exactly matching terms.

### The Omnibus proposal

The Digital Omnibus introduces three fundamental pillars:

| Pillar | Description | Impact |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| **Definition harmonization** | Unified terms across regulations | Less legal ambiguity |
| **Single window** | Single point of supervision per country | Bureaucracy reduction |
| **Proportionality** | Tiered obligations by size | SME relief |

## 2. Key changes by regulation

### GDPR: From theory to practice

The Omnibus modifies the GDPR to **reduce formal obligations** in certain contexts:

- **Records of activities (Art. 30)**: Simplification for companies < 250 employees that don't process high-risk data
- **External DPO**: Possibility to share DPO among group companies without requiring complex justification
- **Impact assessments**: Standardized templates valid for multiple regulations (GDPR + AI Act)

> "The Omnibus does not weaken the GDPR. It makes it **functional** for organizations that invest excessive resources in bureaucratic compliance rather than real data protection."

### AI Act: Clarification of scope

One of the most requested improvements:

- **Definition of "AI system"**: Aligned with the technical definition of the AI Act, eliminating confusion with traditional software
- **Low-risk systems**: Exemption from registration in the EU database for non-critical internal systems
- **Conformity assessment**: Possibility to use a single audit to comply with AI Act + GDPR when both apply

### DORA: Integration with sectoral cybersecurity

The Omnibus **connects DORA with NIS2** to avoid duplication:

- **Incident reports**: Single format for notifying financial and cybersecurity authorities
- **Resilience testing**: Coordinated program instead of multiple parallel exercises
- **Third-party risk management**: Unified framework for critical providers

## 3. Impact on law firms

### Business opportunities

The Digital Omnibus creates **three high-value advisory areas**:

#### 1. Simplification audit
Review which obligations have disappeared or been reduced for the client:
- Reduction of internal records
- Consolidation of regulatory reports
- Elimination of duplications in policies

#### 2. Compliance restructuring
Redesign integrated compliance programs:
```
Before: 5 separate programs (GDPR, AI Act, DORA, DSA, NIS2)
After: 1 unified program with specific modules
```

#### 3. Litigation and sanctions
The Omnibus introduces **grace periods** for adaptation:
- 6 months to adjust internal policies
- Reduced sanctions for "demonstrable adaptation effort"
- Mandatory mediation before administrative sanctions in certain cases

### Practical challenges

## 6. Criticisms and cautions

### What the Omnibus does NOT do

- **Does not reduce data protection**: Data subject rights remain intact
- **Does not eliminate the AI Act**: Only simplifies procedures for low-risk systems
- **Does not unify sanctions**: Each regulation maintains its sanctioning regime
- **Is not retroactive**: Previous contracts and decisions are not affected

### Interpretation risks

> "Misinterpreted simplification can lead to **under-compliance**. The Omnibus reduces bureaucracy, not standards."

Areas where caution is paramount:
1. **Special categories of data**: No changes in processing
2. **International transfers**: Safeguard mechanisms intact
3. **High-risk AI systems**: Strict obligations without substantial changes
